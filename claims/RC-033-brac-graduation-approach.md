# Reality Check #033

## Claim

> "We have now demonstrated that the Graduation Approach can work across a wide range of contexts — from sub-Saharan Africa to South Asia to Latin America. The evidence is strong enough to move from research to scale. Ultra-poor households anywhere can benefit from receiving productive assets, training, and social support delivered as an integrated package."

## Source

- Speaker: Esther Duflo and Abhijit Banerjee (co-authors, Banerjee et al. 2015); CGAP (Consultative Group to Assist the Poor); BRAC International; J-PAL (Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab); Stuart Rutherford (Grameen Foundation commentary)
- Organizations: BRAC (Bangladesh Rural Advancement Committee); CGAP; J-PAL; Ford Foundation; Mastercard Foundation; Innovations for Poverty Action
- Date: BRAC Targeting the Ultra Poor (TUP) programme Bangladesh origin: 2002; multi-country pilot study: 2006–2014; Banerjee et al. (2015) *Science* publication; long-term follow-up: Banerjee et al. (2021)
- Link: Banerjee et al. (2015), "A multifaceted program causes lasting progress for the very poor," *Science*: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1260799; CGAP/Ford Foundation Graduation Programme: https://www.cgap.org/graduation; BRAC TUP programme: https://www.brac.net/targeting-the-ultra-poor/

*Note: The Graduation Approach is an integrated, time-bound intervention for ultra-poor households. Its core components: (1) transfer of a productive asset (livestock, small business inventory, or comparable); (2) training in the selected livelihood; (3) consumption support during the transition period; (4) savings mobilization; (5) health education and linkages; (6) coaching and mentoring by programme staff. Developed by BRAC in Bangladesh from 2002 onward, the approach was piloted across six countries simultaneously (2007–2014) through a CGAP/Ford Foundation initiative: Ethiopia, Ghana, Honduras, India (West Bengal), Pakistan, and Peru. Banerjee et al. (2015) published the results of pre-registered RCTs from all six sites in a single Science paper. The claim as audited is the cross-context inference: Bangladesh evidence plus the six-country RCT results justify global scale-up on the grounds that the approach works "across a wide range of contexts."*

## Why This Matters

RC-033 is selected for its position in the four-cell observation map that emerged from RC-028 through RC-032, not primarily for the claim's importance. The map:

| Cell | Discovery timing | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Replication | None | Observed — NFP UK (null result) |
| Discovery infrastructure | During implementation | Observed — MST Norway (positive result) |
| Upfront adaptation | Before implementation | Partially observed — Ethiopia HEP RC-028 (attribution confounder limits conclusion) |
| Adaptation + discovery infrastructure | Both | Not cleanly observed |

The repository's primary goal in RC-033 is to determine whether the BRAC multi-country case can cleanly populate Cell 3 (upfront adaptation, limited ongoing discovery infrastructure) or whether a pre-registered confounder — external BRAC advisory involvement in higher-performing implementations — collapses it into Cell 4 (upfront adaptation plus during-implementation discovery). Cleanly occupying Cell 3 would be the most informative outcome. Collapsing into Cell 4 would be informative in a different way: it would mean Cell 3 has not yet been observed in a transfer context.

The claim's policy importance is real: approximately 1.3 billion people live in extreme poverty globally (World Bank, 2022 revision). The Graduation Approach is now operating at scale in multiple countries under the assumption that the 2015 multi-country RCT evidence justifies broad transfer. Whether that evidence is as generalization-supporting as the claim implies is an open question.

## Watch Items

**Watch A:** Does this claim's structure match any prior structure in the repository?
**Watch B:** Does the evaluation bottleneck recur? Does it locate at source-context causal attribution or at the generalization step?

**Pre-registered expectation:**

Watch A expected present: cross-context inference structure — Bangladesh origin evidence and six-country pilot RCTs used to justify global scale-up across all contexts with ultra-poor populations. Ninth instance of cross-context inference structure (RC-019, RC-020, RC-026, RC-028, RC-029, RC-030, RC-031, RC-032, RC-033).

Watch B expected absent: The Banerjee et al. (2015) study itself conducted pre-registered RCTs in all six countries; the paper is the transfer evidence, not just the source evidence. Source causal identification at Bangladesh (quasi-experimental) is adequate; the multi-country design provides additional within-study identification. CE-1 predicts Watch B absent.

**Pre-registration: cell classification as the primary goal**

The primary task for RC-033 is not to test whether adaptation predicts outcomes. It is to classify whether the BRAC multi-country case occupies Cell 3 or Cell 4 of the observation map.

The classification hinges on the BRAC advisory involvement variable:

*Cell 3 (upfront adaptation only):*
Local implementing organizations in each country adapted the Graduation Approach to local context before and during rollout — selecting contextually appropriate assets, training content, and delivery structures — but operated largely independently, without intensive ongoing advisory involvement from BRAC International or equivalent expert support that could detect and respond to implementation mismatches in real time. The primary discovery mechanism was pre-implementation design work by local partners.

*Cell 4 (upfront adaptation + discovery infrastructure):*
BRAC International or equivalent actors maintained ongoing advisory relationships with country implementations — reviewing results, identifying implementation problems, and guiding adjustments — such that the higher-performing implementations benefited from both upfront adaptation and ongoing discovery infrastructure. This would mean the BRAC case is a Cell 4 case being classified as Cell 3 due to the invisibility of the advisory involvement.

**Pre-registered confounder — BRAC advisory involvement:**

The BRAC advisory involvement variable is the most dangerous source of cell misclassification in this audit. BRAC International provides technical assistance to implementing organizations worldwide, but the intensity of that involvement varied across the six-country study sites. If higher-performing sites (Ethiopia, Ghana, India) received more intensive ongoing BRAC advisory support than lower-performing sites (Peru, Honduras to a lesser extent), the Cell 3 classification would be incorrect: the advisory involvement would constitute during-implementation discovery infrastructure, and the variation in outcomes would be tracking discovery infrastructure intensity, not upfront adaptation depth.

Pre-registering this as a variable to assess before the outcome ordering is examined: what was the level of BRAC International advisory involvement in each of the six country sites?

**Pre-registered alternative confounders:**

Before examining outcomes, register additional alternative explanations for cross-country variation:
- Baseline ultra-poverty severity: countries where the ultra-poor faced more severe asset constraints may have shown larger treatment effects independent of adaptation depth
- Implementing organization capacity: different local NGOs varied in program delivery quality
- Asset-to-livelihood match: quality of the fit between the transferred asset and local livelihood opportunities, which may be driven by adaptation but may also reflect pre-existing local market conditions

**Pre-registered wound condition (primary):**

If BRAC advisory involvement intensity (not adaptation depth) is the primary predictor of cross-country outcome variation — specifically if the highest-performing country implementations received the most intensive BRAC advisory support regardless of how deeply they adapted the intervention design — then Cell 3 (upfront adaptation only) has not been cleanly observed in this audit. The successful cases were actually Cell 4, and the discovery-timing dimension remains incompletely observed at the upfront-adaptation cell.

*This wound condition matters more than the ordering prediction.* If it is triggered, RC-033's contribution is: confirmed that what appeared to be Cell 3 was actually Cell 4, and Cell 3 remains unobserved in clean form.

**Pre-registered ordering prediction (secondary):**

Countries where local implementing organizations made explicit, documented adaptations to the core protocol — selecting assets matched to local market conditions, adapting training content to local skills gaps, modifying delivery structure to fit local social norms — should outperform countries where the standard Bangladesh-derived protocol was applied more closely. This prediction holds only if the BRAC advisory involvement variable does not confound the comparison.

If BRAC advisory involvement is comparable across high- and low-performing sites, and adaptation depth varies, then outcome variation tracking adaptation depth would be evidence that upfront adaptation (Cell 3) is doing something real. If BRAC advisory involvement also varies and covaries with outcomes, the prediction is confounded.

**Pre-registered cell classification assessment (the primary deliverable):**

This audit's primary output is not a causal claim. It is:

> Where in the four-cell observation map does the BRAC multi-country case sit?

Secondary outputs:
- Does any evidence bear on whether the cell occupancy is explanatory or merely descriptive?
- Does the wound condition trigger?

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The Graduation Approach generalizes broadly; outcome variation reflects implementation quality rather than adaptation depth

The core Graduation Approach mechanism — asset transfer plus livelihood training plus consumption support plus coaching — addresses a fundamental constraint that ultra-poor households face across diverse contexts: they are trapped in low-productivity livelihood strategies because they lack the initial asset stock to enter more productive activities. This asset trap operates across cultures and geographies. Where Graduation Approach implementations underperformed, the primary cause was implementation quality (insufficient asset values, inadequate training, high dropout rates) rather than design mismatch with local context. Adaptation to local context matters for surface features (asset type, language of training materials) but the core mechanism transfers without deep redesign. Under H1, cross-country variation reflects implementation fidelity and quality, not adaptation depth.

### H2 — Success requires upfront adaptation of the intervention package to local economic and social context; standard-protocol implementations underperform regardless of other variables (upfront adaptation explanation)

The Graduation Approach must match the transferred asset to local market conditions (livestock that thrive in local environments, goods with local market demand) and the training content to local livelihood skills and social structures. Countries where implementing organizations explicitly redesigned the asset selection and training protocols to fit local context — based on pre-implementation assessment of local markets, skills, and social norms — outperform countries where the standard asset-and-training package was applied without such redesign. Under H2, adaptation depth (measured independently before outcomes) should predict cross-country ordering. This is the upfront adaptation claim that, if supported, would confirm a cleaner observation of Cell 3.

### H3 — Success depends on ongoing BRAC advisory involvement; countries with intensive technical assistance outperform those operating independently

BRAC's operational success in Bangladesh reflects decades of accumulated knowledge about how to match programme design to local ultra-poor populations. BRAC International exported not just the programme model but also human capital — experienced BRAC staff who advise on implementation, monitor early results, and guide course corrections. Countries where BRAC International maintained intensive advisory relationships with implementing organizations were better positioned to detect and respond to implementation mismatches. Under H3, BRAC advisory intensity predicts outcomes more robustly than adaptation depth, and the upfront adaptation cell (Cell 3) is conflated with the combined cell (Cell 4) because the successful implementations always had both.

### H4 — Outcome variation reflects baseline ultra-poverty severity and asset constraint depth; the mechanism is prerequisite alignment

The Graduation Approach's mechanism assumes a specific type of ultra-poverty: households in asset traps who would exit if given the initial capital stock. Where the ultra-poor face this specific constraint structure, the intervention should work. Where they face different constraint structures (lack of market access, health-driven poverty, lack of social capital for enterprise), the asset-and-training mechanism does not address the binding constraint. Cross-country variation tracks which type of ultra-poverty predominates, not adaptation depth or discovery infrastructure. Under H4, a prerequisite alignment explanation accounts for the variation: South Asian and West African contexts have asset-constrained ultra-poor populations that match the intervention's mechanism; Latin American contexts have different constraint structures where the Graduation Approach mechanism does not bind as strongly.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Banerjee et al. (2015) *Science* multi-country RCTs: pre-registered RCTs in all six country sites; pooled results showed significant positive effects on consumption, assets, food security, financial inclusion, psychological well-being, and time use (livestock and agriculture); effects were positive in 5 of 6 countries on most outcomes at 18-month follow-up, with Peru as the weakest case
- Long-term follow-up (Banerjee et al. 2021, forthcoming): follow-up evaluation approximately 3–7 years post-programme in four sites (Ethiopia, Ghana, Honduras, India) showed sustained effects in most contexts; Ethiopia showed particularly strong sustained gains; suggests that positive short-term results were not transient
- Bangladesh origin evidence: BRAC TUP programme has been operating since 2002; subsequent rigorous evaluations including Bandiera et al. (2017, *Review of Economic Studies*) found substantial and sustained effects on consumption and asset accumulation for programme participants; this represents a strong evidence base at source
- Scale validation: the Graduation Approach has been taken to scale in multiple countries; programmes currently operating in Ethiopia (PSNP Graduation), India (multiple state-level programmes), Pakistan, and others; at-scale implementation demonstrates deliverability, though effectiveness evidence from at-scale settings is more limited than the pilot RCTs
- Banerjee et al. heterogeneity analysis: the 2015 paper included analysis of effect heterogeneity; baseline consumption was a significant predictor of treatment effect magnitude in some sites, suggesting the intervention works better for the most severely constrained households — consistent with a mechanism targeting a specific type of poverty trap

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Peru null/weak result: Peru showed the weakest results in the Banerjee et al. study; consumption effects were smaller and some outcomes were null; Peru's ultra-poor had different characteristics from other sites (higher urban concentration, more market integration, different livelihood base); if Peru's weaker results reflect a different ultra-poverty constraint structure rather than implementation quality, the claim that the approach works across all ultra-poor contexts is overstated
- Implementation variation: the six-country study was implemented by six different local organizations with different organizational capacities; standardizing for implementation quality is difficult; some apparent cross-country variation may reflect implementing organization capacity rather than adaptation depth or discovery infrastructure
- Asset-specificity concerns: the Graduation Approach requires selecting an asset that matches local livelihood opportunities; this selection is not straightforward, and poorly matched assets can produce weaker or null effects; to the extent that successful implementations required careful pre-implementation market analysis (upfront adaptation), this is consistent with H2 but not visible in the published paper's treatment of "the programme" as a fixed intervention
- BRAC advisory involvement heterogeneity: BRAC International provided different levels of technical assistance to different implementing partners across the six countries; the exact mapping of advisory intensity to performance is not systematically published in the main Banerjee et al. paper, making the pre-registered Cell 3 vs. Cell 4 classification difficult to resolve from the published evidence alone

---

## Missing Evidence

- BRAC advisory involvement intensity by country: systematic documentation of the level of BRAC International staff involvement in each of the six country implementations — frequency of advisory contact, type of support provided, whether ongoing monitoring and course-correction occurred — would allow the Cell 3 vs. Cell 4 classification the audit requires; this information may exist in programme management documents but is not published in the Banerjee et al. paper
- Adaptation depth documentation: systematic documentation of how each country implementation modified the standard BRAC protocol — asset selection process, training content, delivery structure, conditionality structure — would allow independent measurement of adaptation depth before comparing to outcomes; no such cross-country adaptation comparison is published
- Implementation quality measures: standardized implementation quality indicators across the six sites would allow separation of adaptation depth effects from implementation quality effects; the Banerjee et al. paper does not include comparable implementation quality measures across sites
- At-scale effectiveness data: the 2015 paper reports pilot RCT results from relatively small samples; effectiveness at national scale may differ; Ethiopia's national graduation programme (within PSNP) provides some evidence but has not been evaluated with the same rigour as the pilot

The repository has established: Cell 3 is plausible and the BRAC case is consistent with it. The repository has not established: Cell 3 is occupied by a clean case with identifiable upfront adaptation and demonstrably limited during-implementation discovery.

## Open Questions

1. The BRAC advisory involvement data exists in programme records that are not publicly accessible in the form the repository requires. Is there a different case — with published implementation documentation — that would allow clean Cell 3 classification without requiring unpublished data?

2. The Peru underperformance within the Banerjee et al. study is the most informative within-study variation. If the Peru implementing organization had comparable BRAC advisory support to Ethiopia and India, and Peru still underperformed, that would be more consistent with H4 (prerequisite alignment — Peru ultra-poor had different constraint structure) than H2 or H3. What do the implementation records show about Peru's advisory support level?

3. The Graduation Approach has subsequently been evaluated at national scale in Ethiopia (within PSNP). Ethiopia appears in the repository as the location with the strongest pilot results. If national-scale results in Ethiopia diverge from pilot results, this would raise whether the pilot's success was specific to the tightly supervised pilot context rather than to Ethiopia's prerequisites or adaptation quality.

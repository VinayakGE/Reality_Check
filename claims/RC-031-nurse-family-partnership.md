# Reality Check #031

## Claim

> "The evidence for the Nurse-Family Partnership programme is exceptional — among the strongest for any social intervention ever tested. Any country with the capacity to train nurses and reach at-risk first-time mothers has the prerequisites to replicate these results. What we need now is not more research. We need to scale it."

## Source

- Speaker: David Olds (developer of NFP, University of Colorado); Graham Allen MP (*Early Intervention: The Next Steps*, 2011); UK Department of Health (FNP adoption rationale, 2006–2007); Frank Field (*The Foundation Years*, 2010)
- Organizations: NFP National Service Office (Denver); UK Department of Health / NHS England; The Dartington Social Research Unit; Early Intervention Foundation (UK)
- Date: NFP evidence base established 1997–2010 (Elmira, Memphis, Denver trials); UK FNP adoption announced 2006; UK government commissioned Building Blocks RCT to confirm transfer; results published 2015 (Robling et al., *BMJ*)
- Link: Olds et al. (1997), "Long-term effects of home visitation on maternal life course and child abuse and neglect," *JAMA*; Robling et al. (2015), "Effectiveness of a nurse-led intensive home-visitation programme for first-time teenage mothers," *The Lancet*; Building Blocks trial: *BMJ* 2016; Graham Allen (2011), *Early Intervention: The Next Steps*, https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/early-intervention-the-next-steps--2

*Note: The Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP) is a structured home visiting programme in which trained registered nurses visit first-time mothers at risk (low income, young, limited social support) during pregnancy and for two years after birth. Nurses follow a structured curriculum covering prenatal health, parenting practices, child development, and maternal life course. The programme was developed by David Olds beginning in Elmira, New York, in 1977. It is one of the most extensively tested interventions in social policy, with three independently conducted randomized controlled trials in the US. The UK adopted a fidelity replication under the name Family-Nurse Partnership (FNP) in 2007. The claim as audited is the cross-context inference: the US evidence base, established through RCTs, justifies UK and broader global adoption of the programme with the same design.*

---

## Why This Matters

Home visiting programmes represent one of the largest investments in early intervention in high-income countries. As of 2022, NFP operates in 43 US states and in multiple countries including UK, Netherlands, Canada, and Australia, serving hundreds of thousands of families annually. The policy justification in nearly every case cites the US RCT evidence base as establishing that the programme works and that replication is warranted. If the NFP transfer claim is correct — that countries with nurses and at-risk first-time mothers have the prerequisites to replicate the results — then the scaling strategy is sound. If incorrect — if the US results depend on a real prerequisite structure that is not visible at the design specification level and requires redesign to discover — then the current scaling strategy is misallocating resources in contexts where the apparent alignment is not actual alignment.

RC-031 is selected to pressure weak selector in a way RC-029 and RC-030 did not. The UK FNP case has a specific evidentiary feature that most failed transfer cases lack: the decision not to redesign was deliberate and documented. UK FNP was explicitly positioned as a fidelity replication — testing whether the US results would transfer unchanged. This removes the usual escape route for transfer failures ("they adapted badly," "they changed the model"). The UK case is observing directly what happens when apparent prerequisite alignment is treated as sufficient and no redesign is undertaken.

**Pre-registration: definitions and predictions frozen before evidence review**

The definitions carried forward from RC-030:

*Weak selector:*
Success requires real prerequisite alignment between the intervention package and the transfer context. Real alignment may differ from apparent alignment. When failure occurs in apparently well-aligned contexts, weak selector attributes failure to absence of real alignment. The defense available to weak selector is: invoke the apparent/real distinction retroactively.

*Adaptation:*
Success depends on discovering the real prerequisite structure of the implementation environment through active redesign — not matching the prerequisite structure assumed by the original design. The prerequisite structure is not directly observable from design specifications. Redesign is the discovery process. Apparent prerequisite alignment (nurses, target population, institutional support) does not predict success; alignment of the redesigned package with the discovered real prerequisites does.

The critical evidentiary gap between them: weak selector's apparent/real distinction is invoked after observing failure. Adaptation predicts that the failure itself is caused by not undertaking redesign. If weak selector defends against NFP UK failure by invoking the apparent/real distinction, it has accepted adaptation's structural premise — that apparent design specifications do not reveal the real prerequisite structure — while attributing the discovery problem to context rather than process.

**Pre-registered discriminating predictions:**

*UK FNP (fidelity replication — decision not to redesign was explicit):*

| Explanation | UK FNP prediction | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Weak selector | Positive effects | UK has trained nurses, low-income first-time mothers, NHS health visiting infrastructure, institutional funding — strong apparent alignment with US NFP design prerequisites |

*Netherlands VoorZorg (explicit adaptation of NFP for Dutch context):*

| Explanation | Netherlands prediction | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Weak selector | Similar to UK if apparent prerequisites comparable across UK and Netherlands | Prerequisite similarity between high-income European health systems; if apparent alignment predicts outcomes, similar countries should produce similar results regardless of redesign |
| Adaptation | Better than UK | Explicit redesign occurred; different nurse training, targeting criteria adapted to Dutch health system, cultural framing modified; redesign increased probability of discovering real prerequisite structure |

**The comparative prediction is the sharper test.** If UK and Netherlands produce similar outcomes despite different redesign approaches, weak selector is supported (apparent alignment predicts; redesign is irrelevant once alignment is present). If Netherlands substantially outperforms UK despite similar apparent prerequisites, adaptation is supported (redesign quality, not apparent prerequisite matching, predicts outcomes across comparable transfer contexts).

**Pre-registered wound conditions:**

*For adaptation:* If Netherlands VoorZorg (explicit redesign) produces null or near-null effects while UK FNP (fidelity replication) shows positive effects, adaptation takes a direct hit. The ordering Netherlands > UK is the specific prediction; its reversal wounds adaptation.

*For weak selector:* If UK shows null/near-null effects AND weak selector's defense requires invoking the apparent/real distinction — i.e., requires claiming that "real" prerequisites were absent despite the presence of nurses, target population, infrastructure, and institutional support — then weak selector has accepted the structural premise that apparent design specifications do not reveal real prerequisites. At that point the dispute between weak selector and adaptation is no longer about the explanation; it is about whether the discovery problem is caused by context (weak selector) or by the absence of a redesign process (adaptation). If the within-RC-031 evidence can distinguish those two accounts, weak selector faces a direct wound. If it cannot, the dispute narrows but is not resolved.

**Pre-registered confounder:**

UK at-risk mothers in the FNP population may have had substantially higher baseline social support and lower risk profiles than Elmira or Memphis mothers, due to UK welfare state provision (NHS health visiting, social services access, housing support). If the comparison arm in UK received substantially more support than the comparison arm in US, the null results may reflect a weaker treatment-control contrast rather than a design failure. This is a genuine confounder. Unlike MDR-TB in RC-030, this confounder is structurally entangled with the theoretical dispute: invoking it requires claiming that "real prerequisites" (adequate risk severity, adequately deprived comparison arm) were absent despite apparent design alignment — which is adaptation's structural territory. Recording the confounder does not resolve the theoretical dispute; it changes the nature of weak selector's required defense.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — NFP generalizes broadly; the UK null results reflect implementation quality rather than design mismatch

The NFP model is essentially behavioral and relational: a trained nurse builds a trusting relationship with a first-time mother over the course of pregnancy and early childhood, delivering a structured curriculum that modifies health behaviors and parenting practices. The core mechanism — trusted professional relationship plus structured behavioral content — is not culturally specific in the way infrastructure-dependent models are. Where NFP has failed to replicate, the cause is implementation quality: nurses undertrained or overstretched, programme fidelity not maintained, dosage lower than specified. The UK Building Blocks trial found lower-than-specified programme doses were delivered, which may explain null results. Under H1, the design was fine; the execution fell short.

### H2 — NFP transfer depends on prerequisite profile alignment; apparent alignment is insufficient if real prerequisites (adequate risk severity, appropriate comparison arm baseline) differ

NFP's causal mechanism depends on the target population being genuinely at risk — isolated, low social support, high-risk behavior profile — and the comparison arm receiving minimal support. US Elmira and Memphis mothers fit this profile: unmarried, low income, minimal health visiting in the comparison condition. UK mothers designated at-risk still receive substantial NHS health visiting, social services involvement, and welfare support. The comparison arm in UK is not equivalent to the comparison arm in US. The apparent alignment (nurses, at-risk mothers) conceals a real misalignment: the UK context has a much higher baseline for both intervention and control arms. Under H2, the null results are explained by prerequisite absence at the level of risk severity and baseline support gap — not by redesign failure. Weak selector can accommodate this: the program requires a specific real prerequisite (high-risk, low-support population with minimal comparison arm provision) that UK does not have despite apparent alignment.

### H3 — NFP transfer depends on discovering the real prerequisite structure of the implementation environment; fidelity replication without redesign fails to make this discovery

NFP's real prerequisite structure is not fully revealed by the design specifications. The US design assumes a specific risk profile, nurse role configuration, maternal social isolation baseline, and comparison arm condition. In each new context, the real values of these variables differ from the assumed values, and only redesign — systematically examining what the local context actually provides and modifying the delivery structure accordingly — can identify and address the gaps. UK FNP undertook a fidelity replication and treated apparent alignment (nurses, at-risk mothers, infrastructure) as sufficient. It did not redesign the targeting approach to match UK risk distributions, the nurse role to fit NHS care norms, or the programme structure to account for higher baseline social support. The Netherlands VoorZorg programme explicitly redesigned on these dimensions. Under H3, the UK/Netherlands outcome difference tracks redesign quality, not apparent prerequisite alignment. The null result in UK is not explained by population difference alone — it is explained by the absence of a discovery process that would have identified the real prerequisite structure and restructured the programme accordingly.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- US RCT evidence base: Elmira trial (Olds et al., 1997, *JAMA*): children of visited mothers had 56% reduction in verified child abuse/neglect at age 15 compared to control; visited mothers had fewer subsequent pregnancies, longer intervals between births, higher maternal employment; effects largest for highest-risk mothers (low income, unmarried, low social support). Memphis trial (Olds et al., 2004, *Pediatrics*): confirmed effects in a predominantly Black urban population — additional evidence that source results are not ethnically or geographically specific within the US. Denver trial (Olds et al., 2002): paraprofessionals produced weaker effects than nurses, strengthening the case that the nurse role is a specific prerequisite.
- UK FNP programme reach: by 2014, FNP was operating in 132 local authority areas across England with over 10,000 families served annually; demonstrates that the programme is deliverable at scale in the UK context; delivery fidelity data (nurse training completion, visit dosage) shows programme was implemented broadly as designed
- Cross-country adoption: NFP/FNP programmes operating in Canada, Netherlands, Australia, Germany; suggests the basic model is replicable in some high-income contexts; adoption scale is consistent with H1's claim that the model generalizes broadly
- Netherlands positive results: VoorZorg pilot and implementation studies showed positive effects on several outcomes including maternal smoking reduction, breastfeeding initiation, and parenting confidence — consistent with adaptation enabling transfer where fidelity replication did not

## Evidence Against the Claim

- UK Building Blocks RCT (Robling et al., 2016, *BMJ*): largest RCT of NFP outside the US; 1,645 participants across 18 NHS sites; randomized to FNP vs. standard NHS services; primary outcomes: language development at age 2, breastfeeding at age 6 months, subsequent pregnancies, smoking, child maltreatment. Results: **no statistically significant differences** on any primary outcome; several secondary outcomes also null; long-term follow-up (2019) similarly showed no significant effects on child behaviour or development. This is a fully powered, pre-registered trial of a fidelity-replicated programme in a context with strong apparent prerequisite alignment — producing null results.
- Scotland FNP evaluation: smaller implementation evaluation, also showed limited evidence of effects compared to comparison group; consistent with Building Blocks null finding
- Dosage shortfall: Building Blocks trial found UK families received fewer home visits than the programme specifies (median visit number below protocol target); this is a partial H1 defence — implementation quality was lower than intended; but dosage shortfalls are themselves informative about whether the programme structure fits the delivery context, which is a design question not merely an execution question
- Comparison arm confound: UK NICE guidance and NHS practice means comparison arm mothers in UK receive health visiting, which US comparison arm mothers did not receive at equivalent levels; this reduces the treatment-control gap; consistent with H2
- Programme retention: UK FNP showed higher-than-expected programme dropout rates; this could reflect design misfit with UK context (mothers found the intensity or framing inappropriate) — which would be a redesign gap

## Missing Evidence

- Direct comparison of targeting criteria: systematic data on actual risk severity (measured, not assumed) across UK FNP, Netherlands VoorZorg, and US NFP populations would allow independent assessment of whether H2 (real prerequisite absence) or H3 (redesign failure) better explains UK null results; this data would need to be collected consistently across contexts
- Redesign documentation for Netherlands: systematic account of which specific design elements VoorZorg modified and why would allow testing whether Netherlands success tracks the redesign dimensions or other population factors
- Dosage-stratified UK analysis: within the Building Blocks trial, whether families who received protocol-level dosage showed positive effects would help distinguish H1 (execution failure) from H3 (design mismatch); some dosage-stratified analysis exists but is not the primary published finding
- Qualitative implementation data: documentation of how UK nurses experienced and adapted (or failed to adapt) the NFP curriculum to UK mother contexts would help characterize whether low redesign was a choice, a constraint, or a result of inadequate tools for discovery

## Confidence

**High (on NFP efficacy in US source contexts; that nurse home visiting during pregnancy and early childhood improves outcomes in high-risk, low-support populations when intervention-control gap is sufficient) / Low-Medium (on the transfer claim; that the US evidence base justifies expecting comparable results wherever nurses and at-risk mothers are available)**

The US evidence is exceptionally strong and not the source of uncertainty. The UK null results are real and well-powered. The dispute is mechanistic: whether UK failure is explained by real prerequisite absence (H2, weak selector) or redesign failure (H3, adaptation). The Netherlands comparison partially disambiguates this — if similar high-income European context with explicit redesign produces positive effects where UK fidelity replication produced null, that is evidence for H3. The evidence is consistent with H3 but the confounder (UK comparison arm already well-supported) prevents treating this as definitive.

## Open Questions

1. The weak selector defense in RC-031 (UK mothers had higher baseline social support, reducing treatment-control gap) is logically available and partially supported by the dosage shortfall data. But if weak selector requires case-by-case identification of why apparent prerequisites did not constitute real prerequisites — invoking different explanations in each case (MDR-TB in Russia, population risk profile in UK) — does that flexibility make weak selector empirically vacuous? At what point does a series of post-hoc apparent/real distinctions become indistinguishable from unfalsifiability?

2. The adapted-not-replicated pattern now has three instances. None of the three produced a contrary case (a fidelity replication that succeeded). Is the pattern driven by the audit selection criteria — the repository is selecting high-visibility claims where failure is already known — or is it a substantive finding about how complex interventions transfer? The sampling artifact concern from RC-029's Open Question 3 is now a more pressing methodological issue.

3. The NFP source evidence establishes that nurse home visiting works in the US across multiple populations. The UK null result does not refute the source evidence — it raises the question of what the US results actually established. Is the evidence that "nurse home visiting works" or that "nurse home visiting works in high-risk, low-support populations with low comparison arm baseline provision"? The distinction matters for every future deployment decision.

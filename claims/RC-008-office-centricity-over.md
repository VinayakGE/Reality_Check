# Reality Check #008

## Claim

> "Office centricity is over. As of today, Shopify is a digital by default company."

## Source

- Speaker: Tobi Lütke
- Organization: Shopify
- Date: May 21, 2020
- Link: https://twitter.com/tobi/status/1263483496087064579

---

## Why This Matters

This claim is the third resolved audit, chosen to test whether the Supported / Not Supported binary is sufficient. RC-006 (WeWork) collapsed prediction and mechanism together into Not Supported. RC-007 (Bitcoin) split prediction (Supported) from mechanism (Unresolved). RC-008 was selected because the outcome is neither cleanly Supported nor clearly Not Supported. By 2025–2026, the evidence is in: office attendance patterns for knowledge workers have shifted permanently from pre-2020 norms, but "office centricity is over" is not the world that exists. Both halves of that sentence are partially true. If the template cannot represent a partial outcome, RC-008 will surface that gap, as RC-006 and RC-007 surfaced theirs.

---

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The claim is true: office centricity as a cultural default is over
The pre-COVID assumption that professional legitimacy required physical co-presence has been permanently disrupted. Even companies with aggressive return-to-office mandates negotiate hybrid arrangements, offer flexible schedules, and compete for talent partly on location flexibility. The five-day-a-week, all-hands-in-office norm that defined professional culture through 2019 no longer exists as a universal default. In this reading, the claim is about the *default assumption*, not about office attendance percentages.

### H2 — The claim is false: office centricity returned, re-labeled as hybrid
By 2024–2025, Amazon, Dell, JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and others issued five-day return-to-office mandates. Apple, Google, and Meta settled at three days per week, which is office-centric with a carve-out. The dominant pattern for knowledge workers is not digital-by-default — it is office-primary with negotiated exceptions. Lütke's claim reflected a specific moment (COVID-forced experimentation) that produced a durable but moderate shift, not the transformation the claim asserted.

### H3 — The claim is correct for a specific class of company and irrelevant to the rest
"Office centricity is over" turned out to be true primarily for distributed-first software companies (Shopify, GitLab, Automattic, Basecamp) and for roles where geographic flexibility was already being tested pre-COVID. For financial services, healthcare, manufacturing-adjacent, and sales-heavy organizations, office centricity continued without meaningful disruption. The claim was an accurate description of one sector's trajectory, overstated as a universal claim.

---

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Remote and hybrid work arrangements for US knowledge workers stabilized at approximately 25–30% fully remote and 50–55% hybrid by 2023–2024 (BLS, Stanford WFH Research Project data) — a permanent structural shift from the pre-COVID near-zero remote baseline
- Major commercial real estate markets in San Francisco, New York, and Chicago reported office vacancy rates of 20–30% by 2025, levels that had not recovered toward pre-COVID norms — physical evidence of reduced office centricity at the market level
- Fully remote job postings, which were under 3% pre-COVID, stabilized at 10–15% of all knowledge-worker postings by 2024 — below the COVID peak of ~20%, but permanently elevated
- Shopify specifically maintained its remote-first structure through 2025, successfully hiring globally without a physical headquarters requirement — the policy worked for at least the claimant's own organization
- Multiple surveys showed that knowledge workers who had experienced remote work rated office flexibility as a top-3 retention factor, creating permanent employer pressure on the practice regardless of mandate

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Amazon's September 2024 announcement requiring all corporate employees to return five days per week was followed by similar announcements at Dell, AT&T, and several major financial institutions — the trend in 2024–2025 moved toward more office presence, not less
- Shopify itself required significant in-person gatherings (multi-day quarterly events) and has certain management roles requiring physical presence — "digital by default" was qualified in practice
- The Stanford WFH Research Project found that fully remote days for US workers had declined from a peak of ~60% in mid-2020 to approximately 28% by mid-2024, and continued declining — the trajectory contradicts "office centricity is over" if measured by time in office
- Hybrid work, which became the modal arrangement, often means 3 days in office — which is still office-centric in the sense that the office is the primary coordination mechanism, just used on a compressed schedule
- Junior workers and recent graduates showed measurably lower career advancement rates in remote settings according to multiple studies published 2022–2024, creating an institutional incentive for RTO mandates that will likely persist

## Missing Evidence

Not empty, but sparse.

- Long-run equilibrium data: whether the current hybrid arrangements (stabilizing around 2–3 days/week for many knowledge workers) persist through the next economic cycle or collapse back toward full office attendance when labor markets tighten further
- Generational data: whether Gen Z workers, who entered the workforce during COVID, settle into different expectations than Millennials who had pre-COVID office baselines — this would determine whether the cultural shift is durable or cohort-specific

The Missing Evidence section is sparse compared to the Unresolved audits. The evidence has largely arrived. What remains is longer-run trajectory data, not immediate outcome data.

**Neither Supported nor Not Supported. The outcome is partial.**

This is the third consecutive resolved audit to find the Status section insufficient. RC-006 needed Not Supported. RC-007 needed a way to express Supported (prediction) + Unresolved (mechanism). RC-008 needs something between Supported and Not Supported — the claim is partially true at the population level, true for a specific sector, and false for others.

"Office centricity" is objectively reduced from 2019 norms. It is not "over" in any broad sense. The claim was directionally correct and quantitatively overstated. The current four-option status system (Supported / Not Supported / Unresolved / Currently Unresolvable) has no representation for this outcome.

## Confidence

**Medium-High (on partial outcome) / Low (on which H is correct)**

Reason: High confidence that the outcome is partial — both the evidence base and the direction of the claim support this. Low confidence on which alternative explanation is most accurate. H3 (true for distributed-first companies, not for the broader workforce) has the most precise empirical support. H1 (the cultural default has shifted) is also defensible. H2 (office centricity returned, relabeled) is most accurate for financial services and large enterprise. All three may be simultaneously correct for different sectors.

## Review Trigger

The long-run equilibrium trigger: if a major tech company that currently operates hybrid (Google, Meta, Microsoft) issues a five-day RTO mandate by end of 2026, this would move the claim further toward Not Supported. If hybrid arrangements persist without further erosion through 2027 economic cycles, the claim's partial truth becomes more durable.

## Open Questions

1. RC-006 needed Not Supported. RC-007 needed compound status (Supported + Unresolved). RC-008 needs partial status. Three consecutive resolved audits have produced three different template insufficiencies. Is the Status section fundamentally under-specified, or is this sample of resolved claims atypically complex?
2. "Office centricity is over" — is this a prediction (offices will empty out) or a cultural claim (the assumption that office presence equals professionalism is ending)? Those resolve differently. The cultural claim has more support than the attendance prediction.
3. H3 (sector-specific truth) is interesting because it implies that the claim's failure was not a magnitude error but a scope error. The mechanism was right for some populations and wrong for others. Does Reality Check need a way to record scope errors separately from magnitude errors?

# Reality Check #004

## Claim

> "India will achieve 500 GW of non-fossil fuel energy capacity by 2030."

## Source

- Speaker: Narendra Modi
- Organization: Government of India
- Date: November 2021 (COP26, Glasgow)
- Link: https://pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=1775157

---

## Why This Matters

This claim is the adversarial test for Reality Check's emerging pattern. The first three audits all found claims that were difficult for external observers to falsify. This claim should not have that property. The metric is gigawatts — measured by a national grid agency, tracked by the International Energy Agency, independently verified by IRENA and Bloomberg NEF. The owner is a national government with public reporting obligations. The date is fixed. The outcome is binary. If Reality Check cannot audit this cleanly, the problem is the auditing process, not the claims. If it audits cleanly, it wounds the pattern observed in RC-001 through RC-003.

---

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — India reaches 500 GW on schedule
Solar and wind installation rates sustained through 2024 compound to hit the target. Grid infrastructure investment keeps pace. State-level procurement translates into commissioned capacity. The 2030 deadline is met within a reasonable margin.

### H2 — India misses 500 GW but the trajectory is real
Installation rates fall short due to grid bottlenecks, land acquisition delays, financing constraints, or curtailment problems. India reaches 350–450 GW by 2030 — a significant achievement, but a missed commitment. The direction is right; the number is aspirational rather than operational.

### H3 — The target is redefined before 2030
India revises the goal upward (to claim ambition) or adjusts the definition of what counts (to claim success). "Non-fossil fuel" expands to include large hydro, nuclear accounting changes, or installed vs. commissioned capacity is switched mid-measurement. The number is reached on paper through definitional adjustment rather than physical installation.

---

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- India's installed renewable capacity reached approximately 203 GW as of early 2025 (MNRE data), up from roughly 100 GW in 2021 — the installation rate has been accelerating, not stalling
- Solar auction pipelines in 2024 showed 50–70 GW of projects in procurement or construction stages; if commissioned on schedule, they contribute materially toward the 2030 target
- India's solar manufacturing capacity has expanded significantly with the Production Linked Incentive scheme, reducing import dependency and cost risk
- The IEA's 2024 India Energy Outlook projected India as the fastest-growing major economy for renewable additions through 2030
- State-level renewable purchase obligations and the Green Energy Corridor transmission upgrades have created structural demand pull independent of federal targets

## Evidence Against the Claim

- At the 2025 installation rate, reaching 500 GW by 2030 requires approximately 50–60 GW of new capacity added annually for five consecutive years — India has not yet demonstrated that annual run rate at scale
- Grid integration is the binding constraint: India's transmission infrastructure has not kept pace with generation additions, and curtailment rates in high-renewable states (Rajasthan, Gujarat) were increasing through 2024
- Land acquisition for utility-scale solar and wind remains slow due to state-level regulatory variation, community opposition, and competing agricultural use — several large projects have faced multi-year delays
- Financing costs remain elevated for state electricity distribution companies (DISCOMs), which are the primary offtakers; several DISCOMs have payment arrears to existing generators, which depresses willingness to sign new long-term agreements
- Large hydro (which India counts toward the target) faces its own delays: several Himalayan projects have been stalled by environmental review, seismic concerns, and China border-adjacent restrictions

---

## Missing Evidence

- Current annual installation rate for 2025 and 2026 — the trend from 2025 onward is the most predictive input and is not yet fully visible
- Transmission capacity additions: whether Green Energy Corridor Phase II is commissioned on schedule determines how much of the generation pipeline can actually reach the grid
- Whether India's accounting methodology for the 500 GW includes large hydro, nuclear, and small hydro consistently — the number shifts by 30–60 GW depending on what is included

## Confidence

**Medium**

Reason: This is the first audit where confidence can be stated above Low. The terms are defined, the data is public, and the trajectory is partially visible. H2 (India misses but the direction is real) has the most empirical support as of mid-2026: the installation rate is meaningful but not yet sufficient to guarantee the 500 GW target on schedule. The claim is not on track to be comfortably met, and not so far off track that it can be dismissed. It is genuinely unresolved — but for evidentiary reasons, not definitional ones.

**This is the first claim in the repository that is unresolved because of missing evidence, not because of missing definitions.**

## Review Trigger

- India's Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) annual capacity report showing 2026 or 2027 installed figures — if the annual addition rate reaches 55+ GW, H1 becomes plausible; if it stalls below 40 GW, H2 becomes likely
- IEA or IRENA annual tracking report specifically assessing India's 2030 target progress
- Any official revision to the 500 GW target or its definition (would confirm H3)

---

## Open Questions

1. Does "500 GW non-fossil fuel" include large hydro? India's MNRE has used different definitions in different documents. The answer shifts the baseline by approximately 45 GW and materially changes the assessment.
2. Is the binding constraint generation capacity, transmission capacity, or DISCOM financial health? The policy response differs for each, and the answer determines whether the gap is closable by 2030.
3. If India reaches 480 GW by 2030, does the claim succeed or fail? There is no stated tolerance band. This is a real ambiguity — but it is a measurement ambiguity, not a definitional one.

# Reality Check #003

## Claim

> "We will have AGI by 2030. Possibly sooner."

## Source

- Speaker: Sam Altman
- Organization: OpenAI
- Date: November 2024 (Axios interview; variants repeated in January 2025 blog post)
- Link: https://blog.samaltman.com/reflections

---

## Why This Matters

Unlike RC-001 and RC-002, this claim has an expiration date. Either something called AGI exists before January 1, 2030, or it does not. That should make it more testable than the previous two claims. Whether it actually is depends entirely on whether "AGI" resolves into something observable — which is where the claim gets complicated. But the stakes are high enough to run it regardless: if this claim is directionally right, most other technology predictions become secondary. If wrong, it implies the current investment cycle is built on a timeline that doesn't hold.

---

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The claim is true under the definition OpenAI is using
OpenAI's internal definition of AGI, stated in their governance documents, is "AI that is at least as capable as a human at most economically valuable work." That is a productivity and labor-market threshold, not a consciousness or general reasoning threshold. Under this definition, the claim may already be partially true in specific domains, and fully true before 2030 is plausible given the trajectory of coding, research synthesis, and professional task completion in current models.

### H2 — The claim is true as a marketing event, not a technical one
OpenAI declares AGI on a schedule that serves its governance structure — specifically, the clause in its Microsoft agreement that triggers a change in IP access upon AGI declaration. "AGI before 2030" happens because OpenAI announces it, not because external observers agree. The technical state of AI in 2028 or 2029 is irrelevant to whether the claim is satisfied; what matters is whether OpenAI's board makes a declaration. This is a different kind of truth than the claim implies.

### H3 — The claim is false because the hard problems don't yield to scale
Current models fail at sustained multi-step reasoning under novel constraints, reliable self-correction, and causal inference that generalizes outside training distribution. These are not engineering problems that more compute solves; they may require architectural innovations that are not on a predictable timeline. The 2030 deadline comes from extrapolating scaling curves that may be flattening. The claim is sincere but based on a model of progress that the evidence is beginning to contradict.

---

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- GPT-4 passed the bar exam, USMLE, and CPA exam at or above human-average performance in 2023; subsequent models have extended this across professional certification tests — if "economically valuable work" is the threshold, multiple domains are already crossed
- The compute inputs to frontier models have scaled consistently with predicted capability gains through at least mid-2025; no sustained inflection point suggesting a wall has been publicly confirmed
- AI systems are now generating novel scientific hypotheses in biology and materials science that are subsequently verified experimentally — a capability that was considered a hard AGI prerequisite as recently as 2021
- Google DeepMind's AlphaProof solved International Mathematical Olympiad problems at gold-medal level in 2024, representing a form of novel reasoning under constraint that previous systems could not perform
- The leading labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, xAI) have all set internal milestones in the 2025–2028 range for what they internally call AGI-equivalent capability — revealed in governance documents, investor decks, and regulatory filings

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Benchmark saturation: models that score near-ceiling on professional exams still fail simple physical reasoning, spatial tasks, and novel logical puzzles that humans with no domain training solve immediately — the capability profile does not match general intelligence
- Scaling curves for leading models show measurable slowdown in benchmark improvement per unit compute in 2024–2025, with multiple labs privately and publicly acknowledging that the next capability jump requires something other than more data and compute
- No current model reliably corrects its own errors in real-time without external verification — a human professional who cannot self-check is not a human professional
- The "economically valuable work" definition is circular: work becomes economically valuable partly because humans can reliably do it; an AI that does work unreliably at scale does not meet the economic threshold even if it occasionally performs above human average
- Every prior confident AGI timeline from credible researchers has been wrong; the history of AI is a history of capability thresholds being reached without triggering the predicted general competence

---

## Missing Evidence

- A reliable, third-party benchmark for "general capability" that does not saturate within 18 months of creation — current benchmarks are created and saturated faster than consensus forms around what they mean
- Whether the compute scaling slowdown visible in 2024–2025 is a temporary plateau (as after the AlexNet era) or a structural ceiling approaching a hard limit
- How OpenAI's governance-linked definition of AGI interacts with their public claims — if the Microsoft contract clause drives the declaration, the "claim" is not about AI capability at all

## Confidence

**Low — with a specific note**

Reason: This is the first audit where the definitional problem is *known and documented by the claimant*. OpenAI has published their AGI definition. That makes RC-003 different from RC-001 and RC-002: the terms are defined, but the definition was chosen specifically to be reachable. A claim designed to be satisfiable by its author is not the same as a falsifiable prediction. The audit cannot assess whether AGI will arrive by 2030 — it can only assess whether OpenAI will *declare* it. Those may not be the same event.

## Review Trigger

- OpenAI, DeepMind, or Anthropic makes a formal AGI declaration before 2030
- A third-party evaluation (academic consortium, government body, or independent benchmark) reaches consensus that a deployed system meets a specified general capability threshold
- Scaling curves for frontier models show sustained benchmark improvement resuming after 2025 plateau (would support H1); or plateau extends beyond 18 months with no architectural breakthrough (would support H3)
- The Microsoft–OpenAI AGI clause is triggered, revealing what threshold was actually used

---

## Open Questions

1. Is there a meaningful difference between "AGI declared by OpenAI" and "AGI achieved"? The claim is from OpenAI's CEO. His definition applies. Does that make the claim self-fulfilling rather than predictive?
2. If models continue to fail at physical reasoning, spatial tasks, and robust self-correction, but pass all professional certification benchmarks — has AGI arrived?
3. Is 2030 a prediction or a commitment? OpenAI's commercial relationships and governance structure may create incentives to declare AGI on a schedule independent of capability.

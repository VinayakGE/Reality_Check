# Reality Check #022

## Claim

> "Conditional cash transfer programs reduce poverty and improve health and educational outcomes. Countries at all income levels should adopt them as a cornerstone of social protection policy."

## Source

- Speaker: Ariel Fiszbein and Norbert Schady (World Bank economists); representative of a broad consensus across the IDB, World Bank, UNDP, and affiliated researchers
- Organization: World Bank; *Conditional Cash Transfers: Reducing Present and Future Poverty* (World Bank Policy Research Report, 2009)
- Date: 2009 (flagship synthesis report); the claim has been continuously reinforced by World Bank country programs through 2024
- Link: https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/handle/10986/2597

*Note: Mexico's PROGRESA program (Prospera, Oportunidades) is the evidentiary foundation for the global claim. PROGRESA was explicitly designed as a randomized rollout from 1997 onward — one of the first government anti-poverty programs anywhere to use random assignment of treatment. The causal identification is unusually strong for a large-scale government program. The policy inference — that CCT programs should be adopted globally — rests substantially on PROGRESA's evidence base.*

---

## Why This Matters

Conditional cash transfer programs have become the dominant anti-poverty tool in Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and increasingly in OECD countries. More than 60 countries now operate CCT programs, reaching approximately 760 million people (World Bank, 2022). The scale of global adoption — informed substantially by PROGRESA's results — makes the question of external validity more than academic. If CCTs produce PROGRESA-equivalent effects globally, the policy community's enthusiasm is well-founded. If external validity depends on institutional and contextual conditions that many adopting countries lack, the program's effectiveness is being systematically overstated in global policy.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — PROGRESA's evidence is robust and the global CCT model generalizes
PROGRESA's random rollout allows clean causal identification that is not available in most social programs. The results across multiple independent evaluations are consistent: households receiving transfers show significantly higher school enrollment rates (12–18 percentage points at secondary level), higher health clinic attendance, and reduced short-term poverty. The program's conditionality design (transfers contingent on children attending school and health visits) addresses two primary channels simultaneously. Brazil's Bolsa Família — the world's largest CCT, reaching 50 million people — has produced similar directional effects to PROGRESA at an order of magnitude larger scale. The mechanism is robust: giving cash to mothers conditional on health and education behaviors changes household investment patterns in ways that accumulate over time. The conditionality addresses coordination failures (families know education is valuable but face short-term cash constraints that prevent compliance).

### H2 — PROGRESA's effects depend on institutional complements that many CCT adopters lack
PROGRESA was implemented in a context with pre-existing infrastructure: public schools within reach of recipient communities, health clinics to verify compliance, a functioning administrative system for transfer delivery, and a relative absence of complementary distortions (labor markets where education genuinely increases returns). In contexts where these complements are absent — where schools are low quality, health clinics are inaccessible, administrative capacity for compliance verification is weak, or local labor markets do not reward education — CCT programs may produce lower returns despite similar design. The evidence from CCT adoptions in Sub-Saharan Africa, where complementary infrastructure is thinner, is more mixed than the Latin American evidence. The program design (cash conditional on behaviors) is replicable; the institutional environment required for those behaviors to produce the projected outcomes is not.

### H3 — The conditionality requirement generates compliance theater rather than behavioral change in low-capacity contexts
CCT programs require that recipients demonstrate compliance with specified behaviors (school attendance, health visits) to receive transfers. In high-capacity settings like Mexico, compliance monitoring is administrative and relatively rigorous. In low-capacity settings, compliance requirements may be difficult to enforce, leading to either: (a) de facto unconditional transfers where compliance is assumed rather than verified, removing the behavioral incentive; or (b) compliance theater where recipients fulfill formal requirements (showing up at a clinic once) without the substantive behavioral change (preventive care engagement) that the program intends. Several African CCT programs have moved to unconditional formats precisely because conditionality enforcement proved too administratively demanding. If the conditionality is the active ingredient, its absence undermines the theoretical mechanism.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- PROGRESA randomized evaluation (Schultz, 2004; Behrman et al., 2005; Gertler, 2004): school enrollment increased 3–4% at primary and 12–18% at secondary level; health clinic visits increased 35%; stunting in children reduced by ~1 cm per year of program exposure; consumption poverty fell among recipients
- Brazil's Bolsa Família: long-run evidence (Chiavari and Gorenstein, 2021; Paes-Sousa et al.) shows similar directional effects at scale; extreme poverty fell significantly during the period of Bolsa Família expansion; a 2021 natural experiment (program interruption) showed immediate negative welfare effects when transfers stopped, demonstrating behavioral dependence on ongoing transfers
- Mexico long-run evaluation (Molina-Millán et al., 2019): children who received PROGRESA transfers as young children had 10–16% higher earnings as adults, demonstrating intergenerational effects beyond the initial behavioral changes
- World Bank meta-analysis (Baird et al., 2014): CCT programs in 75 countries showed consistent positive effects on school enrollment across contexts; effect sizes varied but direction was consistent
- Indonesia's PKH program, Philippines' 4Ps, and Colombia's Familias en Acción all produced directional results consistent with PROGRESA, suggesting the model does transfer at least within middle-income countries with comparable institutional capacity

## Evidence Against the Claim

- CCT programs in Sub-Saharan Africa have produced smaller and more variable effects than Latin American programs: Tanzania's PSSN, Zambia's MCTG, and Kenya's CT-OVC programs showed modest educational effects but limited intergenerational human capital accumulation, consistent with H2's institutional complement hypothesis
- The conditionality contribution is itself contested: a systematic review (Baird et al., 2011) comparing conditional vs. unconditional cash transfers found that the conditionality requirement did not consistently add value beyond the cash transfer itself; if conditionality is not the active ingredient, programs that enforce it incur administrative costs for uncertain marginal benefit
- Effect size variation is large across contexts even within successful programs: school enrollment effects in PROGRESA ranged from 3–18 percentage points depending on baseline enrollment rates, child age, and local school quality; contexts with near-universal primary enrollment (as in much of East Asia and Latin America now) have limited room to show enrollment effects, raising the question of what CCTs do when enrollment is not the binding constraint
- Middle-income country adoption context has changed: PROGRESA operated in 1997–2002 Mexico with large rural populations with low baseline enrollment; many current CCT adopters are in contexts where the low-hanging-fruit effects have already been achieved through other means (conditional enrollment increases), and CCTs face diminishing returns
- Fiscal sustainability has emerged as a binding constraint: Brazil's Bolsa Família expansion contributed to fiscal pressure that the Brazilian government has struggled to sustain across political transitions; the program's political economy of maintenance is a transferability challenge not captured in the original evaluation

---

## Missing Evidence

- Long-run intergenerational evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa CCT programs: the Latin American evidence on intergenerational effects requires 15–20 year follow-up periods; Sub-Saharan African programs are not yet old enough to produce comparable evidence
- Experimental comparison of conditional vs. unconditional programs at scale: the Baird et al. (2011) systematic review compared across different program contexts; a within-context randomized comparison of conditional vs. unconditional formats at PROGRESA's scale does not exist
- Institutional capacity thresholds: at what level of administrative capacity, school quality, and health infrastructure does CCT effectiveness switch from positive to near-zero? The threshold has not been empirically identified

## Confidence

**Medium-High (on CCT effectiveness in middle-income countries with PROGRESA-comparable infrastructure) / Low-Medium (on global generalization)**

Reason: PROGRESA's causal identification is unusually strong, and its effects in Mexico are well-established. The transfer to other middle-income countries with comparable institutional capacity (Brazil, Colombia, Indonesia, Philippines) has been largely confirmed. The transfer to low-income countries with thinner institutional complements is more contested, with smaller and more variable effects. "Global adoption" as a policy prescription overstates the evidence base for low-capacity contexts.

Whether the structure would appear with equal frequency in other domain clusters is not yet known.

**Watch B (does the bottleneck recur?):**

**Watch B does not fire.**

Pre-registered expectation confirmed.

PROGRESA's causal identification is strong enough that the source-context causal attribution — conditional cash transfers in rural Mexico increased school enrollment, health visits, and reduced short-term poverty — is not meaningfully contested. The program was designed as an RCT; multiple independent evaluations have confirmed the directional effects; the long-run earnings effects have been replicated with additional data. The source-context outcome is observable and its cause is not in dispute.

The primary uncertainty is at the transfer step. Two distinct mechanisms:
1. **Institutional complement gap**: CCTs require functioning schools, health clinics, and administrative capacity; these vary across adopters
2. **Conditionality effectiveness in low-capacity enforcement contexts**: the behavioral change mechanism may require rigorous compliance monitoring that many adopters cannot maintain

Both are transferability uncertainties, not source-context attribution disputes.

## Open Questions

1. The pilot-to-policy structure now has three instances (RC-017, RC-018, RC-022), all from policy/social science domains. If this structure appears in a non-policy domain in RC-023–025, the structural recurrence is more likely genuine. If it stays confined to policy domains, it may be an artifact of how policy evidence is generated and used.

2. The institutional complement gap mechanism in RC-022 is related to but distinct from the political economy / infrastructure dependency in RC-020 and the cultural context in RC-019. The mechanisms are becoming more specific as the sample grows. Should the transferability sub-table distinguish between "context creates obstacles to adoption" (RC-019, RC-020) and "context lacks the prerequisites for the mechanism to operate" (RC-022)? These seem like different failure modes: the first is about resistance, the second is about missing preconditions.

3. RC-022 raises the question of whether PROGRESA's evidence is a good model for global CCT adoption, or whether the program's success has been used to justify adoption in contexts where the required institutional complements do not exist. This is an example of what might be called "evidence borrowing" — the specific causal claims from one context being used to justify programs in a different context without validating that the transfer conditions hold. Is evidence borrowing a general phenomenon in the repository, or is it specific to development economics and its policy adoption dynamics?

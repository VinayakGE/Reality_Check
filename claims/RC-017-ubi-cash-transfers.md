# Reality Check #017

## Claim

> "The evidence from guaranteed income pilots is now strong enough to conclude that direct cash transfers reduce poverty without meaningfully reducing work effort. A Universal Basic Income would work."

## Source

- Speaker: Michael Tubbs (Mayor of Stockton, CA; founder, Mayors for a Guaranteed Income)
- Organization: Stockton Economic Empowerment Demonstration (SEED); synthesis of academic literature by Ioana Marinescu (Penn), Damon Jones (Chicago Booth), and David Looney (Chicago Booth)
- Date: March 2021 (SEED final report, published in *PNAS*); reinforced by Finland UBI results (February 2020) and GiveDirectly Kenya long-run results (ongoing, 2017–present)
- Link: https://www.stocktondemonstration.org/; https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2206220119

*Note: The claim as evaluated here is the inference from pilot evidence to universal policy — that what worked in pilots would work at scale as a national or universal program. The pilots themselves have counterfactuals (control groups); the universal policy they are being used to justify does not. The gap between the evidence base and the policy claim is the primary evaluation problem.*

---

## Why This Matters

Direct cash transfer programs have expanded significantly: the US Child Tax Credit expansion (2021) temporarily cut child poverty nearly in half before expiring; guaranteed income pilots now operate in more than 100 US cities; multiple countries (Kenya, Finland, Namibia) have run large-scale pilots. The academic literature on cash transfers in low-income countries is now large enough to support meta-analyses.

If the pilot evidence correctly predicts universal program behavior, this is among the most important policy findings in modern social science — direct cash transfers are administratively simple, welfare-state-bypassing, and consistent with both left (poverty reduction) and libertarian (freedom of choice) frameworks. Several prominent economists (Milton Friedman's negative income tax, Charles Murray's "In Our Hands") have endorsed cash transfer variants across the political spectrum.

If the inference from pilots to universal policy is invalid, billions in guaranteed income spending may produce different outcomes than pilots predict — either better (program effects are underestimated in small pilots) or worse (general equilibrium effects, price inflation, labor supply responses at scale that don't appear in small pilots). The direction of inference error is not obvious.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The pilot evidence is robust and generalizes: UBI would work as claimed
Multiple independent pilots — Finland (2017–2018, n=2,000), Stockton CA (2019–2021, n=125), GiveDirectly Kenya (2017–present, n=~20,000), Manitoba Mincome (1970s) — have consistently found: (1) modest to no reduction in work effort among recipients; (2) measurable improvements in mental health, food security, and child outcomes; (3) some recipients using cash for productive investment (starting businesses, education). The consistency across very different populations, transfer amounts, and institutional contexts is the strongest argument for generalization. The evidence base for cash transfers is now more robust than for many social programs that have been scaled without hesitation.

### H2 — Pilot results do not generalize to universal programs because of scale effects and selection
Small pilots produce results that cannot be extrapolated to universal programs for three reasons. First, general equilibrium effects: if all workers in a local economy receive transfers, labor supply falls, wages may adjust, and landlords may capture transfer income through rent increases — none of which appear in small pilots where the local economy is unaffected. Second, expectation effects: recipients in a time-limited pilot know the income is temporary and treat it differently than permanent guaranteed income. Third, selection: pilots attract self-selected participants or target vulnerable populations; universal programs include populations whose responses may differ. The SEED program had 125 participants in a city of 320,000 — its effects cannot be multiplied by population without solving the generalization problem.

### H3 — Work disincentive effects are real but the welfare comparison is still positive
Recipients of cash transfers do reduce work effort measurably. The Finland experiment found a 6-day/year reduction in days worked; the SEED program found full-time employment increased among recipients (partially offsetting the concern). The honest reading of the evidence is: some participants reduce work, others use the income to enable job search or investment, and the net effect on work effort is near zero or modestly negative. The policy question is whether the welfare gains from poverty reduction and economic security outweigh the welfare costs of modestly reduced labor supply. That is a values question, not an empirical one, and the claim as stated ("without meaningfully reducing work effort") pre-decides the values question by minimizing the work reduction evidence.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- SEED (Stockton) final results (Lowrey et al., *PNAS* 2023): full-time employment among recipients increased from 28% to 40% over 24 months vs. 25% to 37% for control group; income volatility fell; mental health improvements significant
- Finland UBI experiment (Kela, 2020): 2,000 unemployed recipients received €560/month for two years; recipients reported higher wellbeing, worked marginally more than control group (6 additional days/year), and maintained employment search behavior
- GiveDirectly Kenya long-run evaluation (Egger et al., *QJE* 2022): large-scale cash transfers in rural Kenya showed sustained income and asset gains 3 years post-transfer; general equilibrium effects in treated villages were positive, not negative — local economic activity increased
- Meta-analysis of 165 cash transfer studies in low-income countries (Baird et al., 2018): consistent positive effects on consumption, education, and health; work effort effects small and heterogeneous
- Natural experiment from Alaska Permanent Fund (annual cash dividend to all Alaskans since 1982): no measurable negative employment effects in 40-year longitudinal analysis (Jones & Marinescu, *JPE* 2022)

## Evidence Against the Claim

- All major pilots are either geographically limited (Stockton: 1 city), population-limited (Finland: unemployed only), or conducted in contexts (Kenya, Namibia) where generalization to high-income universal programs is unclear — the Alaska Permanent Fund is the closest analog to a universal high-income-country program but involves a relatively small annual amount ($1,000–$2,000/year)
- General equilibrium effects observed in GiveDirectly Kenya villages were positive in a labor-scarce rural context; in high-income countries with more elastic labor markets, price effects (especially housing) could offset income gains for non-recipients and reduce the program's net welfare impact
- The US Child Tax Credit expansion (2021) provides suggestive evidence: child poverty fell sharply while the expansion was in effect; labor force participation among affected parents did not fall significantly. But the program expired before generating long-term data, and the political conditions for a permanent program remain contested
- No pilot has been run at national universal scale in a high-income country with a large, diverse labor market — the inference from "pilots in selected cities or countries" to "nationwide universal program" crosses a scale threshold where general equilibrium effects become potentially significant
- The most credible concern — that permanent income guarantees produce different behavioral responses than time-limited pilots — cannot be tested without a permanent universal program, creating a methodological catch-22: the evidence required to establish generalization requires the policy to be implemented first

---

## Missing Evidence

- A national, permanent universal cash transfer program in a high-income country: this is the evidence that would directly test the policy claim. It does not exist. The Alaska Permanent Fund is the closest approximation but involves amounts and contexts that limit generalization
- Long-run labor supply effects in high-income country pilots: most pilots run 1–3 years. Whether work reduction effects persist, accumulate, or reverse over 10+ years is unknown. The permanence assumption (UBI is permanent income, not temporary) cannot be tested in time-limited pilots
- Price and rental market responses at universal scale: if all households in a housing market receive additional income, landlords may capture a significant share through rent increases, reducing the net welfare benefit. This has not been measured in small-scale pilots

## Confidence

**Medium (on the pilot-level findings) / Low (on universal-program generalization)**

Reason: The claim has two separable components. The pilot-level findings — cash transfers at tested scales produce the measured outcomes — are well-supported by multiple independent studies with control groups. Confidence in the pilot findings is Medium-High.

The policy claim — that these findings generalize to a universal national program — is significantly less supported. The gap between pilot evidence and universal policy involves general equilibrium effects, permanence effects, and scale effects that have not been empirically tested. Confidence in the universalization inference is Low.

The confidence split here is structurally similar to RC-016 (SPARC demonstration vs. commercial plant deployment) and RC-009 (near-term stagnation vs. Japan-style lost decade). In each case, the nearer-term or smaller-scale component has better evidence than the larger-scale or longer-horizon claim. The word "Universal" in Universal Basic Income is doing the same work as "commercial" in RC-016's "commercial fusion" — it is the term that requires the generalization inference for which the evidence is thinner.

## Watch Item Assessment

**Watch A (does the structure repeat?):**

RC-017 introduces a structure that has not appeared before: **pilot-to-policy inference claim** — experimental evidence exists and is relatively clean at the tested scale, but the policy claim overstates to a scale at which the counterfactual is unavailable and general equilibrium effects have not been measured.

The closest prior structures are:
- RC-013 (Hall RCT proves contribution, not primacy): evidence is available for a specific proposition, claim requires evidence for a broader proposition. But RC-013's gap was about effect size; RC-017's gap is about scale and equilibrium effects.
- RC-004 (measurable prediction with clear terms): no, RC-017 involves inference from controlled evidence, not a policy commitment.
- RC-016 (demonstrated milestone + contested deployment pathway): related — the pilots are the "demonstrated milestone" and the universal program is the "deployment pathway."

Tentative new structure: **Pilot-to-policy inference claim** — controlled evidence at test scale; contested generalization to universal scale; counterfactual available at test scale, unavailable at policy scale. Zero clean Watch A repeats. Count: still 0.

**Watch B (does the bottleneck recur?):**

This is the primary test for RC-017.

The bottleneck in RC-017 is not straightforwardly "outcome visible, cause uncertain." The *pilot* outcomes are visible and attribution is relatively clean (randomized controls provide causal identification). The Watch B structure would apply at the policy level: if a universal program were implemented and produced different outcomes than pilots predicted, the cause of the divergence (general equilibrium effects? permanence effects? scale effects? all three?) would be contested.

But Watch B does not fully apply because the universal policy has not been implemented. The claim is a prediction about what a universal program would do, and the bottleneck is not causal attribution of an observable outcome — it is the inference gap between pilot evidence and policy prediction.

**Assessment: Watch B does not cleanly apply at the policy level because the outcome (universal program performance) has not occurred. The bottleneck is inferential, not attributional.**

This is the first RC-017-level finding: the counterfactual structure here is different from RC-010, RC-015, and RC-016. In those cases, the policy or event occurred and the counterfactual (what would have happened otherwise) was unavailable. In RC-017, the *policy itself* has not occurred at universal scale — the counterfactual is unavailable because the event hasn't happened, not because we can't rerun history with different conditions.

This is a meaningfully different type of counterfactual unavailability:

| Instance | Type |
|---|---|
| RC-010 | Event occurred; counterfactual world unavailable |
| RC-015 | Policy enacted; counterfactual world unavailable |
| RC-016 | Technology not yet deployed; deployment counterfactual unavailable |
| RC-017 | Policy not enacted at scale; cannot observe universal-scale outcomes |

RC-017's counterfactual unavailability is not "we can't rerun history" — it is "the thing being claimed has never been tried at the scale claimed." This is closer to RC-016 (technology not yet deployed) than to RC-010/RC-015 (event occurred, counterfactual world missing).

**Watch B count: still 7.** RC-017 does not add a clean instance. The bottleneck is inferential scale-mismatch, not outcome-cause uncertainty. This is the first audit in the Watch B series that has not added an instance.

**Dependency test result:** RC-017 has counterfactual unavailability (at the policy scale) but does not cleanly trigger Watch B (because the universal-policy outcome has not occurred to attribute). This is partial evidence against the dependency — but it may be partial because RC-017 has a different *type* of counterfactual unavailability than prior instances. The dependency was observed in cases where an event occurred and its counterfactual was missing. RC-017 is a case where the event hasn't occurred. These may be different phenomena wearing the same label.

**Implication:** "Counterfactual unavailability" may need to be disaggregated into at least two types:
- Type A: Event occurred; counterfactual world cannot be observed (RC-010, RC-015, RC-016)
- Type B: Event has not occurred at scale; outcome at universal scale is unknown (RC-017)

Type A consistently co-occurred with Watch B. Type B may not — because Watch B requires an observable outcome to attribute, and Type B's outcome hasn't been observed.

This distinction is not compression. It is a count-level observation: the three prior instances of counterfactual unavailability may be Type A; RC-017 may be Type B. If that holds, the dependency (counterfactual → Watch B) may apply specifically to Type A, not to counterfactual unavailability in general.

## Open Questions

1. RC-017 reveals that "counterfactual unavailability" may cover two distinct situations: (A) an event occurred and the alternative cannot be observed; and (B) an event has not occurred at universal scale and cannot be inferred cleanly from pilots. These are different bottlenecks wearing the same label. Should they be tracked separately? The answer affects whether the three prior instances of counterfactual unavailability are a coherent recurrence or a heterogeneous cluster.

2. The pilot-to-policy inference problem in RC-017 (evidence exists at test scale, claim applies at policy scale) may be a general feature of social science policy claims. If multiple claims in the repository have this structure without it being named, it may be more common than visible. Should Reality Check flag the scale of evidence vs. scale of claim as a variable at audit entry?

3. Watch B did not fire in RC-017. This is the first audit since Watch B was identified where the bottleneck type did not appear. But it may not be a disconfirmation of Watch B — the outcome the claim makes hasn't occurred. Is "outcome not yet occurred" (RC-014 structure) a general category that is immune to Watch B by definition? If so, Watch B may apply only to claims about events that have already happened.

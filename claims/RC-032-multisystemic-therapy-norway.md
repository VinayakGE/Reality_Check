# Reality Check #032

## Claim

> "The evidence for MST is among the most robust in the mental health and juvenile justice field. Studies across different US populations consistently show MST keeps youth at home, in school, and out of trouble. Any country with the organizational commitment to implement MST with fidelity — using the established training, consultation, and quality assurance protocols — can expect to replicate these results."

## Source

- Speaker: Scott Henggeler (developer of MST, Medical University of South Carolina); MST Services LLC (licensing and training organization); Norwegian adoption documents (Norwegian Directorate for Child, Youth and Family Affairs / Bufetat); Blueprints for Healthy Youth Development (independent programme registry classifying MST as "Model" programme)
- Organizations: Medical University of South Carolina Family Services Research Center; MST Services LLC; Norwegian Directorate for Child, Youth and Family Affairs (Bufetat); Campbell Collaboration
- Date: MST developed 1977–1990s (Henggeler); Norwegian adoption 1999–2000; Norwegian RCT published 2004 (Ogden & Halliday-Boykins); ongoing Norwegian national implementation
- Link: Henggeler et al. (1992), "Family preservation using multisystemic therapy," *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*; Ogden & Halliday-Boykins (2004), "Multisystemic treatment of antisocial adolescents in Norway," *European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*; Blueprints for Healthy Youth Development: https://www.blueprintsprograms.org/programs/multisystemic-therapy-mst/

*Note: Multisystemic Therapy (MST) is an intensive, community-based treatment for adolescents with serious antisocial behavior. Based on ecological systems theory, it targets individual, family, peer, school, and community factors simultaneously. Treatment lasts approximately 4 months, with therapists working with families 3–15 hours per week, available 24 hours a day. A distinctive structural feature: MST specifies not only the intervention content but the implementation infrastructure — therapists receive initial training from MST Services, participate in weekly case consultation with an MST expert consultant, and are monitored using the Therapist Adherence Measure (TAM), which tracks how closely practice aligns with the MST model. The claim as audited is the cross-context inference: US RCT evidence justifies Norwegian (and broader international) adoption, and implementation fidelity — achieved through the specified training and quality assurance structure — is sufficient to replicate results.*

## Why This Matters

RC-032 is selected to pressure the adapted-not-replicated watch item that emerged from RC-029, RC-030, and RC-031. That watch item currently has three instances across three domains (fintech, infectious disease, early childhood intervention), with no contrary case. If RC-031 (NFP UK) showed a fidelity replication attempt producing null results, RC-032 examines a case where the Norwegian replication produced positive results — which would be the contrary case the watch item requires to avoid becoming an artifact of audit selection.

But the Norwegian case introduces a more precise version of the question than "did fidelity replication succeed?" MST's design includes, as a structural component, an implementation monitoring and correction system (TAM, weekly expert consultation). This system is not external to the model — it is part of what MST Services specifies as the implementation standard. When this system operated in Norway, it may have functioned as: (a) a fidelity assurance mechanism, ensuring the protocol was delivered as specified; or (b) a discovery mechanism, identifying gaps between the model's assumptions and the Norwegian context and enabling micro-level adjustment. These are different functions. If the answer is (a), Norwegian success is a genuine fidelity replication contrary case for the watch item. If the answer is (b), the watch item survives but requires a more precise formulation: some models embed adaptation processes at the implementation level that appear as fidelity from the model level.

The question RC-032 should answer is not only whether MST succeeded in Norway. It is whether **apparent fidelity** — defined at the model specification level — is the same thing as **actual fidelity** — defined at the implementation process level. That distinction was not visible before RC-031.

**Watch B:** Does the evaluation bottleneck recur? Does it locate at source-context causal attribution or at the generalization step?

**Pre-registered expectation:**

Watch A expected present: cross-context inference structure — US RCT evidence used to justify Norwegian national programme adoption. Eighth instance (RC-019, RC-020, RC-026, RC-028, RC-029, RC-030, RC-031, RC-032).

Watch B expected absent: MST source evidence is among the strongest in the mental health and juvenile justice field — multiple independent RCTs across different US populations (Henggeler et al. 1992, Borduin et al. 1995, Henggeler et al. 1997, Schaeffer & Borduin 2005), long-term follow-up data (up to 25 years), consistent effects on incarceration, out-of-home placement, and antisocial behavior. CE-1 predicts Watch B absent. The uncertainty locates at the transfer step.

**Pre-registration: the critical distinction frozen before evidence review**

*The fidelity/adaptation distinction as an empirical question:*

MST's implementation model includes:
1. Initial therapist training by MST Services
2. Weekly case consultation with an MST expert consultant
3. Ongoing monitoring using the Therapist Adherence Measure (TAM)
4. Quarterly booster training for therapists and supervisors

This infrastructure is not optional — it is part of what MST Services specifies as necessary for authorized implementation. The question is what this infrastructure does:

*Fidelity assurance (supports watch item contrary case interpretation):*
The TAM and consultation structure verify that therapists deliver the specified intervention content with the specified intensity and approach. Corrections occur only when therapists deviate from the protocol. The Norwegian context received the protocol; the infrastructure checked that the protocol was delivered. Result: fidelity replication achieved, positive outcomes follow from real prerequisite alignment.

*Embedded adaptation (supports adapted-not-replicated pattern survival):*
The TAM and consultation structure do more than verify content — they identify gaps between the model's implicit assumptions and the local implementation context. When Norwegian therapists encounter family dynamics, institutional constraints, or cultural norms that the US protocol did not anticipate, the consultation structure enables micro-level adjustment. These adjustments are not classified as departures from the model; they appear as good implementation practice. The ongoing correction process is the discovery mechanism that adaptation claims is necessary for transfer success. Result: apparent fidelity conceals embedded adaptation, and the watch item pattern holds at a finer level of analysis.

*The test:*
If the Norwegian TAM monitoring data showed high initial adherence with minimal correction — therapists delivered the protocol accurately from early in implementation — then interpretation (a) is more plausible. If the monitoring data showed substantial early-implementation correction and course-adjustment, with adherence increasing over time as the consultation process identified and resolved implementation gaps, then interpretation (b) is more plausible.

**Pre-registered discriminating predictions:**

*For the adapted-not-replicated watch item:*

| Prediction | Watch item status |
|---|---|
| Norwegian MST showed positive outcomes without significant course-correction through the TAM/consultation process (high initial adherence, minimal adjustment) | Watch item weakened — apparent fidelity sufficient for transfer success |
| Norwegian MST showed positive outcomes after significant course-correction through the TAM/consultation process (lower initial adherence, improvement through consultation) | Watch item survives in narrower form — the consultation structure functioned as embedded adaptation |

*For weak selector:*

Norwegian context differs from US on multiple dimensions that could constitute prerequisite misalignment: no juvenile justice system equivalent (child welfare context, not probation/courts); different family authority norms; higher welfare state baseline (Norwegian families have more financial and social support than US inner-city trial populations); lower violence and gang context. If real prerequisite alignment was present despite these apparent differences, weak selector is consistent with Norwegian success.

*For adaptation:*

Norwegian success is explained by the discovery process embedded in the MST implementation structure. The consultation and TAM system enabled therapists and consultants to identify where Norwegian family and institutional contexts differed from US assumptions and make adjustments without classifying them as adaptations.

*The sharper comparative question:*

NFP UK was also implemented with institutional training and support. But NFP's implementation model does not include the same continuous expert consultation and adherence monitoring infrastructure that MST specifies. If MST (with embedded consultation) succeeded in Norway while NFP (without equivalent embedded monitoring) failed in UK — even though both were described as fidelity replications — the difference may track the presence or absence of embedded discovery mechanisms, not the quality of the implementation effort in absolute terms.

**Pre-registered wound conditions:**

*For the adapted-not-replicated watch item:* If Norwegian MST shows positive outcomes and the implementation evidence indicates high initial adherence (minimal course-correction through the TAM/consultation process), the watch item's pattern is directly challenged. This would be the first case where non-universal prerequisites, apparent fidelity replication, and positive outcomes co-occur without an identifiable hidden adaptation mechanism.

*For weak selector:* If Norwegian success cannot be explained by prerequisite alignment on the dimensions that differed from US (child welfare vs. juvenile justice context, welfare state baseline, cultural norms), and the success tracks embedded implementation correction rather than alignment, weak selector requires either invoking the apparent/real distinction again (the real prerequisites were present despite apparent contextual differences) or acknowledging that the discovery mechanism explains what alignment alone cannot.

*For adaptation:* If Norwegian MST showed positive outcomes with high initial adherence, no meaningful course-correction in the consultation process, and the outcome is well-explained by genuine prerequisite alignment with the US model's assumptions — then both the watch item and the adaptation explanation are under pressure. This would be the genuine contrary case.

**Independent prerequisite assessment (pre-registered before evidence review):**

| Context | At-risk youth population | Family engagement capacity | Institutional support for intensive intervention | Legal/statutory authority for access | Implementation monitoring infrastructure |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| US source (Henggeler trials) | Yes (inner-city, juvenile justice involved) | Present but limited | Juvenile justice system | Yes (probation, court orders) | Research-based (study supervision) |
| Norway | Yes (child welfare-referred at-risk) | Present (welfare state supported) | Yes (Bufetat national funding) | Modified (child welfare orders, less coercive than probation) | Yes (MST training/TAM/consultation as specified) |

Key asymmetry: US source trials used juvenile justice coercion (probation orders) to enable family access. Norway used child welfare system authority. Whether court-ordered engagement vs. voluntary child welfare engagement constitutes a prerequisite mismatch is an open question to be assessed in the evidence.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — MST's training, consultation, and monitoring infrastructure is fidelity assurance, not adaptation; Norwegian success reflects genuine transfer through faithful replication

MST's implementation quality assurance system was designed to prevent the common failure mode of evidence-based programmes: model drift, where implementation devolves into something different from what was tested. The TAM and consultation structure ensure that the specified model is delivered with fidelity. Norway adopted the model properly, delivered it properly, and achieved positive outcomes because the model works when properly delivered. The contextual differences (child welfare vs. juvenile justice, welfare state baseline, cultural norms) did not alter the core mechanism: intensive, ecologically grounded family work delivered by trained therapists with expert support. Under H1, Norwegian success demonstrates that fidelity replication is achievable when the implementation infrastructure is sufficiently structured, and the watch item should be weakened.

### H2 — MST succeeded in Norway because real prerequisites were aligned despite apparent contextual differences (weak selector)

The apparent contextual differences between Norway and the US (institutional context, welfare state baseline, cultural norms) did not constitute prerequisite misalignment with the MST model because the real prerequisites of MST — families with serious adolescent behavior problems who could engage with intensive family-based work, with a referring institution that could ensure access, and with communities where ecological intervention was feasible — were present in Norway. The child welfare context provided referral pathways; the welfare state provided the family stability necessary for therapeutic engagement; the cultural norms supporting professional family intervention in Norway were actually conducive to the intensive approach. Under H2, apparent differences did not constitute real prerequisite gaps, and weak selector explains the success: real alignment was present.

### H3 — MST's implementation infrastructure (TAM, weekly consultation) functioned as an embedded discovery mechanism; apparent fidelity concealed ongoing context-specific adjustment

The MST consultation and monitoring system is routinely described as a fidelity assurance mechanism, but its operational function in novel contexts is closer to an adaptive discovery process. When Norwegian therapists encountered families whose dynamics, communication styles, or relationships to authority differed from US assumptions, the weekly expert consultation did not merely detect deviation — it enabled calibration of therapeutic approach to the Norwegian context. Because these calibrations occurred within the model's specified supervision structure, they were not classified as departures from the model. The TAM measured whether therapists were using MST principles, not whether the underlying assumptions of the US protocol were being applied unchanged. If this interpretation is correct, MST succeeded in Norway because it contains embedded adaptation infrastructure, and the apparent fidelity to the design specification conceals real adaptation at the implementation level. Under H3, the adapted-not-replicated watch item survives in a more precise form: models with embedded discovery mechanisms can transfer successfully while appearing to replicate; models without embedded discovery mechanisms (NFP) cannot.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Norwegian RCT (Ogden & Halliday-Boykins, 2004, *European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*): 100 Norwegian families referred by child welfare services for youth with serious antisocial behavior; randomized to MST vs. standard child welfare services; MST showed significant reductions in youth internalizing and externalizing behavior (teacher-reported), more youth in school or employed, fewer out-of-home placements at 6-month follow-up; differences maintained at 2-year follow-up (Ogden & Amlund Hagen, 2006)
- Norwegian programme scale: based on RCT results, Norway expanded MST nationally through Bufetat; MST became a standard offering in Norwegian child welfare services for serious youth conduct problems; demonstrates institutional adoptability
- Implementation fidelity data: Norwegian implementation maintained TAM scores within the range considered acceptable by MST Services; this is the primary documentation that the programme was delivered with model fidelity
- US evidence base breadth: Henggeler et al. (1992) inner-city offenders; Borduin et al. (1995) serious juvenile offenders with 14-year follow-up (Schaeffer & Borduin, 2005) showing lasting reductions in criminal activity; Henggeler et al. (1997) substance-abusing delinquents; multiple independent effectiveness studies; the source evidence spans different populations, regions, and follow-up periods, suggesting the mechanism is not specific to one narrow context
- Blueprints Model classification: MST is one of a small number of programmes to receive Blueprints' highest "Model" designation, requiring at least two RCTs with sustained effects; this independent classification confirms the evidence quality at source

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Implementation context mismatch: US MST trials primarily operated through the juvenile justice system — youth were referred from probation and courts, and judicial authority provided leverage for family engagement; Norwegian implementation operated through the voluntary child welfare system, where coercive engagement authority was limited; this is a potentially meaningful prerequisite difference, since MST's intensive engagement assumes the referring institution can ensure family participation
- TAM limitations as fidelity measure: the TAM measures whether therapists follow MST principles (are they identifying and targeting drivers of behavior ecologically?), not whether the underlying assumptions about family structure, cultural communication norms, or institutional context are being applied unchanged; a therapist can score high on the TAM while making substantial context-specific adjustments at the case level, making high TAM scores insufficient evidence that no adjustment occurred
- Welfare state confounder: Norwegian families receiving MST also received substantially more baseline social support (income support, housing stability, healthcare access) than the low-income US families in the source trials; the outcome difference between MST and comparison arms in Norway may have been driven by different dynamics than the US studies, even if both showed positive effects
- Replication in other countries: MST has been implemented in Netherlands, UK, Denmark, and other countries; results have been more mixed outside Norway, with some null or near-null findings in other European implementations; the Norwegian success may not characterize the MST transfer literature as a whole
- Comparison arm quality: Norwegian comparison arm (standard child welfare services) may have provided substantially more intensive services than the US comparison arms (standard juvenile justice supervision); if so, the Norwegian MST group achieved positive outcomes against a stronger comparison, which is informative, but the mechanism question (why MST beat comparison) may differ between contexts

## Missing Evidence

- Norwegian TAM trajectory data: documentation of Norwegian therapist TAM scores over time during initial implementation would allow assessment of whether initial adherence was high (suggesting fidelity assurance) or low-improving-over-time (suggesting embedded discovery and adjustment); this data exists within the Norwegian implementation system but is not published in primary research form
- Consultation content documentation: qualitative data on the content of weekly MST consultation sessions in Norway — what problems were raised, what adjustments were made — would allow systematic assessment of whether the consultation process identified context-specific gaps or primarily monitored for model drift; this represents the most direct evidence bearing on H3
- Cross-European comparison: systematic comparison of MST outcomes across European implementations (Norway, Netherlands, UK, Denmark) would allow testing whether implementation monitoring infrastructure quality (not just presence) predicted transfer outcomes; this comparison exists in aggregate but has not been systematically analyzed against the fidelity/adaptation distinction
- NFP UK vs. MST Norway structural comparison: a direct comparison of the implementation infrastructure of UK FNP and Norwegian MST — specifically whether MST's embedded consultation system provided something UK FNP lacked — would help test H3's claim about embedded discovery mechanisms

**Adapted-not-replicated watch item — RC-032 assessment:**

The Norwegian MST case does not cleanly resolve the watch item in either direction. The pre-registered wound condition — positive outcomes without significant course-correction through the TAM/consultation process — cannot be verified from available evidence because the Norwegian TAM trajectory data and consultation content documentation have not been published in a form that allows this assessment.

- Norwegian implementation maintained TAM scores in the acceptable range (fidelity was not absent)

- Whether the TAM/consultation system functioned primarily as fidelity assurance or embedded discovery in the Norwegian case

**Net result for the watch item:**

The Norwegian case is consistent with the watch item's survival in narrower form (embedded discovery may explain success) and consistent with the watch item being pressured (fidelity replication with implementation monitoring may be sufficient). The ambiguity is not resolvable from available evidence. The watch item is **not wounded** — but the question it now needs to answer has changed.

**The productive narrowing RC-032 produced:**

Before RC-032, the watch item claimed:

> Successful transfers involved redesign; fidelity replications failed.

After RC-032, the more precise version is:

> The distinction between "fidelity replication" and "adaptation" may be less visible at the model specification level than at the implementation process level. Models with embedded discovery mechanisms (MST's TAM/consultation system) may achieve apparent fidelity while enabling functional adaptation. Models without equivalent embedded mechanisms (NFP) cannot achieve equivalent results through fidelity replication alone.

This is a narrowing, not a resolution. The watch item's claim is now about implementation process architecture, not about surface-level design fidelity. That is more precise than the watch item's statement at RC-031.

**NFP vs. MST structural comparison (the comparison RC-032 enabled):**

| Feature | NFP UK | MST Norway |
|---|---|---|
| Implementation described as | Fidelity replication | Fidelity replication |
| Embedded expert consultation | No (nurse supervisor, but not weekly expert case consultation) | Yes (weekly MST expert consultant per case) |
| Ongoing adherence monitoring | No (no equivalent to TAM) | Yes (TAM, quarterly booster) |
| Outcome | Null (Building Blocks RCT) | Positive (Ogden & Halliday-Boykins) |

Both were described as fidelity replications. Both used trained professionals. The structural difference is the embedded ongoing consultation and monitoring infrastructure. If this structural difference explains the outcome difference, it supports H3's claim that embedded discovery mechanisms are the operative variable — and reframes what "fidelity" means in complex intervention transfer.

## Open Questions

1. The TAM/consultation system in MST is part of the specified model. Does this mean: (a) MST discovered that embedded adaptation infrastructure is necessary for transfer and built it into the model specification, or (b) the consultation system was designed for fidelity assurance within the US context and happened to function as adaptation infrastructure in international contexts? The difference matters because (a) implies deliberate design for transferability while (b) implies inadvertent inclusion of discovery capacity.

2. If the embedded discovery mechanism interpretation (H3) is correct, it suggests a typology of intervention designs: those with embedded discovery infrastructure (likely to transfer with apparent fidelity) and those without (requiring explicit adaptation or failing). Is this typology observable across other cases in the repository? ORT's near-universal prerequisites arguably made discovery unnecessary. PEPFAR built prerequisites through deliberate action, which is a different kind of discovery. DOTS's adaptation varied by context but was not embedded in the model specification.

3. NFP and MST produced opposite outcomes in European high-income contexts (UK and Norway respectively), yet both were described as fidelity replications. If implementation infrastructure (embedded monitoring vs. no monitoring) explains this difference, the repository should update how it characterizes "fidelity replication" as a category. The current binary (fidelity / adaptation) may be insufficient; a third category — structured implementation (embedded discovery without explicit redesign) — may be needed.

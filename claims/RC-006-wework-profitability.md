# Reality Check #006

## Claim

> "WeWork is a technology company. Our mission is to elevate the world's consciousness. Our company's valuation and size is just a matter of perspective."

> [Supplemented by investor roadshow projections, August 2019]: WeWork will be cash-flow positive in 2019 and achieve profitability at scale in 2020–2021.

## Source

- Speaker: Adam Neumann
- Organization: WeWork (The We Company)
- Date: August 2019 (S-1 prospectus filed with the SEC, August 14, 2019; investor roadshow materials, August–September 2019)
- Link: https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1533523/000119312519220749/d781982ds1.htm

---

## Why This Matters

This is the first Reality Check audit with a past deadline. WeWork filed for bankruptcy on November 6, 2023. The claim is not unresolved — it resolved, negatively, four years after the S-1 was filed. The question this audit is answering is not "what will happen?" but "what did happen, and what does the template do with a finished outcome?" If the template handles a resolved claim cleanly, the path-to-resolution framing is confirmed for claims at the evidentiary end of the spectrum. If the template cannot accommodate a finished audit, the Status section has a structural gap.

---

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — WeWork was a legitimate business temporarily overwhelmed by capital misallocation
The core business — co-working space — had genuine unit economics in specific markets. Neumann's spending, governance failures, and the SoftBank relationship created a situation where a viable business was wrapped in an unsustainable structure. Had WeWork IPO'd at a rational valuation and operated without the related-party transactions, a path to profitability existed. The claim was wrong about the timeline; the underlying business was not fraudulent.

### H2 — WeWork was a real estate company that sold itself as a technology company to access software-company valuation multiples
The "technology company" framing was the operative claim, and it was false. WeWork signed long-term leases and sub-let on short-term contracts — the business model was a leveraged real estate bet with no proprietary technology. The profitability projection was structurally impossible at the claimed growth rate because the unit economics deteriorated as the company expanded. The S-1 financials showed this clearly; the claim was inconsistent with the published data at the moment it was made.

### H3 — SoftBank's capital created the conditions for the claim to appear plausible and then destroyed the conditions for it to be true
With unlimited capital, WeWork could grow fast enough that future unit economics might have justified the structure. The Vision Fund's decision to cut off funding in late 2019 — not the business model itself — triggered the collapse. The claim was conditionally true given continued access to capital, and false once that access was removed. This is a different failure mode: the claim was dependent on an external variable (SoftBank's continued investment) that was never disclosed as a dependency.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- WeWork's community-adjusted EBITDA (a metric the company invented) was positive in several reporting periods, indicating that at the individual location level, stripped of corporate overhead, some locations were profitable
- Membership growth was real: WeWork grew from 220,000 members in 2017 to 527,000 in H1 2019 — the demand for the product existed
- Post-bankruptcy, WeWork emerged from Chapter 11 in June 2024 with a restructured balance sheet, suggesting the underlying business had residual value and was not entirely fictitious
- Several co-working competitors operating on the same model (IWG/Regus) have been consistently profitable — the business model itself is not inherently unprofitable

## Evidence Against the Claim

- WeWork lost $1.9 billion in H1 2019, the same period the S-1 was filed — the company was losing money faster than it was growing revenue
- The S-1 itself showed that WeWork's lease obligations ($47 billion) dwarfed its committed revenue from members ($4 billion) — the structural gap was disclosed in the document projecting profitability
- WeWork's lease liabilities were primarily long-term (10–15 years) while member contracts were month-to-month — a mismatch that made the business model acutely sensitive to any demand downturn
- The "technology company" claim was operationally unfounded: WeWork had no proprietary software platform, no defensible data asset, and no technology licensing revenue — it was a commercial real estate operator with better interior design
- The company went public via SPAC in October 2021 at a $9 billion valuation, down from $47 billion in 2019; it was delisted in November 2023 after filing for bankruptcy — the trajectory was continuous decline, not a temporary setback

## Missing Evidence

None material. The claim has resolved. WeWork filed for bankruptcy in November 2023. The profitability projection was not met. The "technology company" claim was not validated by any independent assessment. The evidence needed to assess this claim arrived and was unambiguous.

**This is the first audit where the Missing Evidence section has nothing to report.**

## Current Status

- [ ] Supported
- [ ] Unresolved
- [ ] Currently Unresolvable

**None of the above. The claim is Not Supported.**

The template's Status section does not include a "Not Supported" option. This is the first audit that has surfaced that gap. WeWork did not become cash-flow positive in 2019 or profitable at scale in 2020–2021. It filed for bankruptcy in 2023. The claim resolved negatively and completely.

This finding — that the Status section lacks a category for "claim resolved, outcome was opposite to prediction" — is an observation about the template, not a reason to change it yet. But it is worth noting that all prior audits were Unresolved; this is the first audit where a definitive outcome exists and none of the three status options accurately captures it.

---

## Confidence

**High**

Reason: This is the first audit where confidence is High. The outcome is known. WeWork went bankrupt. No reasonable alternative reading of the evidence produces a different conclusion. H1 (the business was viable, mismanaged) is partially true but does not rescue the profitability claim. H3 (capital dependency was the hidden variable) is the most charitable reading and is probably accurate — but "conditionally true given unlimited capital" is not what the claim stated.

---

## Review Trigger

None. The claim is resolved. Review would only be appropriate if WeWork's post-bankruptcy entity makes new claims about its path to profitability — at which point a new audit would be warranted, not a revision of this one.

---

## Open Questions

1. Should the template include a "Not Supported" status? Five audits produced Unresolved. One produced a definitive negative outcome with no applicable status. If the next ten audits include more resolved claims, the gap becomes a structural problem rather than an edge case.
2. H3 (the claim was conditionally true, dependent on continued SoftBank capital) is the most analytically interesting reading. Does a claim that is true under conditions never disclosed in the claim count as a false claim or a hidden-dependency claim? That distinction matters for how Reality Check handles corporate financial projections going forward.
3. The S-1 disclosed the structural gap (lease obligations vs. committed revenue) at the same moment the profitability claim was being made. Does a claim that is contradicted by the claimant's own concurrent disclosures belong in a different category than a claim that was simply wrong?

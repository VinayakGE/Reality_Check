# Reality Check #024

## Claim

> "People with AIDS in places like Haiti and Rwanda are no different from people with AIDS in Boston. They deserve the same treatment. And they can receive it — the infrastructure constraints cited as prohibitive are not a technical barrier, they are a resource and political-will barrier."

## Source

- Speakers: Paul Farmer (Partners in Health co-founder) and Jim Yong Kim (Partners in Health co-founder; later World Bank President); position operationalized at scale by PEPFAR (President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief), announced by George W. Bush, State of the Union Address, January 28, 2003
- Organizations: Partners in Health (PIH) — demonstrating feasibility in Haiti (1998) and Rwanda (2002); PEPFAR — $15 billion initial commitment (2003), scaled to $85 billion through 2022, supporting ART delivery in 50+ countries
- Date: PIH Haiti demonstration: 1998 onward; *Pathologies of Power* (Farmer, 2003) articulates the claim most explicitly; PEPFAR announcement: January 2003; PEPFAR reauthorizations: 2008, 2013, 2018
- Link: Farmer & Kim on ART feasibility: *New England Journal of Medicine* (2004), "Unfair Advantage and the Health of Nations"; PEPFAR: https://www.pepfar.gov; UNAIDS global ART coverage: https://www.unaids.org/en/resources/documents/2022/2022_global-aids-update

*Note: The claim has two components. First, a mechanistic claim: ART can be delivered effectively in resource-limited settings in sub-Saharan Africa — the absence of contextual prerequisites is not a fundamental barrier if resources are mobilized. Second, a scale prediction: large-scale ART delivery will substantially reduce HIV/AIDS morbidity and mortality in SSA. Both components are evaluated.*

*Context: The dominant expert view in 2001–2003 was that ART delivery in SSA was not feasible at scale. A Harvard School of Public Health report (2001) cited logistical challenges, adherence monitoring requirements, drug resistance risk, and healthcare worker scarcity as prohibitive. Farmer and Kim's claim was made directly against this expert consensus.*

---

## Why This Matters

At the time PEPFAR was announced, approximately 25 million people in sub-Saharan Africa were living with HIV. AIDS-related deaths in SSA peaked at approximately 1.3 million in 2004. Antiretroviral therapy was available and effective in wealthy countries; in SSA, treatment coverage was below 2% of those in need. The expert consensus held that the prerequisites for effective ART delivery — clinical monitoring, drug supply chains, trained providers, adherence support infrastructure — were too context-specific and too absent to permit large-scale transfer of the treatment model.

Farmer and Kim's argument was that this framing misidentified the problem. The prerequisites were not universally absent; they were *absent because no one had invested in building them*. The distinction matters for the repository's current leading candidate explanation. If PEPFAR succeeded because resources built prerequisites that were initially absent, the correct causal variable is not prerequisite universality but prerequisite buildability. That is a different claim about what drives transfer success — and it generates a different prediction about future cases.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — ART's mechanism generalizes universally once prerequisites are deliberately built
HIV suppression by antiretroviral therapy is biochemistry — it works identically in every HIV patient regardless of geography. The prerequisites for delivery (trained providers, drug supply, clinical monitoring, adherence support) were context-specific but *buildable* given adequate resource commitment. Farmer and Kim's PIH model demonstrated this in conditions more constrained than most of SSA: in rural Haiti with no electricity grid, no running water, and no trained physicians initially, community health workers (accompagnateurs) provided adherence support, simplified treatment protocols reduced monitoring requirements, and clinical outcomes matched wealthy-country standards. PEPFAR then operationalized this demonstration at national scale. Generic drug pricing negotiations (Clinton Foundation, 2003) reduced treatment costs from $12,000 to $140 per patient per year, collapsing the resource barrier that had underwritten most "not feasible" arguments. The transfer succeeded because the intervention model was designed to build prerequisites rather than assume them.

### H2 — PEPFAR's success reflects exceptional, non-generalizable resource mobilization
PEPFAR was the largest single commitment to an international disease program in history. The $85 billion committed through 2022 operated with US diplomatic pressure, negotiated generic drug agreements, established parallel supply chains bypassing dysfunctional domestic systems, and in many contexts built healthcare infrastructure exceeding host country baseline capacity. Other high-burden infectious diseases (malaria, tuberculosis, neonatal sepsis, pneumonia) causing comparable mortality in SSA, without equivalent resource commitment, have not shown equivalent progress. The lesson from PEPFAR may not be that complex interventions can transfer when prerequisites are built — it may be that essentially unlimited resource mobilization can overcome any context-specific gap. The case may not generalize to interventions without comparable political and financial support.

### H3 — ART's global success conceals significant heterogeneity and dependency that limits the claim's scope
PEPFAR's aggregate statistics obscure variation. The UNAIDS "90-90-90" targets (90% of people living with HIV diagnosed, 90% on treatment, 90% virally suppressed) have been met in some contexts but not achieved broadly across SSA as of 2022; the region had reached approximately 83% at the first stage, 80% at the second, 73% at the third, producing an effective treatment cascade of approximately 59% viral suppression among all PLHIV — not 73%. Loss to follow-up at 12 months remains 20–40% in many settings. Key populations (MSM, sex workers, people who inject drugs) have been systematically underserved due to criminalization and stigma in recipient countries. In some countries, PEPFAR funds more than 80% of the national HIV response; fiscal sustainability questions parallel Bolsa Família (RC-022) — the transfer may require permanent external resource mobilization rather than producing self-sustaining national systems.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- UNAIDS (2022): 28.7 million people on ART globally, up from approximately 300,000 in 2002; AIDS-related deaths globally fell from 1.9 million (2004) to 650,000 (2021)
- PEPFAR attribution (Bendavid & Bhattacharya, 2009, *Annals of Internal Medicine*): quasi-experimental comparison of PEPFAR-eligible vs. non-PEPFAR countries matched on epidemic characteristics; PEPFAR countries showed significantly larger mortality reductions; estimated 1.1–1.2 million deaths averted in the first five years
- Botswana national ART program (launched 2002 with PEPFAR and Gates Foundation support): adult HIV mortality reduced by approximately 70% from peak within eight years of scale-up; the program reached >95% treatment coverage among eligible patients within a decade
- PIH demonstration results: Cange, Haiti (1998 onward) — treatment outcomes among rural Haitian patients on ART were comparable to outcomes reported in wealthy-country clinical settings; adherence rates exceeded 90% using accompagnateur model; the "not feasible" expert consensus was empirically refuted in the most constrained possible setting
- Generic drug price reduction: Clinton Foundation negotiations (2003) reduced first-line ART from $12,000 to $140 per patient per year; the cost argument that anchored "not feasible" claims collapsed within months of serious negotiation
- Provider training: PEPFAR-supported programs trained approximately 300,000 health workers across supported countries through 2019; healthcare worker scarcity — cited as a fundamental barrier in 2001 — proved addressable through investment
- Uganda case study: AIDS-related mortality fell by approximately 60% between 2005 and 2015 in Uganda, correlating directly with treatment scale-up; Uganda had been cited in 2001 as a high-complexity, high-burden context where ART delivery was not feasible

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Attribution problem: PEPFAR's launch coincided with the Global Fund for AIDS, TB and Malaria (established 2002), generic drug availability, improved rapid diagnostic tests, and domestic health system strengthening across recipient countries; isolating PEPFAR's specific contribution from concurrent improvements requires quasi-experimental methods with untestable assumptions
- Heterogeneous treatment cascade: viral suppression among all PLHIV in SSA was approximately 59% as of 2022 — far below the 73% implied by meeting 90-90-90 targets — with men, adolescents, and key populations showing significantly lower rates than aggregate statistics suggest
- Loss to follow-up: systematic reviews find 20–40% patient loss to follow-up at 12 months in many SSA settings; long-term retention — not initiation — is where the transfer model shows the most stress
- Fiscal dependency: in countries including Mozambique, Uganda, and Kenya, PEPFAR funds more than 60–80% of the national HIV response; when US foreign policy shifts affect PEPFAR funding (as occurred under several administrations), countries lack the domestic fiscal capacity to maintain programs; the transfer built delivery capacity but not financial sustainability
- Conditionality distortions: the original PEPFAR legislation (2003–2008) required recipients to sign an anti-prostitution pledge and allocated at least one-third of prevention funding to abstinence-only programs; these requirements restricted harm reduction for sex workers and distorted delivery in contexts where sex work was a primary HIV transmission route

---

## Missing Evidence

- Counterfactual on key populations: what would HIV incidence and mortality in MSM, sex workers, and people who inject drugs in SSA look like under a model without criminalization constraints on PEPFAR delivery? This is not a data gap — it is a permanently unavailable counterfactual (Type A)
- Self-sustaining transfer test: whether domestic health systems in recipient countries could maintain ART delivery at current scale without PEPFAR funding has not been tested because PEPFAR funding has not been withdrawn at scale; the transfer's durability is unobserved
- Generic ART cost trajectory contribution: what proportion of the access improvement is attributable to cost reduction (a price mechanism) vs. PEPFAR delivery infrastructure (a system-building mechanism)? Both occurred simultaneously; causal attribution within the PEPFAR success story has the same structure as the ORT attribution problem

**Prerequisite taxonomy update:**

RC-023 and RC-024 now define two distinct routes to transfer success:

| Route | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Prerequisites already present | Near-universal prerequisites; no construction required | RC-023 (ORT) |
| Prerequisites successfully constructed | Context-specific prerequisites; built through deliberate resource mobilization | RC-024 (ART/PEPFAR) |

The three Unresolved pilot-to-policy cases (RC-017, RC-018, RC-022) share a third profile: context-specific prerequisites that were *not* built, either because resource mobilization was insufficient, the prerequisites proved less buildable than assumed, or the transfer has not yet been attempted at scale.

This generates a revised taxonomy — replacing the binary Universal/Context-Specific with a three-branch structure:

```
Prerequisite Condition
├── Near-Universal (present in essentially all transfer contexts)
│   └── Example: RC-023 — water, salt, sugar for ORT
├── Context-Specific and Buildable (absent, but constructable given resources)
│   └── Example: RC-024 — drug supply, monitoring, trained providers for ART
└── Context-Specific and Not (Yet) Built
    └── Examples: RC-017, RC-018, RC-022 — institutional complements for CCT/UBI/housing
```

**The decisive variable is no longer prerequisite universality alone.** RC-024 demonstrates that context-specific prerequisites that are *buildable* and *built* produce the same transfer outcome as near-universal prerequisites. The variable that predicts transfer success is not the nature of the prerequisite but whether the prerequisite is present at the moment of transfer — either because it always was, or because it was constructed.

**What this does not resolve:** RC-024 does not tell us what makes prerequisites buildable in some cases and not in others. PEPFAR involved exceptional resource mobilization ($85 billion over 20 years), US diplomatic leverage, and strong civil society advocacy. Whether the "buildable" category is a genuine general property of certain prerequisite types, or merely a descriptor for "sufficiently funded," remains open.

**Candidate explanation update:**

The prerequisite-universality explanation that emerged from RC-023 is now refined by RC-024:

*Version 1 (from RC-023):* Transfer succeeds because prerequisites already exist.

*Version 2 (from RC-024):* Transfer succeeds because prerequisites can be built faster than the intervention scales, given sufficient resource mobilization.

These are different causal structures. Version 1 requires no deliberate construction. Version 2 requires deliberate construction, which requires resources, political will, and buildable prerequisites. The repository now has one instance of each.

The two versions are not competing — they may both be true, describing different routes to the same outcome. But Version 2 is less predictive than Version 1: "prerequisites can be built" is only useful as an explanation if we know in advance which prerequisites are buildable and what resources are required.

**Pilot-to-policy pattern update (5 instances):**

| RC-017 (UBI) | Unresolved | Present | Context-specific, not built |

## Open Questions

1. PEPFAR's prerequisite construction required $85 billion, US diplomatic leverage, and twenty years. Does "buildable" denote a property of the prerequisites themselves (can they be built in principle?) or a property of the resource environment (were they built in this case because of exceptional mobilization)? If the latter, the "buildable" branch may not be a stable category — it may be a residual for "well-funded cases," which is not explanatory.

2. RC-024 introduces fiscal sustainability as a distinct unresolved dimension: the ART delivery model transferred, but the *financing* for that model has not transferred from PEPFAR to domestic health budgets. Is fiscal sustainability a form of transferability that operates at a longer timescale than the outcomes audited here? If so, RC-024's Supported status may require a time horizon qualifier.

3. The repository now has two Supported cases in the pilot-to-policy structure (RC-023, RC-024) and three Unresolved cases (RC-017, RC-018, RC-022). The Supported cases are both in global health / public health; the Unresolved cases are in social policy and development economics. Is the domain clustering real? Or does the prerequisite condition account for the variation across domains?

# Reality Check #030

## Claim

> "DOTS — Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course — is the proven solution for global tuberculosis control. Any country that implements DOTS with fidelity can achieve treatment success rates of 85% or above. The technical requirements are manageable across a wide range of health system capacities; political commitment is the primary binding constraint."

## Source

- Speaker: WHO Global Tuberculosis Programme; Karel Styblo (International Union Against Tuberculosis and Lung Disease, IUATLD); World Bank technical assistance documents; Arata Kochi (WHO TB chief from 1991)
- Organizations: WHO (DOTS declared global strategy, 1991; Stop TB Partnership, 2000); World Bank (funding and technical endorsement through 1990s–2000s); MSF / Partners in Health (later critics of narrow DOTS formulation)
- Date: DOTS formalized as WHO strategy: 1991; global target (85% treatment success, 70% case detection) announced: 1991 and codified in 1993–1994; restated continuously through Stop TB Partnership and WHO Global TB Reports through present
- Link: WHO Global TB Report (annual): https://www.who.int/teams/global-tuberculosis-programme/tb-reports; Styblo IUATLD bulletins (1980s); Kochi (1991) "The global tuberculosis situation and the new control strategy of the World Health Organization," *Tubercle*

*Note: DOTS is a five-component strategy: (1) political commitment with increased and sustained financing; (2) case detection using quality-assured bacteriology (sputum smear microscopy); (3) standardized treatment supervised directly by health workers or community volunteers; (4) an uninterrupted drug supply; (5) a monitoring and evaluation system. The strategy originated in clinical research by Karel Styblo in Tanzania and the Netherlands in the 1970s–1980s, then tested in pilot programs in Tanzania and China before WHO adopted it globally. The claim as audited is the cross-context inference: that the DOTS strategy proven in Tanzania and pilot contexts generalizes to high-burden countries across differing health system architectures.*

---

## Why This Matters

Tuberculosis remains one of the leading infectious disease killers globally — approximately 1.5 million deaths annually as of 2022 (WHO). For most of the twentieth century, TB was considered a disease of poverty with no scalable treatment protocol for low-income settings. DOTS represented the claim that a systematic, reproducible strategy could work. Since 1995, approximately 76 million lives have been saved under DOTS-related programs by WHO estimates. If the DOTS transfer claim is correct — that any country with political commitment can achieve 85%+ success rates — the binding constraint for TB control is governance, not health system architecture. If incorrect — if DOTS only transfers where specific design adaptations are made to fit local prerequisites — then the current framing of TB control priorities is misspecified.

RC-030 is selected as the primary discriminating test between the selector explanation (strong form) and the adaptation explanation that emerged from RC-029. The claim's historical record includes cases that should disaggregate the two explanations: India and Russia both received major DOTS implementation efforts in the 1990s–2000s with roughly comparable political backing, but produced strikingly different outcomes. The question is whether that difference is explained by pre-existing prerequisite alignment (strong selector) or by the quality of design adaptation to available prerequisites (adaptation).

**Pre-registration: definitions frozen before evidence review**

*Strong selector (the form under test in RC-030):*

Transfer success depends primarily on pre-existing prerequisite alignment between the intervention package and the available infrastructure in the transfer context. The DOTS package requires: laboratory infrastructure for sputum smear microscopy, a reliable drug supply chain, health worker capacity to supervise treatment, and basic clinic infrastructure for patient registration and follow-up. Transfer contexts with higher pre-existing alignment on these dimensions should outperform contexts with lower alignment. Redesign is not excluded, but it is not the mechanism — alignment is. If redesign creates alignment in a context that previously lacked it, that is the adaptation explanation, not selector.

*Adaptation (the competing explanation):*

Transfer success depends on whether implementing organizations actively redesigned the DOTS delivery package to fit available social, institutional, and health infrastructure in the transfer context. The original DOTS model assumed clinic-based DOT supervision by trained health workers. Transfer contexts that redesigned the supervision mechanism around available infrastructure — community health workers, village volunteers, private practitioners in adapted roles — should outperform contexts that attempted to replicate clinic-based supervision without structural adaptation, even when the replicating context had nominally higher baseline health infrastructure.

The critical difference: strong selector is static (match is discovered), adaptation is dynamic (match is created through redesign). The two explanations diverge when a context lacks pre-existing alignment but succeeds through redesign, or when a context has pre-existing alignment but fails because redesign was not attempted.

**Pre-registered discriminating prediction:**

*Primary test — India/Russia ordering:*

India (RNTCP, launched 1993; national scale 1997–2006):
- Baseline infrastructure: lower than Russia at time of DOTS introduction (health worker density, laboratory capacity, drug supply variability all challenging)
- Redesign effort: high — India explicitly adapted DOTS delivery away from clinic-based observation toward community health workers, village DOT providers, and "patient-wise box" drug packaging systems; integrated with existing village health worker infrastructure; District TB Officer coordination model

Russia (DOTS introduction, 1990s–):
- Baseline infrastructure: higher than India at time of DOTS introduction (Soviet-era health system included laboratory infrastructure, sanatoria with trained staff, established drug distribution channels)
- Redesign effort: low — Russia attempted to layer DOTS onto the existing Soviet-era tuberculosis sanatoria system without fundamental restructuring; sanatoria used inpatient, lengthy treatment models rather than outpatient short-course supervised treatment

*Strong selector predicts:* Russia performs at least comparably to India (Russia's higher pre-existing health infrastructure aligns better with DOTS prerequisites).

*Adaptation predicts:* India substantially outperforms Russia (India redesigned delivery; Russia replicated without structural adaptation to post-Soviet fragmented infrastructure).

*Secondary test — within-India public sector vs. private sector:*

The within-India comparison controls for TB epidemiology, political commitment, economic development, and drug sensitivity profiles. Early RNTCP was implemented primarily through the public sector, which explicitly redesigned DOTS to integrate with government health worker cadre. The private sector (which treated an estimated 50% of TB patients) was initially excluded from RNTCP adaptation — private practitioners continued informal, often partial treatment without DOTS-adapted protocols.

Strong selector prediction for this comparison: if private sector practitioners had adequate lab infrastructure and drug access, they should produce comparable treatment completion rates to public sector.

Adaptation prediction: public sector (explicit redesign) should substantially outperform private sector (no initial adaptation); private sector improvement should follow when PPM-DOTS (Public-Private Mix DOTS) adapts the protocol for private practitioner context.

**Pre-registered wound conditions:**

*For adaptation:* If Russia achieved treatment success rates comparable to or higher than India's RNTCP despite lower redesign effort, adaptation takes a direct hit. The ordering India > Russia with redesign as mediating variable is the specific prediction; its failure wounds adaptation.

*For strong selector:* If India substantially outperformed Russia AND the within-India private sector comparison shows public sector advantage that tracks redesign effort rather than infrastructure availability, strong selector is wounded. The mechanism would be: redesign quality → outcome, not prerequisite alignment → outcome.

**Pre-registered confounder:**

Russia faced substantially higher rates of drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) than India during the same period, partly because Soviet-era treatment practices used inconsistent or inadequate drug regimens prior to DOTS introduction, generating resistance. This is a potential confounder: Russia's poor DOTS outcomes may reflect harder TB to treat (drug-resistant strains), not worse implementation. If MDR-TB prevalence explains the India/Russia ordering independently of redesign quality, neither selector nor adaptation can cleanly claim credit for the ordering. The within-India comparison (same drug sensitivity profile, different redesign effort) provides a partial corrective.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — DOTS transfers through fidelity to the technical protocol; health system architecture is a secondary factor

The core DOTS innovation is behavioral and pharmacological, not infrastructural: short-course multidrug chemotherapy with supervised completion dramatically reduces treatment duration (from 18–24 months to 6 months) and dramatically increases cure rates. The supervision requirement is achievable by almost any actor — family member, village volunteer, shopkeeper — without formal health worker training. Where DOTS failed to transfer, the primary cause was political commitment failure (insufficient drug procurement, inadequate monitoring systems, inadequate case-finding), not design mismatch. The technical requirements — microscopy, drug supply, any supervision mechanism — are constructible given adequate funding. Under H1, DOTS failures are resource and governance failures, not adaptation failures. The India/Russia difference is explained by post-Soviet governance collapse affecting drug supply and monitoring continuity, not by design redesign.

### H2 — DOTS transfer success depends on pre-existing prerequisite alignment with the source design (strong selector)

The DOTS package was developed for a specific health system context (Tanzania's district health system with relatively coherent community structures) and assumes specific prerequisites: laboratory infrastructure capable of sputum smear microscopy, a clinic or health post where patients can present for supervised treatment, a drug supply system that can maintain uninterrupted supply for six-month treatment courses, and health worker capacity to register patients, supervise dosing, and record outcomes. Countries with health system architectures that match these prerequisites more closely transfer DOTS more successfully. Russia's health infrastructure (labs, trained staff) matched several DOTS prerequisites, but the sanatoria model created a structural mismatch with DOTS's outpatient delivery design that pre-existing infrastructure could not resolve. Under H2, the India/Russia difference is better explained by which dimensions of prerequisite alignment matter most: India's community infrastructure aligned with supervision; Russia's sanatoria infrastructure mismatched with outpatient delivery.

### H3 — DOTS transfer success depends on whether implementing organizations redesigned the delivery mechanism to fit available local prerequisites (adaptation)

The DOTS protocol specifies five strategic components but does not specify the delivery architecture. The original Tanzania model used clinic-based supervision; this was not the only possible design. Successful DOTS transfers redesigned delivery around available social and institutional infrastructure: India used community health workers and village volunteers; China used the CDC network; Bangladesh used NGO networks. Transfers that attempted to replicate clinic-based supervision without adaptation to available infrastructure — including Russia's attempt to work through sanatoria, which were designed for inpatient treatment and were collapsing post-Soviet — failed or achieved substantially lower success rates. Under H3, the India/Russia ordering is explained by redesign quality: India's RNTCP explicitly designed the delivery system around available village-level infrastructure, while Russia's DOTS implementation attempted to use existing sanatoria infrastructure without structural redesign. The within-India private-sector gap provides additional support: DOTS succeeded in the adapted public sector delivery, but failed in the private sector before PPM-DOTS redesigned the protocol for private practitioner context.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Global DOTS coverage: by 2005, DOTS had been implemented in 183 countries; global treatment success rate reached approximately 82% for new smear-positive cases (WHO Global TB Report 2006), approaching the 85% target
- India RNTCP scale: from 1997 to 2006, India's RNTCP enrolled approximately 8.5 million patients; treatment success rates for new smear-positive patients reached 85–87% in notification cohorts; this is the world's largest DOTS implementation by patient count and demonstrates the strategy is deployable at extraordinary scale
- China DOTS success: China piloted DOTS 1991–1994 with World Bank support; national scale-up achieved treatment success rates above 90% in project areas; TB prevalence fell by approximately 40% in project areas over 10 years (World Bank evaluation, 2002); China achieved this through a CDC-adapted delivery model explicitly redesigned for its health system
- Tanzania source evidence: treatment success rates under Styblo's DOTS protocols reached 80–85% in pilot programs; this was substantially better than pre-DOTS outcomes in the same settings
- Bangladesh: DOTS adapted through NGO delivery networks (particularly BRAC, repeating the Bangladesh + BRAC Bank pattern from RC-029 in a different domain); Bangladesh National TB Programme achieved treatment success rates above 90% in BRAC-supported areas; suggests the adaptation mechanism operates independently in Bangladesh across health domains

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Russia MDR-TB crisis: Russia's TB burden increased substantially in the 1990s–2000s; MDR-TB prevalence reached approximately 20% of new TB cases and 45–50% of retreatment cases by mid-2000s (WHO estimates); treatment success rates for drug-sensitive TB in Russia were substantially below the 85% target; the sanatoria-based DOTS implementation did not achieve treatment success comparable to India despite higher initial health infrastructure
- South Africa TB/HIV co-epidemic: South Africa implemented DOTS, but TB outcomes remained substantially below 85% targets through the 2000s, complicated by high HIV co-infection rates (HIV substantially weakens immune response to TB); DOTS without HIV co-management could not achieve the projected outcomes in high-HIV contexts — a prerequisite gap (HIV management infrastructure) the original DOTS design did not incorporate
- Eastern European countries: multiple former Soviet bloc countries introduced DOTS in the 1990s with similar sanatoria-replication patterns; outcomes across the region were substantially below targets; MDR-TB became endemic in several countries; the regional pattern is consistent with low-redesign, high-initial-infrastructure deployments underperforming relative to low-initial-infrastructure, high-redesign deployments (India, China, Bangladesh)
- Within-India private sector gap: approximately 50% of TB patients in India sought care from private practitioners; early RNTCP excluded private sector; private practitioners frequently prescribed inadequate regimens, partial courses, or incorrect drug combinations; treatment success rates for privately treated TB patients were substantially lower than RNTCP-treated patients; this created a paradox — India had high aggregate TB burden despite strong RNTCP performance because RNTCP reached only a fraction of cases
- DOTS-Plus / MDR-TB inadequacy: the DOTS framework as originally formulated did not address MDR-TB; treatment requires second-line drugs, longer courses, and substantially more intensive monitoring; the claim that DOTS solves TB globally was incomplete because the original framework created a gap for drug-resistant TB that undermined the treatment success calculation in high-MDR contexts

## Missing Evidence

- Systematic redesign documentation: there is no systematic database measuring redesign effort (adaptation depth) across DOTS implementations, making it difficult to directly correlate redesign intensity with treatment success rates across countries; the India/Russia comparison relies on qualitative evidence about implementation design choices
- Counterfactual Russia: what would India-style community health worker adaptation of DOTS have produced in Russia in the 1990s, given post-Soviet social infrastructure? The absence of a redesigned Russia DOTS program limits the comparison — we observe Russia's low-redesign outcome but not what high-redesign would have produced
- Drug sensitivity separation: studies that separate treatment success rates for drug-sensitive TB vs. MDR-TB in Russia vs. India would help isolate the design quality effect from the epidemiological confounder; such clean separation is available for some cohorts but not systematically across comparable time periods
- PPM-DOTS natural experiment: India's Private-Public Mix DOTS expansion offers a partial natural experiment — states and districts that adopted PPM-DOTS earlier vs. later should show treatment success divergence that tracks adaptation timing, not underlying TB epidemiology; this comparison exists in program data but is not systematically analyzed in peer-reviewed literature in a form usable for causal attribution

## Confidence

**High (on DOTS efficacy at source; that supervised short-course chemotherapy dramatically improves treatment outcomes vs. unsupervised) / Moderate (on the generalizability claim; 85% target reachable across diverse health systems with political commitment alone) / Low (on the implicit claim that pre-existing infrastructure quality predicts transfer success better than redesign quality)**

The clinical evidence base for DOTS is strong and not meaningfully contested. The transfer claim is where the evidence is mixed. India demonstrates that 85%+ success rates are achievable even with lower pre-existing infrastructure through explicit redesign. Russia demonstrates that higher pre-existing infrastructure does not guarantee success when redesign is absent. The MDR-TB confounder prevents a clean causal attribution to redesign quality vs. epidemiological difficulty. The within-India private/public comparison provides a cleaner test and is consistent with the adaptation reading.

**Primary discriminating test — India/Russia ordering:**

The pre-registered ordering prediction was: India (lower baseline infrastructure, high redesign) > Russia (higher baseline infrastructure, low redesign). The historical evidence is consistent with this ordering.

India RNTCP achieved treatment success rates of 85–87% for new smear-positive cases at national scale by 2006 — meeting the WHO 85% target — through a delivery model explicitly redesigned around community health workers and patient-wise drug packaging, not clinic-based observation alone.

Russia did not achieve comparable outcomes over the same period. Treatment success rates remained substantially below the 85% target; MDR-TB became endemic; the sanatoria-based delivery model, which attempted to operate DOTS within inpatient-oriented facilities designed for the Soviet-era treatment approach, did not produce the treatment completion rates India achieved.

**What this means for the pre-registered wound conditions:**

*Adaptation wound condition:* Was Russia's outcome comparable to or better than India's despite lower redesign effort? No. The wound condition for adaptation was not triggered.

*Strong selector wound condition:* Did India substantially outperform Russia despite lower pre-existing health infrastructure? Yes, directionally. Was the within-India public/private comparison consistent with redesign quality as the mediating variable? Yes. The within-India pattern is: public sector DOTS (explicit redesign) achieved substantially higher treatment completion than private sector (no initial adaptation); private sector improvement tracked PPM-DOTS adoption. This pattern controls for TB epidemiology and is consistent with adaptation as the mediating mechanism.

**The MDR-TB confounder — partial resolution:**

Russia's higher MDR-TB prevalence is a confounder that prevents clean attribution. However: the within-India private/public comparison is not confounded by MDR-TB (same drug sensitivity profile, different redesign effort). That comparison is consistent with adaptation. The Eastern European regional pattern — multiple countries with comparable Soviet-era health infrastructure, comparable low redesign effort, comparable underperformance — is also consistent: the underperformance is regional and tracks the sanatoria-replication approach, not country-specific epidemiological factors. These are partial resolutions, not definitive. The confounder is noted but does not fully account for the observed ordering.

**Selector/adaptation discriminating result:**

The strong selector prediction (Russia performs at least comparably to India) is not supported by the evidence. The adaptation prediction (India outperforms Russia; public sector outperforms private sector within India) is consistent with the evidence. The ordering, while confounded at the India/Russia level, is consistent at the within-India level where the confounder does not apply.

**RC-030 result for the selector/adaptation discrimination:**

This is not a decisive wound to strong selector. The MDR-TB confounder leaves the India/Russia comparison ambiguous at the causal level. However, the within-India private/public comparison provides additional evidence that is not confounded and is consistent with adaptation as the mediating variable.

## Open Questions

1. The MDR-TB confounder prevents clean attribution of the India/Russia ordering to redesign quality vs. epidemiological difficulty. What type of evidence would resolve this? Systematic analysis of drug-sensitive TB outcomes (separating MDR) in India vs. Russia over the same period would help, but such analysis faces data comparability issues. Is the confounder resolvable with available data, or does it make the India/Russia comparison permanently ambiguous?

2. The within-India private/public comparison is consistent with adaptation, but it is not a natural experiment — public and private sector patients differ in ways that might confound the comparison (severity, socioeconomic status, access to care). Is the within-India comparison sufficiently controlled to count as a second clean instance of adaptation, or is it an additional confounded observation?

3. Weak selector (where adaptation is nested as a mechanism for achieving prerequisite alignment) survives RC-030 untouched. Under weak selector, India's community health worker redesign is just one path to achieving the "supervision" prerequisite — Russia failed not because it didn't redesign but because it used the wrong prerequisites. This reading is logically consistent. The question is whether weak selector and adaptation make any predictions that actually differ. If they don't, the distinction is semantic rather than empirical.

4. RC-030 introduces a new variant in the cross-context inference structure: a case where the source evidence is strong (DOTS works) but the transfer claim is about a *specific delivery design* (clinic-based, supervised), and the successful transfers all modified that design. This suggests the claim's apparent success (82% global treatment success by 2005) may mask the fact that it was achieved through a family of adapted designs rather than the original design. Does this pattern — apparent global success through design variation rather than design replication — recur in other structures in the repository?

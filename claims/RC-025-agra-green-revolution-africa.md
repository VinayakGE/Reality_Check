# Reality Check #025

## Claim

> "Africa can have a green revolution. We can do for Africa what the green revolution did for Asia — transform African agriculture, double the productivity of smallholder farmers, and lift hundreds of millions out of hunger and poverty."

## Source

- Speakers: Kofi Annan (founding chair, AGRA; former UN Secretary-General) and Bill Gates (co-chair, Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation), at the launch of the Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa (AGRA), 2006; the claim is the organizing premise of AGRA's founding mandate and its 2007–2021 program strategy
- Organization: Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa (AGRA) — founded 2006 with initial $150M Rockefeller Foundation grant + Gates Foundation funding; total investment through 2021 approximately $900M across 13 African countries; supported by USAID, African Development Bank, and bilateral donors
- Date: Founding statement, 2006; reaffirmed in AGRA strategic plans (2007–2012, 2017–2021); explicitly benchmarked against the Asian Green Revolution of the 1960s–70s
- Link: AGRA founding statement: https://agra.org; AGRA 2021 program report: https://agra.org/program-report-2021/

*Note: The claim as stated above condenses several distinct AGRA commitments: (a) doubling staple crop productivity for 30 million smallholder farm households by 2020; (b) halving food insecurity in the 13 focus countries; (c) demonstrating that the institutional model (improved seeds, fertilizer access, market development, policy advocacy) transfers from Asia to Africa. The audit evaluates the general claim and notes where specific targets sharpen the assessment.*

*Structural context: AGRA represents an explicit attempt to transfer the Asian Green Revolution (a resolved, Supported historical claim) to a new context. The Asian Green Revolution had: Norman Borlaug's dwarf wheat varieties, state procurement systems (India's Minimum Support Price), subsidized fertilizer distribution, irrigation infrastructure in Punjab and Haryana, and strong state extension services. AGRA targeted an analogous bundle for Africa without equivalent state capacity, without large-scale irrigation, and across 13 countries with distinct agricultural policies. This is the pilot-to-policy inference structure with a twist: the pilot was not a small controlled trial but a full-scale implementation in a different geography.*

---

## Why This Matters

Agriculture employs approximately 60% of sub-Saharan Africa's workforce and accounts for a disproportionate share of rural poverty. The Asian Green Revolution, credited with preventing hundreds of millions of deaths from famine and lifting rural populations out of poverty across South and Southeast Asia, stands as one of the most consequential technology transfer events of the twentieth century. If the Green Revolution model transfers to Africa with comparable effect, AGRA and its successors represent a viable path to food security for approximately 600 million smallholder farmers across the continent.

If the model does not transfer — if the prerequisites for the Asian Green Revolution's success are not buildable in Africa's agricultural context despite substantial funding and concerted effort — the implications extend beyond agriculture. AGRA is also the repository's most direct test of the Prerequisite Availability hypothesis after RC-024 introduced "Deliberately Built" as a distinct path to transfer success. PEPFAR built its prerequisites successfully with exceptional resources. AGRA attempted to build comparable prerequisites (also with substantial resources) for a different domain. The comparison is the point.

**The pre-registered discriminating question:**

The Prerequisite Availability hypothesis (Checkpoint #005) predicts: *prerequisites available at transfer → transfer succeeds.* PEPFAR succeeded because resources built prerequisites. The test for AGRA:

*Did resources create availability?*

AGRA had substantial, sustained funding ($900M+ over 15 years) directed specifically at building the prerequisites for agricultural productivity gains (improved seeds, fertilizer, markets). If availability emerged and transfer succeeded, Model A (resource constraint) is confirmed and the hypothesis is strengthened. If availability only partially emerged despite sustained funding, the hypothesis requires revision: something beyond resources mediates the construction of availability.

**Secondary hypothesis (coordination density) pre-registered for observation:**

PEPFAR succeeded not merely because money was present but because money, institutional alignment, political commitment, operational capacity, and unified program governance arrived together. The pre-registered question is whether AGRA's agricultural prerequisites required the same coordination density and whether they received it.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The Green Revolution model generalizes and AGRA's approach was sound; resource and time constraints limited results
The causal mechanism of the Green Revolution — genetically improved seed varieties matched to local soil conditions, combined with soil nutrient replenishment through fertilizer, produce yield increases that are scalable — operates through agronomy, not sociology. It does not require cultural fit, political legitimacy, or deep institutional norms. What it requires are: (a) seed varieties adapted to local conditions, (b) farmer access to seeds and fertilizer, and (c) markets or policies that make adoption economically rational. AGRA worked on all three. Results were measurable: across AGRA's focus countries, the adoption of improved seed varieties increased substantially in some countries (Rwanda, Ghana, Zambia); fertilizer use increased; specific crop yields grew in targeted interventions. If the assessment period were longer, or resources larger, the productivity doubling target may still be achievable. On this reading, AGRA is not a failed transfer — it is a transfer in progress that was assessed on an ambitious timeline.

### H2 — The Asian Green Revolution model's prerequisites are not buildable in Africa's agroecological and political context
The Asian Green Revolution succeeded in a specific niche: irrigated lowland agriculture in Punjab and Haryana (India), the Indus basin (Pakistan), and river delta systems in Southeast Asia. Dwarf wheat and rice varieties were developed for that niche. Africa's smallholder agriculture is mostly rain-fed, highly diverse in soil type and elevation, and spread across many small markets rather than concentrated in a few high-productivity zones. The institutional context also differs: India's Minimum Support Price system guaranteed farmers a procurement price for wheat and rice, making adoption of high-yield varieties economically rational even for subsistence farmers. Most African countries lack equivalent state procurement systems. The prerequisites for the Asian Green Revolution's success (irrigation, guaranteed markets, homogeneous agro-ecology) are not present in Africa and are not buildable through the AGRA model, which addressed seeds and fertilizer but not the structural conditions that made seeds and fertilizer adoption rational.

### H3 — AGRA's prerequisites required coordination density that was not achieved
PEPFAR succeeded because an unprecedented concentration of resources, institutions, political alignment, and operational capacity arrived together under unified program governance. The HIV/ART prerequisites — drug supply chains, lab capacity, trained providers, adherence support — were built in a coordinated sequence under PEPFAR's direct program management. AGRA's agricultural prerequisites — seed systems, fertilizer markets, storage infrastructure, credit access, extension services, government procurement policy — required coordination across 13 independent nation-states, private sector seed companies, fertilizer distributors, and commodity markets, without a unified program authority to sequence prerequisite construction. Resources were present but coordination density was insufficient. The mechanism that made PEPFAR's prerequisite construction succeed was not money alone — it was the combination of money and institutional alignment under a single operational mandate.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Seed adoption: improved seed adoption increased in AGRA's focus countries over the program period; Kenya, Ghana, and Rwanda showed measurable adoption growth; in Rwanda, adoption of improved maize and bean varieties grew from near-zero to substantial penetration among targeted smallholders
- Yield impacts in specific interventions: AGRA-funded interventions in targeted plots and demonstration farms consistently showed yield improvements consistent with what the agronomy predicts; the mechanism (improved varieties + fertilizer → yield increase) operated when inputs were available
- Scale of the challenge acknowledged: AGRA's own 2021 program report acknowledges that the transformation of 30 million farm households was not achieved by 2020 but argues the program laid institutional groundwork (national seed systems, fertilizer regulatory frameworks, agricultural policy platforms) that positions future programs better
- Positive country-level cases: Rwanda achieved substantial agricultural productivity gains over the 2010s through a combined program of land consolidation, crop intensification, and government-mandated input distribution; elements of the AGRA model contributed to this outcome, though the specific contribution is contested

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Stated targets not met: AGRA's founding commitment was to double productivity for 30 million smallholder farm households by 2020; independent assessment (GRAIN / Rosa Luxemburg Foundation, 2020) found that food insecurity increased by 30% in AGRA's 13 focus countries over the program's 12 years; AGRA disputed the methodology but did not substantively contest that the doubling target was not achieved
- Yield growth below target: median staple crop yield improvements across AGRA focus countries were approximately 14–27% over 12 years, against a target of 100%; yield growth was real but far below the Green Revolution comparison implied
- Fertilizer access gap: fertilizer prices in SSA remain 3–5x higher than world market prices due to poor infrastructure, small market sizes, and import logistics; AGRA's fertilizer access programs did not resolve this structural constraint; the constraint is not primarily a resource problem — it is a market structure and infrastructure problem that money alone cannot fix within a 15-year program window
- Input treadmill risk: critics (GRAIN, ActionAid) documented cases where AGRA-funded promotion of hybrid seeds and chemical fertilizers created economic dependency for smallholders (hybrid seeds require annual repurchase; fertilizer application becomes necessary to maintain baseline productivity once soil ecology shifts); in contexts without reliable seed and fertilizer access, this dependency became a debt risk rather than a productivity gain
- Coordination gap: unlike PEPFAR, which had a single US government agency (PEPFAR) directing resources to national health ministries, AGRA operated as a grant-making institution supporting national governments that maintained independent agricultural policies; seed regulation, fertilizer subsidies, and market development occurred on different timelines across 13 countries; the prerequisites were not built in a coordinated sequence

---

## Missing Evidence

- Counterfactual yield trajectory: what would productivity growth have been in AGRA's target countries without the program? This is the Type A counterfactual — the intervention was deployed without a control arm; retrospective matching studies exist but confounding from concurrent programs (USAID, World Food Programme, national programs) is difficult to control
- Coordination density measurement: did AGRA focus countries where government agricultural policy, private sector seed investment, and AGRA programming aligned see materially better outcomes? The cross-country variation exists but has not been analyzed explicitly against a coordination density hypothesis
- Long-run prerequisite construction: AGRA's 2021 claim that it built "institutional groundwork" for future productivity gains — national seed systems, regulatory frameworks, training capacity — is not yet testable; whether these prerequisites enable a next-phase scale-up is a question only resolvable 5–10 years hence

## Confidence

**Low-Medium (on the Asian Green Revolution model transferring to SSA as described) / Medium (on agricultural productivity improvements being achievable in Africa through different means)**

Reason: The stated targets were not met. The evidence for the Asian Green Revolution model transferring at equivalent scale is weak. The evidence that agricultural productivity gains are achievable in SSA is stronger — Rwanda demonstrates this — but Rwanda's success required state mechanisms (land consolidation, government-mandated input distribution, single-crop intensification policy) that AGRA's model neither provided nor replicated. The broader claim — that Africa can have a green revolution — may be true through different means, but the AGRA model as implemented has not demonstrated it.

**Transferability assessment:**

**Transferability is present in RC-025.**

The mechanism did not transfer cleanly. Prerequisites for the Green Revolution model — guaranteed procurement markets, irrigation, homogeneous agro-ecology, coordinated state support — partially transferred (seed varieties adapted, some fertilizer access improved) but the overall prerequisite bundle did not materialize.

**Transferability count: 7 (new instance).**

**Transferability mechanism:** Mixed. The AGRA case combines elements of Absent (state procurement systems, irrigation, homogeneous agro-ecology — prerequisites that do not exist in SSA and were not within AGRA's scope to build) and Suppressed (seed systems and fertilizer access — prerequisites partially built but operating below the threshold required for cascade adoption). This is a Mixed classification, the second after RC-022.

**Prerequisite Availability hypothesis — direct test results:**

The pre-registered discriminating question: *Did resources create availability?*

The answer is: partially. Resources created partial availability. Seed systems improved; fertilizer access expanded; national agricultural policy platforms were strengthened. But the full prerequisite bundle — including the state procurement architecture and irrigation infrastructure that made Asian Green Revolution adoption rational for individual smallholders — did not materialize.

This result pressures the Prerequisite Availability hypothesis without defeating it:

- The hypothesis is not falsified: availability remained incomplete, and transfer was partial. The correlation (incomplete availability → incomplete transfer) is consistent with the hypothesis.
- The hypothesis is refined: "resources create availability" is too simple. Resources created some availability but not all required availability. Something other than resources was missing.

**Coordination density as the distinguishing variable:**

The pre-registered secondary hypothesis has evidence. PEPFAR's unique feature was not the funding alone — it was that funding, institutional alignment, political commitment, and operational capacity arrived together under unified program governance. AGRA had comparable per-beneficiary funding but operated across 13 independent governments with independent agricultural policies and no PEPFAR-equivalent unified operational authority.

The cross-country variation within AGRA is consistent with this: Rwanda, where the government provided the equivalent of unified coordination (mandatory land consolidation, single-crop intensification, government-managed input distribution), showed substantially better outcomes than the AGRA average. Rwanda effectively supplied the coordination density that AGRA as a program could not provide across 13 countries.

This does not yet establish coordination density as a candidate explanation. It is consistent with it. One case (Rwanda within AGRA) and one prior comparison (PEPFAR vs. AGRA) do not constitute independent evidence.

**Model A, B, C — discrimination update:**

- **Model A (resource constraint):** Weakened but not falsified. Resources were present and generated partial availability. The failure to achieve full availability despite substantial resources suggests resources alone are not sufficient. Model A survives only in the form: "resources are necessary but not sufficient."

- **Model B (time constraint):** Not yet discriminated. AGRA ran for 15 years; whether a longer program with the same model would eventually build the prerequisite bundle is unknown. AGRA's own post-2020 continuation arguments invoke this model.

- **Model C (structural non-buildability):** Partially consistent. State procurement systems, equivalent to India's MSP, appear to be genuinely difficult to build through external program investment — they require domestic political economy conditions (ruling coalition commitment to farmer price support) that external resources cannot purchase. Agroecological diversity (the absence of a "Punjab equivalent" in SSA) may also represent a structural constraint on yield homogenization. Neither has been proven non-buildable; both have resisted construction so far.

**The emerging candidate explanation — coordination density:**

If coordination density is the mediating variable, the prediction would be:

- High coordination (PEPFAR: unified government program, single mandate, direct procurement and delivery) → prerequisites built → transfer succeeds
- Low coordination (AGRA: grant-making to 13 governments, diverse agricultural policies, private sector intermediation) → prerequisites partially built → transfer partial

This is a more specific version of Model A ("resources + coordination → availability") rather than a replacement for it. It would also explain why Rwanda within AGRA outperformed the program average — Rwanda was effectively a high-coordination context.

Not yet promoted. Two instances are insufficient. RC-026 (Sri Lanka maternal mortality) will add a case where domestic political alignment without exceptional external resources produced full prerequisite construction.

## Open Questions

1. The Mixed classification now appears twice (RC-022, RC-025). Both involve partial prerequisite construction — some prerequisites built, others not. Is there a threshold effect: below some availability level, transfer fails entirely; above it, partial success is possible? Or does availability operate linearly, with transfer outcomes proportional to availability level?

2. Rwanda's within-AGRA success may be the most informative data point in this audit. If Rwanda's national coordination (mandatory land policies, government input distribution) supplied what AGRA's program structure could not, that would mean the effective intervention was not AGRA but Rwanda's government using AGRA's resources. Is "program structure" — who controls the coordination — separable from "resource level" as an explanatory variable?

3. The Prerequisite Availability hypothesis was stated as binary (available/not available) at Checkpoint #005. RC-025 reveals that availability operates on a gradient — partial availability, partial transfer. Should the hypothesis be restated in terms of a threshold or a continuous variable rather than a binary state?

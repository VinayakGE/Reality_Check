# RC-035: East Asian Industrial Policy

**Claim:** "South Korea and Taiwan demonstrate that active state industrial policy — not market-friendly policy — was the primary driver of the East Asian economic miracle."

**Sources:** Alice Amsden, *Asia's Next Giant: South Korea and Late Industrialization* (1989); Robert Wade, *Governing the Market: Economic Theory and the Role of Government in East Asian Industrialization* (1990)

**Key formulations:**
- Amsden: "Korea made getting prices wrong the cornerstone of its industrial policy... government deliberately distorted relative prices to stimulate investment in targeted sectors."
- Wade: "The governments of East Asian countries guided the market, and this guiding role was crucial to their development performance. The market was governed, not free."

**Status:** Unresolved
**Date:** 2026-06-24

---

## Why This Claim Matters

The East Asian economic transformation — South Korea from $82 GDP per capita in 1961 to high-income status by the 1990s; Taiwan's parallel trajectory — constitutes the most dramatic economic development episode of the 20th century. The question of what caused it is directly consequential for development policy globally: if Amsden and Wade are right, late-developing countries have a replicable toolkit. If their critics are right, the toolkit is an illusion that accompanied growth without causing it.

This claim sits at the center of one of the most consequential debates in development economics. It is also the domain for the repository's first cross-audit ontology prediction, recorded before claim selection.

## Watch Items (Pre-Registered Before Evidence Review)

**Watch B:** Does the core dispute concern whether industrial policy had *any* independent causal role, or does it concern how much it contributed among accepted co-factors? The distinction between Watch B and Attribution Precision Gap is the primary live test.

**Adapted-not-replicated pattern:** Does the evidence suggest that what worked in South Korea and Taiwan required conditions that cannot be transferred (embedded bureaucratic autonomy, performance requirements, Cold War geopolitical context)?

**Cross-context inference structure:** Amsden/Wade make an implicit cross-context inference — that the East Asian cases demonstrate a generalizable model. If the critique is that this inference fails, CE-1 and the cross-context inference pattern apply.

**Wound conditions pre-registered:**
- H1 wound: evidence that specific industrial interventions moved sectors ahead of comparative advantage and those sectors became globally competitive, demonstrably beyond what market-compatible incentives would have produced
- H2 wound: a country with equivalent geopolitical positioning and market-compatible policies that failed to achieve comparable industrial transformation
- H3 wound: evidence that industrial policy in South Korea/Taiwan during early lower-intervention periods also produced positive outcomes, suggesting the institutional apparatus wasn't necessary

## Alternative Explanations

**H1 — Market-Compatible Explanation (World Bank position)**

Growth was driven by sound fundamentals: export orientation, macroeconomic stability, high household savings, heavy investment in education, and suppression of rent-seeking. Industrial policy was largely neutral: it sometimes subsidized what markets would have funded anyway, sometimes created inefficient misallocation that growth occurred despite. The 1993 World Bank "East Asian Miracle" report represents this position, crediting "getting the basics right" rather than "governing the market."

*Key prediction:* The growth sectors that received the most intense industrial policy support were not systematically more successful than those that grew through market processes. POSCO and Samsung succeeded because of entrepreneurial quality and export discipline, not because of directed credit per se.

**H2 — Geopolitical-Structural Explanation**

The East Asian miracle required preconditions that industrial policy did not create and cannot replicate: US security relationships providing preferential market access and military aid, land reform in both countries eliminating powerful agrarian elites who would have blocked industrial investment, Confucian institutional frameworks supporting education and hierarchical discipline, and a specific Cold War moment when the US had strategic interest in East Asian development success. Industrial policy operated within this enabling structure; it did not create it. Brazil, India, and Mexico tried comparable industrial policy without comparable structural preconditions and produced different outcomes.

*Key prediction:* Countries with comparable industrial policy instruments but different geopolitical positioning (India under ISI, Brazil under Vargas/subsequent developmentalism) failed to produce East Asian outcomes. This is an H1-compatible alternative to the specific policy-tools hypothesis.

**H3 — Institutional-Selectivity Explanation**

Industrial policy worked in South Korea and Taiwan because it operated through specific institutional arrangements that cannot be separated from the policy itself: embedded bureaucratic autonomy (technocrats insulated from rent-seeking while remaining connected to productive enterprises), performance requirements tying subsidies to export outcomes, disciplined credit allocation through state-controlled banking, and pilot agency coordination (EPB in South Korea, CEPD/MOEA in Taiwan). The same policy instruments deployed without these institutional configurations failed (India's license raj, Latin American ISI produced rent-seeking rather than performance). If the institutional conditions are necessary for the policy to work, "industrial policy" is not the right object — "institutionally-disciplined state coordination of investment" is.

*This explanation is partially compatible with Amsden/Wade's descriptive account while cutting against the strong transferability implication of the claim.*

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

**POSCO as a hard case:** The World Bank refused to fund South Korea's state steel company in the late 1960s, judging it economically unviable. South Korea built POSCO anyway through state financing. By the 1980s, POSCO was among the world's most cost-efficient steel producers. If markets had been allowed to allocate, this capacity would not have been built when it was — and possibly not at all given South Korea's starting resource base. This is the strongest available evidence that industrial policy caused something that market-compatible allocation would not have produced.

**Directed credit magnitudes:** Interest rates for priority industrial sectors in South Korea in the 1970s were 3–8% while the unsubsidized rate was 30%+. This differential is too large to treat as neutral. It substantially changed which investments were viable and who controlled capital allocation. The investments that were funded under this regime (heavy industry, shipbuilding, electronics) were the sectors that generated South Korea's subsequent export earnings.

**Heavy and Chemical Industry (HCI) drive:** The 1973 HCI push targeted steel, petrochemicals, shipbuilding, electronics, and machinery. Critics at the time predicted resource misallocation. By the 1980s, all five sectors had become competitive in export markets. The targeting preceded the competitiveness; this ordering is consistent with the industrial policy having caused the development rather than merely accompanying it.

**Taiwan's ITRI and TSMC origin:** Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, now the world's dominant semiconductor foundry, traces its origins to state-facilitated technology transfer through the Industrial Technology Research Institute, which licensed CMOS technology from RCA in 1976 and developed it into the basis for Taiwan's semiconductor industry. The state did not merely subsidize; it conducted technology transfer that private markets had no incentive to perform at that scale.

**Performance requirements as discipline:** Unlike Latin American ISI, South Korean industrial policy tied subsidies to export performance. Chaebols that failed to meet export targets lost credit access. This conditional structure created accountability that pure rent-seeking models cannot produce. The export discipline built into the interventions is evidence that the policy was designed with market feedback, not against it.

**Wade's cross-country evidence:** Taiwan and South Korea both achieved rapid industrialization with high-intervention states. Hong Kong achieved rapid growth with minimal industrial policy, but developed a very different industrial structure (trade, finance, light manufacturing) without heavy industry competitiveness. Singapore occupied an intermediate position. The cross-case variation is consistent with industrial policy influencing industrial structure even if it is not decisive for aggregate growth rates.

## Evidence Against / Creating Doubt

**The Hong Kong problem:** Hong Kong achieved comparable GDP per capita growth to South Korea and Taiwan through the 1970s–1990s with minimal industrial policy. If industrial policy was the primary driver in South Korea and Taiwan, a similar trajectory under market-compatible policies should not have been possible. The Hong Kong case is the strongest evidence that the growth miracle did not require Amsden/Wade-style intervention.

*Watch B activation point:* This is not a contribution-share argument. It is a causal-presence argument: if comparable growth occurred without the policy, the policy may not have been causally necessary.

**The India counter-case:** India deployed extensive industrial policy through the license raj from the 1950s onward — directed credit, protected markets, state enterprise, production targeting. The result was growth at roughly one-third the rate of East Asian economies with similar instruments. The divergence suggests either that industrial policy was not the decisive factor, or that institutional conditions necessary for the policy to work were present in East Asia and absent in India. If the latter, the claim is about institutional conditions, not industrial policy per se.

**Selection on observables:** The countries Amsden and Wade study (South Korea, Taiwan) are selected from a class of East Asian late developers who shared: pre-existing land reform, high educational attainment before industrialization, strong bureaucratic traditions, Cold War US relationships, and Confucian institutional frameworks. These preconditions predict development success under multiple theories. Attributing the success to industrial policy when many potential confounders predict the same outcome is the core Watch B concern.

**The World Bank's 1993 methodological challenge:** The "East Asian Miracle" report attempted to decompose growth into factor accumulation (savings → investment), human capital, and total factor productivity. On this decomposition, much of the growth can be attributed to inputs (capital, labor, human capital) that industrial policy may have mobilized but did not create. The question becomes: would equivalent savings and human capital investment have produced comparable growth under market-compatible allocation? This is a genuine counterfactual question, not merely a contribution-share dispute.

**Sector-level mixed evidence:** The HCI drive produced some clear successes (shipbuilding, steel, electronics) and some clear failures (nuclear power equipment, aircraft). The failures are rarely included in the Amsden/Wade account. If industrial policy were decisively superior to market allocation, the failure sectors would require explanation. The mixed record is consistent with industrial policy accelerating some sectors while misallocating resources in others — a net calculation that is not available.

**Decomposition difficulty (APG signal):** Even granting that industrial policy contributed, the concurrent factors were substantial: US market access under Cold War foreign policy, Japanese technology transfer, high household savings (driven by demographic structure and financial repression), land reform preconditions, and initial export-oriented low-wage manufacturing. Some economists who accept that industrial policy contributed argue its share was modest relative to these factors. This is an APG-type dispute within an overarching Watch B frame.

## Missing Evidence

**The counterfactual development path:** What would South Korea's industrial structure look like today if it had followed Hong Kong's market-compatible path? This counterfactual is structurally imaginable (Hong Kong provides a partial comparison) but not directly observable. The dispute between Amsden/Wade and their critics is largely a dispute about this counterfactual.

**Sector-level causal attribution:** A clean test would require identifying sectors that received heavy industrial policy support and sectors with comparable starting conditions that did not, then comparing trajectories. Some within-Korea evidence exists (subsidized vs. unsubsidized credit recipients) but is not clean enough to resolve the causal claim.

**Policy counterfactuals within East Asia:** Singapore's intermediate industrial policy and somewhat different trajectory provides partial evidence, but the city-state context limits comparability. South Korea's own early period (pre-HCI) versus post-HCI provides some variation but is confounded by the learning and capital that accumulated during the earlier period.

**Institutional-policy separation:** The central unresolved question is whether the institutional conditions (embedded autonomy, performance requirements) are separable from "industrial policy" as a general tool. If they are inseparable, the claim is not about industrial policy but about a specific institutional configuration. No study has cleanly separated the policy instruments from the institutional apparatus.

## Current Status and Confidence

**Status: Unresolved**

The Amsden/Wade claim cannot be evaluated as stated because its primary bottleneck is a Watch B dispute: the core debate concerns whether active state industrial policy had *any* independent causal role beyond what market-compatible development would have produced, not merely how much it contributed.

The evidence firmly establishes that industrial policy occurred, was large in scale, and preceded the sectors that became successful. It does not establish that the policy caused the success, because the preconditions that predict success under multiple theories were also present, and at least one counter-case (Hong Kong) achieved comparable growth without comparable intervention.

There is a secondary APG dimension: if causal presence is accepted, contribution-share decomposition among industrial policy, US market access, prior land reform, Confucian institutions, and household savings is not available from existing studies. But this is secondary to the Watch B dispute.

**Confidence:** Unresolvable from existing evidence without a clean counterfactual or quasi-experimental design exploiting variation in industrial policy intensity across comparable units.

## Watch Item Assessment

**Watch B:** Confirmed as primary object. The core dispute is about causal presence: did industrial policy have independent causal effect beyond market-compatible development? The Hong Kong counter-case and the India failure case are both deployed as evidence that the policy was not causally necessary, not as evidence about its magnitude relative to other contributors. This is Watch B structure. Watch B is now at 9 instances.

**Attribution Precision Gap:** Present as secondary object. Even within the Watch B frame, there is a sub-dispute about contribution shares conditional on accepting some causal role. This sub-dispute has APG structure (concurrent contributors, accepted contributions, indeterminate shares). The APG dimension is subordinate to Watch B rather than coordinate with it.

**Counterfactual Unavailability:** Absent, as predicted. The counterfactual development path is imagined and contested, not structurally unavailable. Critics invoke Hong Kong; proponents invoke Brazil's ISI failure as evidence of a different counterfactual. The comparison cases are available (if imperfect). The dispute is about which comparison is valid, not about whether a counterfactual can exist.

**Cross-audit prediction result:** Primary object Watch B confirmed. APG present as secondary. Counterfactual Unavailability absent as predicted. Pre-registered prediction holds across all three specified dimensions.

**Adapted-not-replicated pattern:** Present but differently structured than in program transfer cases. The Amsden/Wade claim implies transferability of the industrial policy model; the H3 explanation (institutional preconditions necessary) implies the watch item structure. The evidence is consistent with adapted-not-replicated without providing clean discrimination.

**Cross-context inference structure:** The claim embeds an inference from South Korea/Taiwan cases to a general claim about industrial policy as a development tool. CE-1 applies: the source cases have historical causal uncertainty (Watch B), which is the condition under which cross-context inference bottlenecks on the source case rather than on transfer dynamics.

## Open Questions

**OQ-Regional: RESOLVED**

Resolution mechanism: definitional clarification, not empirical accumulation.

The APG definition contains an embedded activation condition: "the credited cause is accepted as a genuine contributor." When Watch B and APG both address the same causal question, APG's activation is logically downstream of Watch B resolution — not because this is a new structural variant, but because the precondition in the APG definition makes it so. RC-035 made this visible; it did not discover it empirically.

The apparent tension with RC-023 and RC-028 (where Watch B and APG appeared as parallel rather than sequential) is resolved by specifying the unit of analysis: the causal question, not the claim. Different causal questions within the same claim can independently be Watch B or APG (parallel). The same causal question cannot simultaneously be both — Watch B must resolve first (sequential).

Full specification in [status/attribution-precision-gap.md](../status/attribution-precision-gap.md) under "Relationship to Watch B: Sequential vs. Parallel."

**OQ-H3:** If institutional conditions (embedded autonomy, performance requirements) are necessary for industrial policy to work, is "industrial policy" the correct object of the claim, or is the correct object "institutionally-disciplined state-market coordination"? Resolution would require either separating institutional conditions from policy instruments empirically, or reframing the Amsden/Wade claim.

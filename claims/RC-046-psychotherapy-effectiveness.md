## Evidence Review

### General Efficacy: The Existence Question

The meta-analytic evidence for general psychotherapy efficacy is extensive. Cuijpers and colleagues have produced over 40 meta-analyses covering psychological treatments for depression; mean effect sizes relative to waitlist/no-treatment controls range from d = 0.62 to d = 0.87 depending on depression severity, treatment modality, and outcome measure. Wampold and Imel (2015) synthesize a similar body of evidence for anxiety disorders and report effect sizes in the d = 0.70–1.00 range versus inactive controls.

Across diagnostic categories and modalities, the core finding is consistent: psychotherapy outperforms no treatment. This replication is not confined to a single research tradition. CBT researchers (Hofmann, Clark, Barlow), psychodynamic researchers (Shedler, Leichsenring, Driessen), humanistic/common factors researchers (Wampold, Norcross), and integrative researchers (Stricker, Gold) all produce meta-analyses showing positive effect sizes versus inactive controls. The Cochrane Collaboration has reviewed psychotherapy for depression, PTSD, OCD, social anxiety, and generalized anxiety; in each case effect sizes versus waitlist are positive and statistically significant.

**The existence-level Watch B question — "does psychotherapy work at all?" — is not a live research-program dispute.** No credentialed research group is currently arguing that psychotherapy produces zero or negative effects versus inactive comparators. The debate has moved. The existence of general efficacy is not contested.

This is the decisive observation for the mechanism competition. Despite Low Prestige Coordination — no dominant journal, no anchoring citation cluster, actively competing meta-analyst groups with published mutual criticism — existence-level Watch B is absent.

### The Boundary of Consensus: Specific Efficacy vs. Non-Specific Factors

Where the field actively disputes is not existence but *specificity*. The central dispute is:

**Is psychotherapy's effectiveness attributable to specific techniques and components, or to non-specific relational/common factors that any credible psychological treatment would provide?**

This is the common factors debate, operationalized in two competing bodies of work:

**Position 1 — Common Factors (Wampold, Norcross, Lambert):**
The therapeutic alliance accounts for more outcome variance than specific techniques. Therapist effects exceed treatment effects. The "Dodo Bird Verdict" — all bona fide therapies approximately equivalent — is supported across hundreds of head-to-head comparisons. The distinction between "specific" and "non-specific" is illusory; all effective therapies work primarily through relational mechanisms.

*Watch B character:* This position implies that claims about CBT superiority or specific technique efficacy are Watch B-level disputes — the specific components claimed to produce change are not doing the work attributed to them.

**Position 2 — Specific Efficacy (Hofmann, Clark, Barlow, CBT research tradition):**
CBT, exposure therapy, and structured behavioral interventions produce effects via specific mechanisms (cognitive restructuring, extinction learning, behavioral activation) that generic supportive contact does not replicate. The Dodo Bird is an artifact of meta-analytic method — inclusive definitions of "bona fide therapy" prevent detection of genuine superiority. Dismantling studies show component-specific effects for specific elements.

*Watch B character:* This position disputes the common-factors interpretation at the level of specific modality claims, not at the general efficacy level.

**The key structural point:** Both sides agree psychotherapy outperforms no treatment. Their dispute is about *why* — whether specific techniques contribute beyond non-specific relational factors. This is a dispute about mechanism and attribution, not about existence.

---

### Is the Specificity Dispute Theoretical (existence-level) or APG?

This is the critical classification question for the mechanism competition test.

**Evidence for APG classification (consistent with CMech-001):**
The specificity debate does not contest whether psychotherapy produces clinically meaningful outcomes. It contests the *contribution share* of specific vs. non-specific components. This has the structure of an APG dispute — a question about how much, through which pathway — not an existence question. Meta-analyses by both camps show positive effect sizes; they disagree about what drives those effects. The Dodo Bird meta-analyses (Wampold 2001, Benish et al. 2008, Cuijpers et al. 2019 equivalence analyses) do not conclude "psychotherapy doesn't work"; they conclude "modality-specific superiority claims are unsupported."

**Evidence that the specificity dispute has existence-level components (relevant to Alternative A):**
The question "does CBT produce effects via cognitive restructuring specifically?" is structured as a mechanism question but has causal-attribution implications that border on existence-level for modality-superiority claims. If the answer is "no," then claims that CBT is superior to non-directive therapy for specific conditions are undermined. Researchers like Dimidjian et al. (2006) showed behavioral activation alone matched combined CBT for severe depression — this challenges cognitive components' necessity.

But this is existence-level for the *specific-technique superiority sub-claim*, not for the general efficacy claim. The general claim ("psychotherapy treats mental health conditions effectively") survives regardless of whether specific techniques or common factors are responsible. The specificity debate is best classified as **APG + Methodological** (APG for the contribution-share question; Methodological for whether dismantling study design can detect component-specific effects).

### The Active Watch B: Modality-Level Directional Disputes

Watch B in psychotherapy is present and active — but its character is **Directional at the modality level**, not Theoretical at the existence level.

**CBT vs. psychodynamic efficacy for depression:**
Leichsenring et al. (2015, 2022) meta-analyses find no significant efficacy differences between CBT and psychodynamic therapies for depression. Cuijpers et al. counter-analyses find CBT has a small advantage for specific conditions. The dispute is active, published, and has not converged despite decades of head-to-head trials. NICE preferentially recommends CBT; some Cochrane reviews find insufficient evidence for superiority. *This is Watch B Directional: which modality is superior?*

**EMDR, trauma-focused CBT, and PTSD specificity:**
NICE (2018) and VA/DoD guidelines recommend trauma-focused CBT and EMDR as first-line for PTSD; some meta-analyses (van Etten & Taylor 1998 era) find SSRIs comparable; others (Watts et al. 2013) find trauma-focused treatments superior. The relative ranking dispute remains active. *This is Watch B Directional.*

**Third-wave therapies (DBT, ACT, MBCT) vs. standard CBT:**
ACT and mindfulness-based treatments have accumulated substantial RCT evidence. Meta-analyses (A-Tjak et al. 2015 for ACT; Piet & Hougaard 2011 for MBCT) show superiority vs. waitlist. Comparative effectiveness versus standard CBT remains disputed. *This is Watch B Directional.*

**Researcher allegiance effects:**
Meta-analyses consistently find that studies conducted by developers of a therapy show larger effect sizes than independent replications (Munder et al. 2013, Cuijpers et al. 2010). This produces systematic Watch B at the Methodological level: can published effect size estimates be corrected for allegiance? How large is the allegiance premium?

**Efficacy vs. effectiveness gap:**
Stiles, Barkham, and colleagues have documented that RCT effect sizes often do not replicate in naturalistic effectiveness settings. This generates Watch B Methodological: do RCT protocols with highly trained therapists, carefully selected patients, and manualized delivery generalize to routine practice?

---

### What the Specificity Dispute Does NOT Generate

There is no credentialed research program currently arguing:

- "Psychotherapy produces effects no better than chance encounters with non-therapists"
- "The general positive effect size versus waitlist controls is artifactual"
- "No psychotherapy modality outperforms credible inert control for any condition"

The Wampold common-factors position, which is the most skeptical regarding specific techniques, still maintains that *bona fide* psychological treatments produce reliable, clinically meaningful effects. The dispute is about what makes them work, not whether they work.

This is what the pre-registration's CMech-001 prediction anticipated: disputes about which modality contributes how much (APG), whether researcher allegiance inflates estimates (Methodological), and modality-specific ranking (Directional at modality level) — but not existence-level Watch B about the general claim.

### The Placebo / Non-Specific Supportive Contact Comparison

The pre-registration identified this as the most diagnostic sub-question: "Does therapy outperform non-specific supportive contact?"

This has been studied directly. Baskin et al. (2003) meta-analysis compared "structurally equivalent" active controls (matched for time, therapist attention, and rationale) against specific treatments. Wampold et al. (1997) meta-analysis included "bona fide" vs. non-bona fide comparisons. Cuijpers et al. (2012) specifically examined effects of psychological treatments versus active control conditions.

**Results:** Effect sizes versus waitlist/no treatment are consistently larger (d ≈ 0.7–0.9) than effect sizes versus matched active controls or supportive contact conditions (d ≈ 0.2–0.4). The reduction in effect size is real. But it does not reach zero, and the direction remains positive. Psychological treatments outperform credible supportive contact.

This finding reduces — but does not eliminate — the specific efficacy question. The pattern is consistent with partial specificity: some specific technique effects exist beyond non-specific factors, but common factors account for the majority of variance. This is a contribution-share (APG) rather than existence finding.

**Key implication for the mechanism competition:** The placebo/supportive-contact comparison does not generate Theoretical Watch B at the existence level. The question "does therapy outperform active placebo?" has been studied and the answer is "yes, but modestly." The dispute is about the magnitude and mechanism of the advantage, not whether it exists.

## Counterfactual Unavailability

**Weak.** RCTs with waitlist controls explicitly construct counterfactuals. Random assignment to waitlist or active control provides direct counterfactual comparison for acute treatment effects. The counterfactual for long-run naturalistic outcomes (years after treatment, without ongoing contact) is weaker — treatment ends and the comparison group may also receive other treatments over time. But this is an implementation limitation, not a structural unavailability.

## Mechanism Competition Result

**CMech-001 supported; Narrowed Alternative A pressured.**

The pre-registered CMech-001 prediction: existence-level Watch B weak or absent despite Low Prestige Coordination. **Confirmed.** No credentialed research camp argues psychotherapy is ineffective; the existence question is settled across all major paradigms including the most skeptical (common factors position).

The pre-registered Alternative A prediction: existence-level Watch B active due to prestige fragmentation. **Not confirmed at the existence level.** The common-factors debate does not constitute Theoretical Watch B about whether psychotherapy works — it constitutes APG-level dispute about mechanism and contribution share.

**What Alternative A correctly predicted:** Modality-level disputes have not converged. The Dodo Bird vs. CBT-superiority debate has persisted across decades and hundreds of head-to-head trials without resolution. Watch B is more active at the modality-competition level than in High Prestige Coordination domains (compare RC-041 air pollution, where APG disputes exist but modality-level directional disputes do not, because there is no competing "modality" tradition).

**Interpretation:** DAM (named RCT methodology, adherence measurement, network meta-analysis) appears sufficient to suppress existence-level Watch B even when Prestige Coordination is Low. The competing camps — CBT, psychodynamic, common factors, humanistic — converge on general efficacy despite their mutual criticism and fragmented journal structure. They do not converge on modality superiority or mechanism. This is consistent with the third outcome in the pre-registration table:

> *Intermediate result: existence-level Watch B absent (CMech-001 partial support), but modality-level Watch B active (consistent with low PC affecting modality disputes even if not existence disputes)*

## Surprising Finding

**The modality-level Watch B without existence-level Watch B.** All prior repository domains with active Watch B have Watch B at the existence or optimum level. RC-046 has Watch B that is clearly present and active — but only at the modality-superiority level, not the existence level. The research community fragmentation (Low PC) appears to affect which sub-questions converge rather than whether the field converges at all on the existence question.

This is the third Watch B type distinction that has proven informative after the existence/optimum distinction (RC-043/044) and the existence/methodological distinction (RC-041). It suggests Watch B sub-type analysis has accumulated enough instances to warrant pattern-level attention — though formal pattern promotion requires n≥3 for the specific sub-type configuration.

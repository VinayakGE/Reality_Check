# Reality Check #011

## Claim

> "Generative AI could automate up to 25% of all work tasks globally, equivalent to around 300 million full-time jobs."

## Source

- Speaker: Jan Hatzius et al.
- Organization: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research
- Date: March 26, 2023
- Link: https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/pages/generative-ai-could-raise-global-gdp-by-7-percent.html

---

## Why This Matters

This claim is the most widely cited institutional estimate of AI's impact on labor. It appears in congressional testimony, regulatory filings, investment theses, and news articles as the consensus baseline. If roughly true, it implies a transformation of labor markets at a scale comparable to industrialization. If overstated, it implies that current policy responses, retraining investments, and corporate restructurings are calibrated to a risk that does not materialize at the predicted scale. The Goldman Sachs figure is not a prediction with a specific deadline — it is a capability estimate. That structural difference from most prior audits is the first thing to examine.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The 25% figure is approximately correct and the displacement is occurring
Generative AI tools are compressing time-to-output on exactly the work tasks Goldman identifies: document drafting, code generation, data analysis, customer interaction, and content creation. The workers affected exist in countable occupations. The 25% figure represents the share of tasks (not jobs) that are automatable given current and near-term AI capability. The effect is already visible in hiring slowdowns in categories like copywriting, junior legal analysis, and entry-level programming.

### H2 — The figure is correct as a capability estimate but wrong as a displacement estimate
"Could automate" and "will displace" are different claims. Historical automation estimates (from previous waves of technology) have consistently overstated job destruction because they counted task automation without accounting for task complementarity, labor redeployment, and demand expansion. A lawyer whose drafting tasks are automated does 2x the legal work rather than being displaced. The 25% task automation figure may be technically accurate while the implied job loss figure is systematically overstated by a factor of 3–5x.

### H3 — The figure is not testable because "work tasks" has no agreed operational definition
The Goldman analysis uses O*NET occupational data and maps AI capabilities against task descriptions. The mapping methodology is not auditable: two reasonable analysts applying the same method to the same O*NET data produce figures ranging from 10% to 40% depending on how "automatable" is defined. The 25% figure is not a finding about the world — it is an output of a methodological choice. It can be made consistent with almost any observed outcome through post-hoc adjustment of what counts.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Software developer productivity metrics using AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor) show measurable throughput increases of 20–55% on specific task categories (code completion, documentation, test generation) — consistent with the task-level automation thesis
- Customer service AI deployments at Klarna, Intercom, and similar companies reported handling a significant portion of inbound queries that previously required human agents — an observable automation of a specific, countable task category
- Hiring data in task-automatable occupations (copywriting, data entry, junior financial analysis, paralegal work) showed measurable slowdowns in posting volume in 2023–2025, suggesting market-level anticipation of displacement
- Entry-level white-collar hiring rates at professional services firms declined in 2023–2024 in categories consistent with Goldman's high-automation-exposure classifications

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Total employment in economies where AI adoption is highest (US, UK, Canada) did not decline in 2023–2025; unemployment remained near historic lows, inconsistent with large-scale displacement occurring
- The 300 million jobs figure implies a displacement rate of roughly 3–4% of global formal employment per year over a decade — no prior technology transition has produced sustained net displacement at this rate; historical automation has produced churn, not net loss
- O*NET task mapping, the methodology Goldman uses, has been criticized by labor economists for conflating "tasks that AI can perform in isolation" with "tasks that can be fully automated in a production context" — the operational gap between the two is large
- AI capability benchmarks show large variance between controlled settings and production deployment; systems that perform at human level on standardized tasks frequently underperform in unstructured, real-world environments
- Goldman's own follow-up research (late 2023, early 2024) moderated the original estimate, noting that adoption lag, implementation cost, and regulatory constraints would spread displacement over a longer horizon than the initial report implied

---

## Missing Evidence

- Actual task-level productivity data from large employers tracking AI adoption against task completion rates and headcount — this data exists inside firms but is rarely disclosed; without it, the 25% figure cannot be empirically anchored
- Whether observed hiring slowdowns in automatable occupations represent displacement or demand normalization after post-COVID hiring surges — the counterfactual (what hiring would have looked like without AI) is not available
- A replication of Goldman's O*NET methodology by independent researchers with disclosed parameters — the original report did not publish its full task mapping, making independent validation impossible

## Confidence

**Low**

Reason: The claim is a capability estimate, not a prediction with a deadline. That structural difference matters: there is no date by which the 25% figure will be falsified or confirmed. The claim can absorb almost any observed labor market outcome through definitional adjustment (H3 is probably partially true — the figure is not fully testable). Confidence in the directional claim (AI will automate a significant share of work tasks) is Medium. Confidence in the specific figure (25%) or the implied displacement (300 million jobs) is Low because the measurement methodology is not publicly auditable.

## Review Trigger

- A major replication study applying disclosed methodology to O*NET data and producing a substantially different figure (would wound the specific estimate)
- Labor market data showing measurable net employment decline in high-automation-exposure occupations over 24+ consecutive months (would support H1)
- Goldman Sachs significantly revises the estimate upward or downward in a subsequent research publication with an explanation of what changed (would clarify the methodological assumptions)

## Watch Item Assessment (Compression Pass #001)

**Does this match a prior structure?**

Partially — and that is the interesting finding.

The Goldman claim contains:
- A specific metric (25%, 300 million jobs) — this looks like RC-004 (pure measurable prediction)
- A term that is methodologically contested ("automate" / "work tasks") — this looks like RC-001 (undefined predicate) or RC-002 (undefined subjects)
- No specific deadline — which is a structure that has not appeared before

The closest match is RC-001 (undefined predicate), because the evaluability problem centers on what "automate" means and how it is measured. But RC-001's predicate was philosophically undefined ("replace"). The Goldman predicate is operationally contested — there is a methodology, but the methodology is not disclosed well enough to be independently validated. That is a different kind of definitional problem.

**Preliminary assessment:** Not a clean repeat. The structure appears to be a new variant — **measurable prediction with contested measurement methodology** — distinct from both pure undefined predicates (RC-001, RC-002) and pure measurable predictions (RC-004). Whether this constitutes an eleventh distinct structure or a sub-variant of an existing one requires more audits to determine.

Compression Pass #001 observation holds — no clean repetition at audit 11.

## Open Questions

1. "Could automate up to 25%" contains two hedges — "could" and "up to." Those hedges make the claim technically unfalsifiable: any outcome below 25% is within range, and "could" does not imply "will." Does Reality Check audit differently depending on whether the hedge is rhetorical cover or genuine epistemic humility?
2. The Goldman figure is a capability estimate without a deployment timeline. Prior audits all had either a deadline (RC-003, RC-004, RC-005) or a past event (RC-006 through RC-010). Is a capability estimate a different category of claim that requires different audit structure, or is the absence of a deadline just a special case of "very long unresolved"?
3. H3 identifies the methodological opacity as the core problem. If the claim cannot be independently validated because the methodology is not disclosed, is this closer to RC-010 (structurally indeterminate) or RC-002 (undefined terms that the claimant controls)? The line between "definition not provided" and "counterfactual not available" may matter for how Reality Check handles this class of claim.

# Reality Check #028

## Claim

> "Community health workers — selected from their communities, trained to deliver a package of essential health services, supervised and supplied — can dramatically reduce child mortality and improve health outcomes even in very-low-income settings where formal health infrastructure is absent."

## Source

- Speakers: Ethiopian Ministry of Health (Health Sector Development Program IV, 2010–2015: "achieve the health MDGs through community-level deployment of Health Extension Workers"); WHO (Global Community Health Workers Strategy, 2018); Lancet Commission on Community Health Workers (*Lancet*, 2018: "Community health workers at the dawn of a new era")
- Organizations: Ethiopian Federal Ministry of Health; WHO; USAID; the claim is embedded in global health policy from the Alma-Ata Declaration (1978) onward and was specifically instantiated in Ethiopia's Health Extension Program (HEP), launched 2003 under PM Meles Zenawi
- Date: HEP launch: 2003; HSDP IV stated goals: 2010; external evaluation of HEP: multiple (USAID, WHO, Lancet, 2010–2019); MDG performance assessment: 2015
- Link: Ethiopian HEP: https://www.moh.gov.et; Lancet Commission (2018): https://www.thelancet.com/commissions/community-health-workers; WHO CHW strategy: https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789241514873

*Note: The audit evaluates two separable claims. First, the in-context claim: did Ethiopia's Health Extension Program achieve its stated goals in Ethiopia? Second, the cross-context inference: does Ethiopia's experience demonstrate that CHW programs can produce comparable results in other low-income countries? These are evaluated separately because the first is nearly resolved and the second is actively contested.*

---

## Why This Matters

This audit is selected as the opposite-corner test of the multiplicative explanatory model that emerged from RC-027. South Africa under Mbeki was the **high technical availability / low coordination** case — resources unconverted due to near-zero political authorization. Ethiopia's Health Extension Program is the **low technical availability / high coordination** case: one of the world's poorest countries, minimal healthcare infrastructure, scarce drugs and trained clinicians, but strong and sustained political coordination through a unified national deployment program.

If the original gradient model (technical availability → outcome) is correct, Ethiopia should produce outcomes similar to AGRA — modest gains constrained by weak technical prerequisites. If the revised multiplicative model (technical × coordination → conversion availability → outcome) is correct, Ethiopia should substantially outperform AGRA despite comparable or weaker technical availability, because high coordination more than compensates for low technical resources.

That directional prediction — *Ethiopia outperforms what technical availability alone predicts* — is pre-registered before reading the outcome data.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — The HEP model demonstrates that coordinated CHW deployment can transform health outcomes with minimal technical prerequisites
A single trained community health worker, selected from her village and supervised by a district health officer, can deliver a package of interventions (oral rehydration therapy, malaria prevention, family planning, vaccination promotion, antenatal care referrals, hygiene education) that collectively produce large mortality reductions. None of these interventions requires hospital infrastructure. ORT requires only water, salt, and sugar. Insecticide-treated bed nets require no clinical infrastructure. Contraceptive distribution requires trained distributors but not physicians. The HEP worked because it decomposed health improvement into a package of low-technical-threshold interventions that a trained CHW could deliver. The coordination that matters is not coordination of high-tech resources; it is coordination of the right package of low-threshold interventions.

### H2 — Ethiopia's health gains reflect the combination of the HEP with concurrent interventions, not the HEP alone
Ethiopia's under-5 mortality decline from 2003 to 2019 coincided with: the national malaria control program (significant scale-up of insecticide-treated nets and artemisinin treatment, substantially funded by the Global Fund); the expanded immunization program (substantial increase in DTP3 and measles coverage, funded by GAVI); the national nutrition program; and general economic growth (Ethiopia's GDP grew approximately 8–10% annually from 2003 to 2019). Attribution of the mortality decline to the HEP specifically, as distinct from these concurrent programs, requires identification strategies that have not been applied rigorously at national scale. The HEP contribution may be large or modest; the aggregate decline is real but its attribution is uncertain.

### H3 — The CHW model does not transfer consistently across contexts despite Ethiopia's success
Ethiopia's HEP achieved results within Ethiopia through a combination of strong central coordination, a specific cultural context (respect for female community health workers in Ethiopian village settings), and a particular set of priority conditions (malaria, childhood diarrhea, malnutrition) that are addressable by low-technology interventions. CHW programs in other SSA countries with equivalent political commitment but different cultural contexts, different disease profiles, or different supply chain arrangements have produced more variable results. India's ASHA program (10 million CHWs deployed) shows significant state-level variation suggesting that even within-country transfer is constrained by contextual factors. The claim that Ethiopia "demonstrates" what CHW programs can do in low-income countries may overgeneralize from a favorable case.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- MDG 4 achievement: Ethiopia was one of only a handful of sub-Saharan African countries to achieve MDG 4 (reduce under-5 mortality by two-thirds from the 1990 baseline) by 2015; under-5 mortality fell from approximately 204/1,000 (1990) to approximately 59/1,000 (2015) — a 71% reduction
- HEP coverage: by 2012, approximately 38,000 HEWs were deployed, providing more than 90% coverage of rural kebeles; this is one of the most rapidly achieved community health worker coverage rates in any country
- Specific intervention data: malaria case fatality in HEP-served areas decreased substantially; contraceptive prevalence rate increased from approximately 8% (2003) to approximately 36% (2016); DTP3 immunization coverage increased from approximately 25% (2003) to approximately 73% (2016); all of these are attributable at least in part to HEW service delivery
- Cost-effectiveness: USAID and World Bank evaluations found the HEP to be among the most cost-effective maternal and child health programs assessed globally, with estimated cost per DALY averted well below standard intervention thresholds
- Comparison to similar-income countries: Ethiopia's rate of under-5 mortality decline was faster than most comparable sub-Saharan African countries during the same period, and faster than what Ethiopia's GDP growth alone would predict using historical income-mortality relationships

## Evidence Against the Claim

- MDG 5 not achieved: maternal mortality fell significantly (from approximately 1,400/100,000 in 1990 to approximately 401/100,000 in 2015) but did not meet the MDG 5 target of 75% reduction; skilled birth attendance remained low (approximately 27% by 2019); the interventions that require higher-level clinical care (obstetric emergencies) transferred less well than those requiring only CHW-level skills
- Attribution ambiguity: Ethiopia's health gains coincided with the largest malaria control program in the country's history, GAVI-funded immunization scale-up, and substantial external financing from PEPFAR, the Global Fund, and USAID; isolating the HEP's specific contribution to under-5 mortality reduction requires counterfactual estimation that has not been rigorously done
- Global transfer remains variable: India's ASHA program (1 million+ CHWs, designed on similar principles) shows dramatic state-level variation, with some states achieving high effectiveness and others showing minimal impact despite comparable national deployment; this variability suggests that program design alone does not determine outcomes
- Ethiopia's political coordination was exceptional: the EPRDF government had levels of central coordination and disciplined program implementation that are not characteristic of most low-income country governments; the HEP's political conditions may be more difficult to replicate than its technical design

---

## Missing Evidence

- Decomposed attribution: what fraction of Ethiopia's under-5 mortality decline is attributable to HEW-delivered interventions specifically (ORT, bed net distribution, nutrition counseling) vs. externally funded vertical programs (malaria, immunization)? This requires quasi-experimental methods at the district level and has not been produced at adequate scale
- State-level variation within Ethiopia: are there Ethiopian regions where the HEP underperformed the national average, and if so, what were the distinctive features? This within-country variation would sharpen the analysis of what coordination density actually requires
- Counterfactual rate of decline: what would Ethiopia's under-5 mortality decline have been without the HEP, holding the malaria and immunization programs constant? Without this counterfactual, the quantitative HEP contribution remains uncertain

**Watch B fires — tentatively.**

This is the first uncertainty about Watch B since the pattern was observed in RC-019. The source-context causal attribution — did the HEP cause Ethiopia's under-5 mortality decline? — is not cleanly established. Multiple concurrent programs (malaria control, GAVI immunization, external nutrition funding) coincided with the HEP. If the attribution precision gap is a distinct bottleneck type from Watch B, then Watch B does not fire and this is the second instance of attribution precision gap (after RC-023). If the attribution precision gap is a form of Watch B, then Watch B fires — making this the 9th instance.

This is the weakest Watch B firing in the repository — less clear than the six prior strong instances — but consistent with the bottleneck structure.

**Multiplicative model — opposite-corner test results:**

**The pre-registered directional prediction:** Ethiopia (lower technical, higher coordination) should outperform AGRA (higher technical, lower coordination) on the ratio of outcome improvement to technical baseline.

| Case | Technical availability | Coordination estimate | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| AGRA (RC-025) | ~35% | ~25% | 14–27% yield increase; far below target |
| Ethiopia HEP (RC-028) | ~20–25% | ~65–70% | 71% under-5 mortality reduction; MDG 4 achieved |

**The pre-registered prediction is supported.** Ethiopia achieved substantially larger proportional improvements than AGRA despite a weaker technical baseline. The ratio of improvement to technical availability is dramatically higher for Ethiopia than for AGRA. This is the directional result the multiplicative model predicted.

The prediction was not "Ethiopia succeeds fully" — it was "Ethiopia outperforms what technical availability alone predicts." The under-5 mortality result (71% decline; MDG 4 achieved) substantially outperforms what a ~20% technical availability score in the original gradient model would predict.

**Mechanism observation:** H1's explanation for why low-technical CHW delivery succeeded is significant and consistent with the multiplicative model. The HEP package was designed around low-threshold interventions (ORT, bed net distribution, contraception, referrals) rather than high-technology interventions. The coordination achieved high conversion efficiency of a limited technical package, rather than attempting to convert unavailable high-technology resources. This is a more precise characterization of how coordination multiplied in this context: it assembled a package whose prerequisites were low-threshold enough to be near-universal (water, bed nets, basic medicines), then coordinated the deployment of that package consistently.

This creates a nuance in the multiplicative model. The multiplication is not simply:

```
Technical Resources × Coordination → Availability
```

It may be better characterized as:

```
Coordination selects for low-threshold components of the technical package
→ achieves high conversion efficiency of those components
→ produces outcomes from a subset of the total possible intervention set
```

Ethiopia did not convert high-technology prerequisites that were absent. It coordinated delivery of the subset of interventions whose prerequisites were present (or achievable). The implication: coordination may operate not just as a multiplier on a fixed resource set but as a *selector* that identifies which components of the available resource set can be converted. AGRA's coordination failure may partly reflect a mismatch between the intervention package (which assumed state procurement systems and market infrastructure that were absent) and the available resource set.

This does not validate the multiplicative model — the coordination estimates are rough — but the ordering is not random and is not what technical-availability alone predicts.

## Open Questions

1. H1 suggests that coordination operated in Ethiopia by selecting a low-threshold package rather than by converting high-threshold unavailable resources. Is this a general feature of successful coordinated deployment in low-resource settings? If so, the multiplicative model needs an additional parameter: the threshold level of the intervention package, not just the amount of technical resources. AGRA's failure may be partly explained by attempting to convert high-threshold prerequisites (state procurement systems) rather than designing around available low-threshold resources.

2. Watch B's ambiguous firing in RC-028 introduces attribution precision gap as a potentially recurring bottleneck. The prior instance was RC-023 (ORT), where multi-intervention context obscured ORT's specific contribution. RC-028 repeats the structure. This is two instances of the same bottleneck type — not from Watch B (competing causal interpretations) but from multi-program concurrent deployment making proportional attribution uncertain. Is attribution precision gap a stable distinct type, or is it a variant of Watch B that the repository has been classifying inconsistently?

3. India's ASHA program provides significant within-program variation that RC-028 did not examine: approximately 1 million CHWs deployed with similar design to Ethiopia's HEW, showing highly variable state-level results. If similar variation exists in Ethiopia at the regional level, the HEP's national average may obscure cases where coordination was insufficient — which would further test the multiplicative model by identifying the level at which coordination needs to operate.

# Reality Check #026

## Claim

> "Sri Lanka and Malaysia demonstrate that dramatic reductions in maternal mortality are achievable in low-income countries through political commitment, skilled birth attendance, and universal access to care — other countries at low income levels should invest in a trained skilled birth attendant workforce as the primary maternal health strategy."

## Source

- Speakers: Indra Pathmanathan, Jerker Liljestrand, Jo M. Martins, et al. — authors of *Investing in Maternal Health: Learning from Malaysia and Sri Lanka* (World Bank / WHO, 2003); the report draws the explicit policy inference that other low-income countries should replicate the model
- Organizations: World Bank / WHO; the policy position is embedded in MDG 5 (Reduce maternal mortality by three-quarters between 1990 and 2015) and in the WHO's Skilled Birth Attendance initiative; it has been continuously reaffirmed in the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG 3.1)
- Date: Sri Lanka's MMR decline: 1940s–1990s; policy inference formalized in Pathmanathan et al. (2003); reaffirmed by WHO throughout MDG and SDG eras
- Link: Pathmanathan et al. (2003), *Investing in Maternal Health: Learning from Malaysia and Sri Lanka*: https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/handle/10986/15066; WHO Skilled Birth Attendance: https://www.who.int/health-topics/maternal-health

*Note: The audit evaluates two components: (a) the claim that Sri Lanka's success was achievable through domestic political commitment and institutional investment without exceptional external resources — the direct test of the Prerequisite Availability hypothesis; and (b) the policy inference that the model transfers globally. These components are separable: (a) may be Supported while (b) is Unresolved.*

*Structural context: Sri Lanka's maternal mortality rate (MMR) fell from approximately 1,500 per 100,000 live births in 1947 to approximately 90 per 100,000 by 1980, and to approximately 36 per 100,000 by 2020. The decline from 1947 to 1980 — a greater than 90% reduction — occurred when Sri Lanka's per-capita GDP was comparable to current sub-Saharan African nations. The program was primarily domestically funded. Sri Lanka was not a major aid recipient during the critical decades of its decline.*

---

## Why This Matters

Maternal mortality remains one of the largest remaining gaps in global health equity. Approximately 287,000 women died from preventable pregnancy-related causes in 2020, with 95% of deaths occurring in low- and lower-middle-income countries. The difference in MMR between high-income and low-income countries is approximately 200-fold.

Sri Lanka's achievement — reaching MMR levels comparable to high-income countries at a fraction of their per-capita income, primarily through domestic resources — is one of the most widely cited cases in global health as a model for what is achievable. The policy inference (other countries should replicate this) has driven WHO recommendations, MDG targets, and SDG commitments for three decades.

The repository's current leading question is whether prerequisite availability requires exceptional external resources, or whether coordination and political alignment can generate availability through a different path. Sri Lanka is selected specifically as the mirror image of AGRA: where AGRA tested whether high external resources were sufficient, Sri Lanka tests whether they were necessary.

The Prerequisite Availability hypothesis predicts: *availability → transfer success.* The AGRA audit (RC-025) showed that resources partially built availability but coordination gaps limited prerequisite construction. The question for RC-026:

If Sri Lanka's success arose from domestic political coordination converting limited resources into effective prerequisites, this supports coordination density (Model D) as a path to availability distinct from resource level (Model A). If Sri Lanka's success arose because its prerequisites were near-universal or near-universal for its context, Sri Lanka may be closer to ORT (RC-023) than to PEPFAR (RC-024) — which would mean the domestic program was not building prerequisites so much as activating ones already latent in the population.

**Discriminating between these two readings:** If skilled birth attendants required genuine institutional construction (training pipelines, deployment systems, rural health center networks, community acceptance), Sri Lanka demonstrates coordination-driven construction of context-specific prerequisites. If the prerequisites were latent (geographic compactness enabling rural access, high female literacy enabling midwife recruitment, existing community respect for health workers), Sri Lanka demonstrates favorable initial conditions rather than prerequisite construction.

## Alternative Explanations

### H1 — Sri Lanka's success generalizes: domestic political commitment can build prerequisites without exceptional external resources
The Public Health Midwife (PHM) program deployed one government-trained midwife for every 3,000 population across Sri Lanka's rural districts, supported by a network of rural health centers staffed by Medical Officers of Health. The training, deployment, and supervision system was built through domestic political commitment across successive governments — maintained through government changes because it served a broadly popular constituency. Free universal education contributed a pipeline of literate women eligible for midwifery training. Sri Lanka's MMR decline preceded the WHO's skilled birth attendance initiative by decades; the program was developed endogenously, not imported. The policy inference is that the causal variables — government training of a deployed midwife workforce, universal access to care, female education — are politically buildable in other low-income contexts given sufficient political will. The resource requirement is modest by international development standards; the primary requirement is domestic prioritization.

### H2 — Sri Lanka's success depended on pre-conditions that are not themselves policy-buildable and do not characterize most low-income countries
Sri Lanka in the 1940s had several unusual features for its income level: female literacy rates substantially above the regional average, a compact geography (roughly the size of Ireland) enabling unusually low-cost rural access, a political culture in which competing parties in a competitive democracy bid for votes by promising social services (including health), and a Buddhist cultural context with high community respect for formal education and professional roles. These pre-conditions lowered the cost of prerequisite construction: training midwives was easier because literate women were available; deploying them to rural areas was easier because distances were short; achieving community acceptance was easier because of cultural context. Countries without these pre-conditions face a different cost curve. The policy inference — "invest in skilled birth attendance and results will follow" — abstracts away the pre-conditions that made the investment productive.

### H3 — The model transfers some components but not the full package; skilled birth attendance is necessary but not sufficient
The WHO's skilled birth attendance recommendation has been widely adopted in SDG-era maternal health programs. Countries with low MMR have near-universal skilled birth attendance; countries with high MMR have low skilled birth attendance. But cross-sectional correlation does not resolve the direction of causality, and many countries have substantially increased skilled birth attendance rates without achieving comparable MMR reductions. The components that transferred: training midwives and deploying them is achievable in many contexts. The components that transferred less well: the accompanying package (rural health infrastructure, referral systems, community trust, quality of care) has been harder to build. Skilled birth attendance without functioning referral systems for obstetric emergencies (cesarean capacity, blood banking, neonatal care) reduces but does not eliminate preventable maternal deaths. The incomplete transfer of the full package explains why skilled birth attendance rates have risen more than MMR has fallen in several sub-Saharan African contexts.

## Evidence Supporting the Claim

- Sri Lanka MMR trajectory: from approximately 1,500/100,000 in 1947 to approximately 90/100,000 by 1980 to approximately 36/100,000 by 2020 — a greater than 97% reduction from baseline, achieved at income levels comparable to current sub-Saharan Africa
- Malaysia: parallel trajectory through similar mechanisms (government-trained community midwives, rural health centers); MMR fell from approximately 1,000/100,000 in the 1950s to under 30 by the 1990s; also primarily domestically funded during critical decline phase
- Resource level: Sri Lanka's health expenditure during the critical 1950s–1980s period averaged approximately 1–2% of GDP — comparable to or below current sub-Saharan African country averages; the resource requirements were not exceptional
- PHM program design: the PHM-to-population ratio and geographic distribution have been specifically credited with the decline in a series of retrospective studies; the program structure is documented in sufficient detail for replication analysis
- Community acceptance: PHMs were drawn from the communities they served (local women trained at government schools), which generated community trust that external health workers often lack — suggesting the within-community recruitment model is replicable, not Sri Lanka-specific
- Partial transfer cases: Thailand (MMR fell from approximately 400/100,000 in 1970 to under 20 by 2010), parts of Bangladesh (MMR fell from over 600/100,000 in 1990 to under 200 by 2015) — both achieved substantial reductions through programs explicitly modeled on Sri Lanka/Malaysia, with limited external resources relative to contemporaneous PEPFAR-scale programs

## Evidence Against the Claim

- Global transfer gap: despite WHO advocacy for skilled birth attendance since 2001 and MDG/SDG targets for three decades, sub-Saharan Africa's MMR declined from approximately 1,000/100,000 in 1990 to approximately 533/100,000 in 2020 — a 47% reduction against a target of 75%; skilled birth attendance rates have risen substantially (from approximately 40% to approximately 60% of births) but MMR has declined more slowly than the Sri Lanka trajectory would predict
- Quality vs. presence gap: studies document that skilled birth attendance rates (percentage of births with an attendant present) have improved more than the quality of care provided at those births; in many contexts, "skilled birth attendant present" does not mean "obstetric emergency capability available"; the label transfers, the capability does not
- Referral system gap: maternal deaths in SSA are dominated by hemorrhage and hypertensive disorders requiring surgical and blood product intervention; Sri Lanka's success required not just midwives but a functioning referral system (rural health centers → district hospitals → tertiary care); the referral system requires significantly more infrastructure than midwife deployment alone, and it is the part of the package that has transferred least in SSA
- Pre-condition specificity: Sri Lanka's female literacy rate in 1947 (approximately 43%) was substantially above comparable-income countries of the era; the female literacy-to-MMR correlation suggests that some of Sri Lanka's advantage was pre-installed rather than constructed by the program; the program was an accelerator of favorable pre-conditions, not a substitute for them
- Conflation risk: the WHO's skilled birth attendance metric conflates trained (able to manage complications) and untrained (present but unable to intervene) attendants in some country reporting; reported gains in skilled birth attendance may overstate genuine capability gains

---

## Missing Evidence

- Within-country heterogeneity in countries that adopted the Sri Lanka model: which districts or provinces succeeded and which did not, and whether variation correlates with pre-condition availability (female literacy, road infrastructure, political alignment), is not systematically documented
- The pre-condition contribution: what proportion of Sri Lanka's MMR decline is attributable to the PHM program vs. the pre-existing advantages (female literacy, geographic compactness, political competition)? Retrospective attribution studies exist but cannot isolate the contribution of pre-conditions that were present throughout the program
- Quality-adjusted transfer data: what is the MMR impact of skilled birth attendance deployment that includes full referral capability (not just attendant presence)? This would separate the transferable from the non-transferable components of the model

**Prerequisite Availability hypothesis — RC-026 contribution:**

The pre-registered discriminating question: *Can high availability emerge without exceptional external resources?*

The answer is: yes, for the core prerequisites. Sri Lanka's primary prerequisites — a trained midwife workforce, rural health centers, and a referral system — were built through domestic resources at income levels comparable to current high-MMR countries. Resource level (Model A) does not explain the gap between Sri Lanka and countries that have not replicated its success.

**The discriminating result:** Sri Lanka weakens Model A from the opposite direction that AGRA did.

- AGRA: exceptional resources, complex prerequisites, partial availability → Model A weakened because resources were not sufficient
- Sri Lanka: modest resources, complex prerequisites, high availability → Model A weakened because resources were not necessary

Together, RC-025 and RC-026 constrain Model A significantly. Resources are neither sufficient (AGRA) nor necessary (Sri Lanka) for high prerequisite availability. Something else is the operative variable.

**What Sri Lanka shows:** The operative variable is more consistent with coordination density / political alignment than resource level. Sri Lanka's distinguishing features:
- Competitive democratic politics in which both parties competed on social service delivery created durable, multi-decade political commitment to the PHM program across government changes
- Within-community midwife recruitment generated trust that external programs struggle to build
- Geographic compactness reduced the cost of rural deployment to a level domestic resources could sustain
- Pre-existing female literacy provided the recruitment pipeline

Whether these features are "pre-conditions" (favorable initial state; not constructed; not transferable) or "coordination outcomes" (achieved through specific political choices; potentially replicable given similar political conditions) is the key unresolved question.

**The partial-transfer evidence:** Thailand and parts of Bangladesh achieved substantial MMR reductions through programs modeled on Sri Lanka, with limited external resources. This suggests the model does transfer when some combination of political commitment and institutional capacity is present — the transfer failure in high-burden SSA is not universal, it is correlated with specific contextual features.

## Open Questions

1. Sri Lanka's three distinguishing pre-conditions (female literacy, geographic compactness, competitive political market for social services) are partially separable. Female literacy rates in the 1940s were not a policy variable — they were a historical legacy. Geographic compactness is not a policy variable. Only the political structure was, arguably, a product of choices made after independence. If two of three enabling conditions are not constructible, is the Sri Lanka model a case of coordination density enabling availability, or a case of favorable initial conditions lowering the cost of construction to a level domestic resources could sustain? These are different causal claims with different policy implications.

2. RC-025 and RC-026 together constrain Model A (resources) significantly. But they do not yet establish what the operative variable is. "Coordination density" is currently derived from two cases with a shared feature — both had sustained political alignment over decades. Before it is treated as an explanation, it needs a case where coordination density was high but availability did not emerge, or where it was low but availability emerged anyway. Is this the right question for RC-027?

3. The gradient model's first four calibration points are all from the pilot-to-policy or cross-context inference families. Every case with higher availability came from a different prerequisite structure. The calibration is informative but may be confounded by structural differences between domains. What would falsify the gradient model: a case with high availability but partial transfer, or low availability but full transfer.

# Track 2 Experimental Architecture

**Date committed:** 2026-06-25
**Status:** Architecture specification — no Track 2 observations taken yet
**Purpose:** Define what a discriminating Track 2 experiment would require; identify what class of investigation could, in principle, distinguish Alternative B from the reality account.

This document is the Track 2 analog to the operationalization documents in earlier stages. It does not produce a Track 2 observation. It defines what one would look like — a prerequisite for any future Track 2 investigation, and the artifact that makes Track 2 investigable rather than merely conceptually open.

---

## The Track 2 Problem, Precisely Stated

**The observed regularity:** Across repository domains, Watch B character correlates with domain maturity and DAM. High maturity + High DAM → Watch B absent or Methodological. Low maturity + Low DAM → Watch B Theoretical. CMech-001 claims DAM causes this pattern (DAM develops → Watch B transforms).

**Alternative B's challenge:** The observed correlation between DAM and Watch B suppression is not produced by DAM causing Watch B transformation. Instead:

1. Domains with initially strong or clean causal evidence attract methodological development (DAM follows strong evidence)
2. The same strong initial evidence, not the subsequent DAM, is what correlates with Watch B suppression
3. CMech-001's causal arrow runs backward: it correctly identifies the correlation but misattributes the mechanism

Under Alternative B, the repository's regularity is a property of research-program evolution — strong initial evidence generates both DAM development and Watch B suppression independently — not a property of evidential structure in the world that DAM reveals.

**Why this matters:** If Alternative B is correct, then adding DAM to a domain with weak initial evidence would not suppress Watch B. The methodology's operational implication (improve causal attribution to clarify contested claims) would not follow from the observed pattern.

---

## Why Standard RC Audits Cannot Address Track 2

Standard RC audits observe the current epistemic state of a domain: what evidence exists, what Watch B character is active, what DAM has been developed. They do not observe the sequence in which these states developed.

Alternative B's mechanism operates through *historical order* — strong initial evidence preceded DAM development, which preceded Watch B suppression. This is a claim about causal sequence across time, not about current correlation. An audit that observes current correlation between DAM and Watch B suppression is consistent with both CMech-001 (DAM caused Watch B transformation) and Alternative B (common prior cause produced both).

To discriminate CMech-001 from Alternative B, the required observation is not a new domain's current epistemic state. It is the temporal sequence within a domain: which came first, DAM development or Watch B suppression?

That observation type requires historical depth that standard audits do not provide.

---

## The Discriminating Question

**CMech-001 predicts:** DAM development temporally precedes Watch B transformation. Where DAM was introduced at an identifiable moment (a new methodology paper, an institutional application, a formal guideline), Watch B character should be different after that moment than before.

**Alternative B predicts:** Watch B transformation temporally precedes or co-occurs with DAM development. The research program converges on a causal conclusion because of initial evidence quality; DAM formalizes that convergence rather than producing it. DAM and Watch B suppression should both follow strong initial evidence, but DAM should not precede Watch B suppression.

**The discriminating observation:** A domain where DAM was introduced at an identifiable historical discontinuity, and Watch B character can be assessed at two time points — before and after the DAM introduction — with the causal evidence base held approximately constant between those points.

If Watch B is different after DAM introduction (with similar underlying evidence): supports CMech-001.
If Watch B was already changing or had already changed before DAM introduction: supports Alternative B.

---

## Candidate Investigation Approaches

### Approach A — Historical Discontinuity Analysis

**Design:** For one or more repository domains, trace the timeline of:
1. Initial causal claim formulation
2. Watch B character at the outset (was it Theoretical? Directional? Absent?)
3. DAM development event — identifiable moment when the named methodology was introduced or institutionalized
4. Watch B character after DAM introduction

**What would support CMech-001:** Watch B was Theoretical before DAM introduction; Watch B became Methodological or APG-structured after DAM introduction; the underlying causal evidence base did not change substantially between the two time points.

**What would support Alternative B:** Watch B was already weakening or had already shifted before DAM introduction; DAM was introduced to formalize a consensus that had formed through other means; the initial evidence quality is high enough to explain the Watch B pattern independently of DAM.

**Feasibility:** Requires systematic historical reconstruction of a research program — not a literature summary of current evidence, but a period-by-period analysis of epistemic state. This is more like a history-of-science investigation than an RC audit. Feasible for domains with well-documented methodological histories (air pollution research, for example, has a traceable DAM history from the Harvard Six Cities Study through IARC Group 1 classification through GBD).

**Primary limitation:** Clean separation between "Watch B changing" and "evidence base strengthening" is difficult because they tend to co-occur. A domain that introduced DAM also typically produced more evidence at the same time.

---

### Approach B — DAM-Without-Consensus Cases

**Design:** Identify domains where DAM has been developed and applied but Watch B remains active (Theoretical or strongly Directional at the existence level). If such cases exist, they present a problem for Alternative B's most straightforward form: Alternative B predicts DAM follows consensus, so high DAM without consensus should be rare.

**Partial evidence already in repository:** RC-043 (dietary sodium) and RC-044 (alcohol) show high DAM with persistent Watch B at the optimum level. Alternative B can accommodate these by noting that consensus exists at the existence level (which Alternative B explains via strong initial evidence) while optimum-level disputes persist (which Alternative B would explain as a region where initial evidence was weaker).

**What would constitute a stronger test:** A domain with high DAM and persistent Theoretical Watch B at the *existence* level — not merely the optimum level. Such a domain would require Alternative B to explain why the research program developed sophisticated DAM without the initial evidence quality that Alternative B says explains DAM development. This is the configuration Alternative B most struggles to accommodate.

**Feasibility:** Requires searching for high-DAM, existence-level-Watch-B-active domains. This may be rare precisely because Alternative B could be partly correct — strong evidence may genuinely attract DAM. But if such a domain exists, it would be a strong observation.

**Primary limitation:** The absence of such cases is not strong evidence for Alternative B. Rarity could reflect CMech-001 (DAM is effective, so such cases are rare because DAM suppressed the Watch B) or Alternative B (DAM development requires strong initial evidence, so such cases don't arise). The presence of such cases would be informative; the absence would not discriminate.

---

### Approach C — Methodological Natural Experiment

**Design:** Identify a domain where the same causal claim has been studied in two research communities (different countries, disciplines, or institutional traditions) with different rates of DAM adoption, but with similar underlying evidence quality. If DAM is doing causal work, Watch B should differ between the high-DAM and low-DAM communities studying the same claim.

**Example candidate:** Cognitive behavioral therapy was developed and studied in the UK (NICE-driven adoption, manualized protocols, adherence measures — higher DAM) and in continental European psychotherapy traditions (more emphasis on relational and psychodynamic approaches, lower standardization — lower DAM in some traditions). The same depression claim (is CBT effective?) has been studied in both contexts. If Watch B character differs between these communities in predictable ways, that supports CMech-001.

**What would support CMech-001:** Communities with higher DAM adoption show less existence-level Watch B on the same claim, with similar underlying trial evidence.

**What would support Alternative B:** Watch B character is similar between communities regardless of DAM adoption; or DAM-higher communities have different evidence bases that independently explain Watch B differences.

**Feasibility:** Requires within-claim, across-community comparison. Harder to control than a simple domain audit. The "same claim" requirement is difficult because different research communities often study slightly different operationalizations of the same claim.

**Primary limitation:** Community selection into DAM adoption is not random. Communities that adopt DAM may differ systematically from those that don't, making it difficult to attribute Watch B differences to DAM rather than to selection differences.

---

### Approach D — Prospective Domain Tracking

**Design:** Identify a domain currently at low DAM with active Theoretical Watch B. Track the domain over time as DAM develops. If CMech-001 is correct, Watch B should transform as DAM matures. If Alternative B is correct, Watch B will transform when the underlying evidence becomes strong enough to generate consensus, independently of DAM.

**What would support CMech-001:** Watch B transforms at the time of DAM introduction, even without substantial new causal evidence in the interim; or Watch B transforms faster in the presence of institutional DAM adoption than in its absence.

**What would support Alternative B:** Watch B transforms when a critical evidence threshold is crossed, regardless of whether DAM has been formally adopted; or DAM is introduced after a major evidence event and the Watch B transformation began at the evidence event rather than the DAM introduction.

**Feasibility:** Requires long time horizon — domain tracking over years or decades. Not feasible for a single investigation session. However, if the repository continues operating, this approach becomes feasible through repeated observation of the same low-DAM domains.

**Primary limitation:** The time horizon required makes this a long-run program rather than a near-term Track 2 investigation.

---

## Which Approach to Pursue First

**Approach A (Historical Discontinuity Analysis) is the most immediately feasible** for several reasons:

1. It can be applied to existing repository domains with well-documented histories rather than requiring new domain selection
2. It produces a definite observation (before/after DAM introduction) rather than requiring a long time horizon or cross-community comparison
3. The repository already has the domain knowledge to identify where DAM was introduced at specific historical moments (IARC Group 1 classification for air pollution; GBD 2013 for alcohol; the Harvard Six Cities Study through the Pope/Dockery papers for PM2.5 epidemiology)

**Recommended first Track 2 investigation:** Historical discontinuity analysis of the air pollution research program (RC-041 domain). Air pollution has the most clearly documented DAM history of any repository domain: the causal claim existed before the Harvard Six Cities Study (1993) and before IARC Group 1 classification (2013); DAM was developed in identifiable stages; Watch B character can potentially be assessed at each stage. This gives the investigation a timeline to work with.

**What the investigation must not do:** Produce a standard RC audit. The output is not a claim-level epistemic assessment. It is a sequence-level analysis: at each identifiable historical stage, what was Watch B character, and what DAM was present?

---

## What a Track 2 Observation Must Record

A Track 2 observation is valid if it records:

1. **At least two time points** for the same domain/claim: a pre-DAM-event period and a post-DAM-event period
2. **Watch B character at each time point**, assessed against the evidence available at that time (not current evidence)
3. **DAM presence at each time point**, specified independently of Watch B character
4. **Evidence-base assessment at each time point**, to determine whether Watch B changes can be attributed to new evidence rather than to DAM introduction

Without all four elements, the observation cannot discriminate CMech-001 from Alternative B.

---

## What Would Constitute Evidence for Alternative B

Alternative B would receive direct supporting evidence if:

**Pattern 1:** In the historical discontinuity analysis, Watch B transformation preceded DAM introduction across multiple domains. If Watch B was already weakening before the identifiable DAM event, that suggests consensus formed first and DAM formalized it.

**Pattern 2:** In the DAM-without-consensus search (Approach B), no such cases exist and the absence is explained by Alternative B's selection mechanism (high DAM requires strong initial evidence that also independently suppresses Watch B).

**Pattern 3:** In a prospective domain tracking (Approach D), Watch B transforms at a major evidence event and DAM introduction follows the transformation rather than preceding it.

**What would not constitute evidence for Alternative B:** The observation that DAM and Watch B suppression tend to co-occur. Co-occurrence is consistent with both CMech-001 and Alternative B, which is why they have been indistinguishable under standard audits.

---

## What Would Constitute Evidence Against Alternative B

Alternative B would be directly pressured if:

**Pattern 1:** In the historical discontinuity analysis, Watch B transformation followed DAM introduction in a period when the underlying evidence base did not substantially change.

**Pattern 2:** A high-DAM, existence-level-Watch-B-active domain is identified (Approach B variant) — showing DAM can exist without the consensus Alternative B says DAM requires.

**Pattern 3:** In the cross-community comparison (Approach C), communities with higher DAM show less Watch B on the same claim, with similar underlying evidence.

---

## Relationship to Track 1 and Stage 6

A Track 2 result does not resolve Track 1. Even if Alternative B is pressured by a historical discontinuity analysis, CMech-001 remains in competition with narrowed Alternative A (prestige coordination) at the mechanism level. Track 1 and Track 2 are independent investigations. A Track 2 result that supports CMech-001 confirms that the regularity is real (not artifactual); it does not determine which of the Track 1 mechanisms explains the real regularity.

Similarly, a Track 2 result bearing on the regularity's status does not constitute Stage 6 evidence. Whether the methodology improves decisions is a question about deployment, not about whether the underlying pattern is real.

**Date:** 2026-06-23
**Scope:** RC-001 through RC-010
**Method:** Two questions. No framework proposals. No taxonomy.

## Question 1: What kinds of claims are actually being audited?

Not domains. Structures — the shape of the claim, what makes it evaluable or not.

**RC-001 — Prediction with undefined predicate**
"Agents will replace SaaS." The term doing the most work — *replace* — was undefined. The claim was structured as a binary (agents or SaaS) where the relationship between them was never specified. Evaluability blocked by the verb, not the nouns.

**RC-002 — Prediction with undefined subjects**
"One person can build a billion-dollar company." The two nouns doing the most work — *one person* and *company* — were undefined. The claim was a logical proposition with load-bearing variables that had no agreed domain. Evaluability blocked by the subjects, not the predicate.

**RC-003 — Self-referential prediction**
"We will have AGI by 2030." Terms were defined — but defined by the claimant in a way that makes the claimant the arbiter of resolution. Evaluability blocked because the success condition is controlled by the party being evaluated.

**RC-004 — Pure measurable prediction**
"India will achieve 500 GW by 2030." Metric is external (gigawatts, measured by national agencies and international bodies), timeline is fixed, claimant does not control the measurement. Evaluability is high; resolution is blocked by time and execution.

**RC-005 — Serial deadline claim**
"Volume production before 2027." Terms are clear. The problem is the claimant's track record: every prior deadline for the same outcome has been revised. The date is the unreliable variable, not the definition. Evaluability is high; credibility of the specific deadline is low.

**RC-006 — Co-resolved prediction**
"WeWork will be profitable by 2020." Outcome and mechanism were inseparable: the company going bankrupt resolved the profitability claim and the "technology company" mechanism claim simultaneously. No separation possible because the failure mode collapsed both.

**RC-007 — Separable prediction + mechanism**
"Bitcoin will hit $100K because of ETF approval and the halving." Prediction resolved (it did). Mechanism did not (the November move was post-election, not ETF/halving driven). The prediction and its stated cause were separable because one is an outcome and the other is a causal attribution that can be independently assessed.

**RC-008 — Binary threshold claim**
"Office centricity is over." The claim implied a threshold crossing — from office-centric to not. Reality produced a gradient. Evaluability was not the problem; the claim's binary structure was wrong for a continuous outcome.

**RC-009 — Analogical prediction**
"China faces a Japan-style lost decade." The mechanism is borrowed from a historical case (Japan 1991) and can therefore be evaluated independently of the outcome. The audit can ask: does the Japan *sequence* match China's situation, regardless of whether China eventually stagnates? First claim where the mechanism is an imported historical template.

**RC-010 — Counterfactual causal claim**
"Cambridge Analytica caused Trump to win." The outcome is known. The causal attribution requires a comparison world (Trump campaign without CA) that was never run and cannot be constructed. Permanently indeterminate regardless of evidence collected. Evaluability is blocked by the structure of the causal question, not by missing data.

## Question 2: Which sections changed the assessment?

Not which sections were present or filled in well. Which sections actually moved what the audit concluded.

**Alternative Explanations: 9 of 10 audits.**

The only audit where it did not move the conclusion was RC-006 (WeWork), where the evidence was so conclusive that the framing was irrelevant. In every other audit, the key reframe appeared here before evidence was examined. The section moved assessments by preventing premature closure — committing to a competing frame early made subsequent evidence easier to interpret and harder to dismiss.

**Open Questions: 5 of 10 audits.**

RC-002 (what counts as "one person"), RC-003 (AGI declared vs. AGI achieved), RC-005 (fifth distinct mechanism), RC-009 (scope error vs. magnitude error), RC-010 (permanent vs. temporary indeterminacy). In these five, the sharpest articulation of what the audit actually found appeared in Open Questions rather than in the body. The section did not generate insight — it crystallized it more precisely than the surrounding prose.

**Missing Evidence: conditionally high — approximately 4 of 10 audits.**

RC-001 (Fortune 500 SaaS cancellation), RC-004 (installation rate data), RC-006 (empty — that itself was informative), RC-010 (structural gap: counterfactual can't exist). In the others, Missing Evidence underperformed because the obstacle was definitional rather than evidential, and the section conflated the two. Its value is conditional on the claim's obstacle type.

**Evidence Against: 3 of 10 audits.**

RC-004 (DISCOM arrears and installation rate gap), RC-005 (Musk's track record of prior misses), RC-006 (WeWork's own S-1 financials contradicted the claim). In most other audits, Evidence Against populated the picture without moving the conclusion. The assessment was already set by the time Evidence Against ran.

**Evidence For: 2 of 10 audits.**

RC-004 (India pipeline and PLI scheme genuinely informative) and RC-007 (ETF adoption scale). Underperformed more than expected. In most audits, Evidence For confirmed what Alternative Explanations had already established rather than adding new weight.

**Confidence (Reason field): 2 of 10 audits.**

RC-003 ("a claim designed to be satisfiable by its author is not the same as a falsifiable prediction") and RC-005 ("a pattern of revision lowers the prior probability that this deadline holds"). Both appeared in Confidence rather than earlier sections, and both were more precise than the surrounding body text. The section does not generate; it occasionally produces the clearest statement of what was generated elsewhere.

**Status: 0 of 10 audits.**

It never moved the conclusion. It recorded it. That distinction matters.

**Review Trigger: 0 of 10 audits.**

It named the event that would reopen the file. It did not change what the file said.

## What the two questions say together

Claim structures are diverse — ten different structures from ten audits, with no repetition yet. The question of whether structures recur cannot be answered at this sample size.

Section influence is concentrated — one section (Alternative Explanations) moved assessments in 9 of 10 audits. Two sections (Status, Review Trigger) moved zero. The template has a sharp gradient from high influence to low influence that was not visible in the template's design.

The template was designed as if each section contributed roughly equally to the audit. The evidence after ten audits is that this is false. The template's work is done primarily in two places: the frame (Alternative Explanations) and the residue (Open Questions). Everything between is supporting material.

That is not a template redesign recommendation. It is an observation about where the template is actually doing work. If that observation holds through RC-020, it would become pressure.

# Repository Audit: RC-001 through RC-010

**Date:** 2026-06-23
**Scope:** Ten completed audits, one prior meta-audit (RC-001 through RC-005), one open question.
**Method:** Three questions only. No framework proposals.

---

## Question 1: What observations survived all ten audits?

**Alternative Explanations is the highest-yield section.**

This held across all ten audits without exception. The key reframe appeared in Alternative Explanations in every audit — before evidence was examined. In RC-001 it shifted the claim from "agents vs. software" to "agents vs. labor." In RC-007 it separated prediction from mechanism. In RC-010 it identified that the mechanism itself may have been fictitious. The section forces commitment to competing frames early. That sequence — frames before evidence — is the structural reason it outperforms.

This observation has not been wounded. It is the single most durable finding in the repository.

**Different claims encounter different obstacles to evaluation.**

Ten audits, ten distinct obstacles. No two were the same mechanism. This observation was made after five audits and continued to hold through ten. It has strengthened, not weakened.

**Claims can contain prediction and mechanism as separable components.**

This emerged at RC-007 (Bitcoin: prediction resolved, mechanism did not) and was confirmed by RC-009 (China: the split was visible at entry, before any evidence was examined). Two different domains, two different directions of discovery. The observation survived both.

**Missing Evidence is conditional on the claim's obstacle type.**

When the claim's primary obstacle is evidentiary, Missing Evidence is strong and specific. When the obstacle is definitional, Missing Evidence gets confused and requests clarification instead. This held from RC-002 (weakest performance) through RC-010 (where the section correctly identified the gap as structural — the counterfactual cannot exist — rather than circumstantial).

**Open Questions is more valuable than the template design assumed.**

The sharpest single sentence in most audits appeared in Open Questions. This held from RC-001 through RC-010. In RC-003, the sharpest articulation of the self-fulfilling problem appeared there. In RC-010, the question about permanent indeterminacy vs. awaiting evidence appeared there. The section has not underperformed in any audit.

---

## Question 2: Which observations were wounded?

**Open Question #001 was wounded, but not destroyed.**

The original observation — "high-visibility claims are systematically resistant to external falsification" — was based on RC-001 through RC-003, which found three consecutive claims that were difficult for outside observers to verify. RC-004 wounded it directly: India's 500 GW target had clear terms, external tracking data, and was unresolved for evidentiary rather than definitional reasons. RC-006 through RC-010 continued the wound — resolved claims were clearly assessable once outcomes arrived.

What survived: the pattern may be genre-specific. Technology narrative predictions (RC-001 through RC-003) are structurally resistant. Infrastructure commitments (RC-004), market price predictions (RC-007), and labor market claims (RC-008) are not. The observation was overstated as universal. As a genre-specific finding it may still hold.

**The early framing of Status as "weak" was accurate but incomplete.**

After RC-001 through RC-005, the observation was that Status differentiates poorly — all five audits produced Unresolved. RC-006 through RC-010 showed that Status does differentiate, but fails in a different way: it assumes claims have a single outcome that can be captured by one label. RC-007 needed two simultaneous statuses (prediction: Supported; mechanism: Unresolved). RC-008 needed a partial label. RC-010 found that "Currently Unresolvable" covers two fundamentally different situations.

The wound is: Status is not merely incomplete. The abstraction itself is wrong. Claims are not single-outcome objects, and a single-label system will always fail on claims of sufficient complexity. Adding more labels does not fix this — it patches it. That is a deeper wound than the meta-audit identified.

**The assumption that "Currently Unresolvable" meant "awaiting future evidence" was directly wounded.**

RC-010 produced the first use of "Currently Unresolvable" — but for a claim where the event has passed, the investigation ran, and the evidence was collected. The claim is still not decidable because the counterfactual required for causal attribution cannot be constructed. This is not a temporary state. The word "currently" in the status name is wrong for this case.

## Question 3: Which observations generated predictions that reality actually tested?

**Prediction: Claims at the evidentiary end of the spectrum should resolve cleanly as time passes.**

This was predicted after identifying the path-to-resolution spectrum (RC-004/RC-005 discussions). RC-006 tested it: WeWork's outcome was resolved, the Missing Evidence section was empty for the first time, and Confidence reached High for the first time. Confirmed.

**Prediction: Resolved claims will stress the template differently from unresolved claims.**

This was observed after RC-006, before RC-007. The prediction was that RC-007 would produce a different kind of template pressure than RC-006. It did — RC-006 found a missing status label; RC-007 found a compound status problem. RC-008 found a partial outcome problem. RC-010 found a permanent indeterminacy problem. The prediction was confirmed: resolved claims do stress the template differently, and they do so through different mechanisms each time.

**Prediction: The prediction/mechanism split is not a Bitcoin artifact but a general property of causal claims.**

This was observed at RC-007 and implicitly predicted to recur. RC-009 confirmed it in a different domain (macroeconomics vs. cryptocurrency), through a different direction of discovery (split visible at entry vs. discovered at resolution). Confirmed.

**Prediction: A high-visibility claim with clear terms and external tracking would wound Open Question #001.**

RC-004 was explicitly chosen to test this. It partially confirmed the prediction: India's 500 GW target was more assessable than RC-001 through RC-003. It partially wounded Open Question #001. But one definitional ambiguity survived (large hydro accounting), which preserved some resistance to clean evaluation. The prediction was partially confirmed, partially not.

---

## What was not predicted that happened anyway

**The observation "unresolved claims teach about evaluability, resolved claims teach about resolution" was not predicted. It was discovered.**

This distinction was noticed after RC-007 and RC-008. No audit predicted it in advance. The repository arrived at it through accumulation, not anticipation. Whether it holds through a second cycle of audits is not yet known.

**The finding that RC-006 through RC-010 each found a different template gap was not predicted.**

After RC-006, the expectation was that RC-007 would stress the template in some way — but not that every subsequent resolved audit would find a completely different gap. The diversity of template failures (missing status, compound status, partial status, permanent indeterminacy) was not anticipated. It emerged from the claims themselves.

---

## The single most defensible statement after ten audits

Claims fail evaluation for different reasons. Claims resolve for different reasons. Resolution itself is more complex than the original template assumed.

That is the honest summary. Everything beyond it is still under pressure.

---

## What this audit does not recommend

No template changes.
No new sections.
No taxonomy.
No framework for the next phase.

The repository has produced enough pressure to know that the Status abstraction needs rethinking eventually. It has not produced enough resolved audits to know what the right abstraction is. A redesign now would be anticipatory rather than reactive — exactly the posture that produced the original three-option Status section that reality has been attacking for five audits.

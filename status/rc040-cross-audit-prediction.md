## Prediction

**Domain:** Labor Economics / Employment Policy

**Claim:** "Minimum wage increases do not cause significant employment losses — and may in some cases increase employment — demonstrating that the standard competitive labor market model fails to describe low-wage labor markets." — Card and Krueger (1994); Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010); Arindrajit Dube

**Predicted Primary Object:** Attribution Precision Gap

**Predicted Secondary Object:** Watch B — present, secondary

**Predicted Non-Appearance:** Counterfactual Unavailability

**Reason:**

Intermediate-maturity classification predicts APG primary with Watch B present as secondary, following the established gradient pattern.

The natural experiment literature (Card-Krueger state-border design; Dube et al. contiguous-county design; Cengiz et al. bunching estimator) has partially resolved Watch B: catastrophic employment destruction from moderate minimum wage increases is not the live dispute. A substantial research community accepts that moderate minimum wage increases in monopsonistic or oligopsonistic low-wage labor markets do not produce large disemployment effects.

However, Watch B is not fully resolved. Two research programs with clear institutional identities (Dube/Card et al. vs. Neumark/Wascher) reach incompatible conclusions on whether any significant negative employment effect exists. That is not purely an APG dispute about magnitude — it includes a directional dispute about the sign of the effect. Direction-of-effect disagreement sits at the Watch B/APG boundary: it is more resolved than "did the intervention have any effect?" (Watch B core) but less resolved than "how large was the documented effect?" (APG core).

The Intermediate classification correctly captures this: APG should be primary (the live debate is about the size and direction of effects, not about whether minimum wages affect labor markets at all), but Watch B should be secondary (directional disagreement between research programs means causal presence is not fully settled at the granular level).

Counterfactual Unavailability is predicted absent because the natural experiment literature explicitly constructs counterfactual groups.

## What Makes This Test Distinctive

The Card-Krueger/Neumark controversy is unusually explicit: two identifiable research programs with different methodologies, different datasets, and different conclusions. This makes the Watch B/APG boundary unusually visible — the audit should be able to separate "did minimum wage increases cause employment effects?" from "how large are those effects?" without ambiguity. A clean classification here is a genuine test of whether the repository's two-object framework maps onto a well-documented empirical controversy.

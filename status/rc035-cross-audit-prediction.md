# RC-035 Cross-Audit Prediction

**Date recorded:** 2026-06-24
**Status:** Pre-registered — claim not yet selected

This prediction was recorded before the specific RC-035 claim was identified. It constitutes the repository's first ontology-level prospective test: predicting which epistemic structure objects will dominate an audit before the claim or evidence is reviewed.

---

## Prediction

**Domain:** Industrial Policy

**Predicted Primary Object:** Watch B

**Predicted Secondary Object:** Attribution Precision Gap

**Predicted Non-Appearance:** Counterfactual Unavailability

**Reason:**

Industrial policy claims frequently present as Attribution Precision Gap problems at the surface: multiple concurrent factors (directed credit, technology transfer, capital allocation, private investment, export discipline) all appear to have contributed, and the question looks like contribution-share decomposition.

But industrial policy debates often contain an underlying causal-presence dispute: *Would the development have occurred anyway, through market mechanisms or external factors?* If the growth trajectory was already underway — driven by export orientation, labor cost advantages, or US-era trade access — then the specific industrial policy interventions may not have added independent causal contribution. That is a Watch B question, not an Attribution Precision Gap question.

Attribution Precision Gap activates only after causal presence is broadly accepted. In industrial policy debates, causal presence is frequently contested.

Counterfactual unavailability is predicted to be absent because the counterfactual development path (without industrial policy) is structurally imaginable and empirically contested — not structurally blocked. The dispute is about what would have happened, not whether a comparison world is conceivable. That is the terrain of Watch B, not of counterfactual unavailability.

---

## What Makes This Prediction Diagnostic

If **Watch B appears** as primary: confirms that industrial policy debates reduce to causal-role disputes underneath apparent contribution-share framing. The Watch B / Attribution Precision Gap boundary holds where predicted.

If **Attribution Precision Gap appears** as primary: suggests the repository overestimated the frequency with which industrial policy debates collapse into causal-role uncertainty. The causal role of policy was more broadly accepted than predicted. This would be informative about the boundary between the two objects.

If **Counterfactual Unavailability appears**: the prediction was wrong about the structural accessibility of the counterfactual development path. This would require examination of whether industrial policy cases are more structurally unique than assumed.

---

## What This Test Is Not

This is not a test of whether the audit will produce a correct or interesting result. It is a test of whether the repository's current object ontology — Watch B, Attribution Precision Gap, and their specified boundary — is tracking a real distinction in the evidence environment rather than merely organizing past audits.

A correct prediction here would be evidence that the objects have predictive power beyond the cases that generated them.

A wrong prediction would be informative about ontology maturity rather than evidence of failure.

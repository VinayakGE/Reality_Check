# Meta-Audit: RC-001 through RC-005

**Date:** 2026-06-23
**Question:** Which sections of the Reality Check template generated the most insight, changed assessments most often, were weakest, and consistently exposed hidden assumptions?

This is not a framework redesign. It is an honest reading of five completed audits against the template that produced them.

---

## Section-by-Section Assessment

### Alternative Explanations

**Verdict: Highest value. The section that did the most work.**

In 4 of 5 audits, the primary insight — the one that restructured everything that followed — appeared here, not in the evidence sections.

- RC-001: H1 ("agents replace the human operator, not the SaaS") was the key reframe. The entire claim shifted from "agents vs. software" to "agents vs. labor." That happened in the first hypothesis, before a single piece of evidence was examined.
- RC-002: H2 ("one-person revenue operation, not a one-person company") introduced the distinction that made the claim's entity definitions collapse. The evidence sections confirmed it; Alternative Explanations found it.
- RC-003: H2 ("the claim is true as a marketing event, not a technical one") was the sharpest insight in the audit — that AGI declaration might be driven by OpenAI's governance structure rather than capability. This is not an obvious reading and required committing to an alternative frame early.
- RC-004: H3 ("target is redefined before 2030") was the surviving ambiguity after the claim's terms appeared to be defined. The section found the remaining definitional vulnerability even in the most structured claim of the set.
- RC-005: H3 ("regulatory and technical constraints are the actual ceiling") introduced the production/deployment distinction that made the claim technically satisfiable but strategically meaningless as stated.

The section forces a commitment to alternative frames before evidence is examined. That sequence matters. Evidence is easier to interpret when you already have competing hypotheses to test it against.

---

### Missing Evidence

**Verdict: High value — but conditional. Performs well when the claim's obstacle is evidentiary. Underperforms when the obstacle is definitional.**

- RC-001: Strong. "No Fortune 500 SaaS cancellation at enterprise scale" is concrete, observable, and names the specific evidence gap. You could go find this — or confirm it doesn't exist.
- RC-002: Weaker than it appeared. The section asked for definitional clarification disguised as missing evidence: "what counts as one person?" is not missing evidence, it is a missing definition. The section conflated the two.
- RC-003: Moderate. "A reliable third-party benchmark for general capability" is a real gap but abstract — you cannot go retrieve it, only observe its absence.
- RC-004: Strong. Specific sources named (MNRE reports, Green Energy Corridor Phase II status), specific data gaps identified, findable when they appear.
- RC-005: Good. Supplier contracts and NHTSA exemption status are real, specific gaps — though NHTSA's processing status is not publicly tracked in real time, which limits actionability.

The section's value is proportional to how cleanly the claim's obstacle is evidentiary rather than definitional. When definitions are missing, the section gets confused and starts requesting definitional clarification instead.

---

### Evidence For / Evidence Against

**Verdict: Medium value. The sections that filled in the picture, not the sections that generated insight.**

These sections were rarely where the key finding appeared. In RC-001 through RC-003, the Alternative Explanations section had already established the interpretive frame; Evidence sections then populated it. In RC-004, the evidence sections were more useful — the installation rate gap and DISCOM arrear problem were substantively informative. In RC-005, the most important item in Evidence Against (the list of prior missed Musk deadlines) is technically evidence against the current claim but is actually an argument about the claimant's track record, which is a different category of assessment.

These sections are necessary. The assessment would be weaker without them. But they did not change what the audits found.

---

### Confidence

**Verdict: Lower than expected — with one notable exception per audit.**

The Confidence section was largely summary: it restated what the audit had already found, then labeled it Low. All five audits produced Low confidence (RC-004 reached Medium, the only exception).

But the Reason field occasionally crystallized a finding that the audit had not stated as precisely anywhere else:

- RC-003: *"A claim designed to be satisfiable by its author is not the same as a falsifiable prediction."* This sentence appeared in the Confidence section, not in Alternative Explanations. It was the sharpest articulation of the self-fulfilling problem in the entire audit.
- RC-005: *"A pattern of revision does not make this particular claim false; it lowers the prior probability that this deadline holds."* This appeared in Confidence. It correctly separated track record as prior probability rather than proof.

The section does not generate insight but occasionally produces the clearest statement of insight that was generated elsewhere. The Reason field is doing more work than the status label.

---

### Status

**Verdict: Weakest section. A checkbox, not a tool.**

All five audits: Unresolved. The section generated no differentiation across the claim set.

This is partly a selection effect — the claims chosen were all live and unresolved by design. But the section also offers no gradation: Supported, Unresolved, and Currently Unresolvable are too coarse to capture what the audits actually found. RC-001 is unresolved for definitional reasons. RC-004 is unresolved for evidentiary reasons. These are different situations; the same checkbox covers both.

The section's value would increase if claims were revisited after review triggers fire. As a first-pass status, it is barely informative.

---

### Open Questions

**Verdict: Underrated. Often the section with the sharpest single sentence.**

This section was not assessed in the original template design. It should have been.

- RC-001: *"What does 'replace' mean in a world where agents use SaaS APIs as their action layer?"* — The most precise statement of the relationship ambiguity in the audit.
- RC-002: *"What is the actual definition of 'one person'? The claim is unfalsifiable until this is specified."* — Correctly named the epistemic problem before the evidence sections resolved it.
- RC-003: *"Is there a meaningful difference between 'AGI declared by OpenAI' and 'AGI achieved'?"* — The sharpest question in the entire set. This is the thing that would wound or confirm the claim, and it was in Open Questions.
- RC-004: *"Is the binding constraint generation capacity, transmission capacity, or DISCOM financial health?"* — Correctly identified that the policy response differs by answer, which makes the constraint-type a material question, not a rhetorical one.
- RC-005: *"Is this the fifth distinct evaluation mechanism?"* — Meta, but useful for the repository itself.

Open Questions consistently contained the most precise articulation of what remained genuinely uncertain after the audit. The section performs best when it asks questions that are not rhetorical — that would actually change the assessment if answered.

---

## Summary Assessment

| Section | Value | Primary Role |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| Alternative Explanations | Highest | Generates the reframe that structures everything that follows |
| Open Questions | High (underrated) | Produces the sharpest statement of genuine uncertainty |
| Missing Evidence | High (conditional) | Strong on evidentiary gaps; weak on definitional gaps |
| Evidence Against | Medium | Fills in the picture; rarely generates new insight |
| Evidence For | Medium | Same |
| Confidence (Reason field) | Low-Medium | Occasionally crystallizes what was found elsewhere |
| Status | Lowest | A checkbox; no differentiation across five audits |

---

## What This Does Not Recommend

This is not a template redesign recommendation. The template produced five useful audits. The observation that Alternative Explanations did the most work is a reason to take that section seriously when filling it out, not a reason to add more sections or restructure the form.

The one actionable observation: the Status section needs a review date and a gradation that distinguishes "Unresolved due to missing definitions" from "Unresolved due to missing evidence." Those are audits in different states, and the same checkbox does not capture the difference.

---

## What This Confirms About Open Question #001

The meta-audit offers indirect evidence on Open Question #001 (whether high-visibility claims are systematically resistant to external falsification).

The section that did the most work — Alternative Explanations — is also the section where claim resistance to falsification showed up first. The hypotheses generated in that section consistently found the load-bearing assumption that made the claim difficult to test. This suggests the template is structured to find resistance to falsification, not merely to document it.

Whether that is a property of the claims or a property of the template structure is still open.

# RC-037 Cross-Audit Prediction

**Date recorded:** 2026-06-24
**Status:** Pre-registered — audit not yet conducted

This prediction was recorded before reviewing specific evidence on the lead-crime hypothesis. It constitutes the third cross-audit ontology prediction in the repository's history and the first to test a gradient prediction rather than a binary one.

---

## Prediction

**Domain:** Environmental Epidemiology / Crime

**Claim:** "The phaseout of leaded gasoline in the 1970s was the primary cause of the violent crime decline that began in the 1990s." — Rick Nevin / Jessica Reyes

**Predicted Primary Object:** Attribution Precision Gap

**Predicted Secondary Object:** Watch B — present, but weaker than in RC-035

**Predicted Non-Appearance:** Counterfactual Unavailability

**Reason:**

The lead-crime hypothesis has a substantial natural-experiment literature (cross-state phaseout timing variation, cohort effects, cross-national correlations) that has moved a significant portion of the research community toward accepting causal contribution. This partially resolves the Watch B question — causal role is not broadly accepted the way it is in climate attribution, but it is not as contested as the causal role of industrial policy in East Asian development. The domain sits at intermediate evidence maturity.

Because causal role is partly resolved, the live scholarly dispute centers on contribution magnitude: how much of the crime decline (estimated range: 20%–56%+ depending on method) is attributable to lead versus the Donohue-Levitt abortion hypothesis, policing changes, crack epidemic dynamics, and incarceration rates. That is an APG dispute.

Watch B is predicted to remain present as a secondary object because the alternative explanations (abortion-crime, policing) are genuine causal competitors, not merely magnitude disputes. Some researchers dispute whether lead had any primary causal role, not merely how large it was. This is structurally different from RC-036, where attribution science had largely moved past causal-role disputes.

Counterfactual unavailability is predicted absent because the natural experiments (phaseout timing, cross-state variation) explicitly construct comparison groups.

## The Gradient Prediction

This pre-registration makes a more specific claim than simply "APG primary":

| Frontier | RC-035 (Industrial Policy) | RC-037 (Lead-Crime) | RC-036 (Climate Attribution) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Domain maturity | Low | Intermediate | High |
| Predicted primary | Watch B | APG | APG |
| Predicted Watch B strength | Dominant | Present but secondary | Minimal |

If the observed outcome matches — APG primary, Watch B present but less dominant than in RC-035 and more present than in RC-036 — that is evidence for a gradient relationship between domain evidence maturity and epistemic object dominance, not merely a binary switch.

If Watch B is absent (as in RC-036), the domain-maturity hypothesis would predict a sharp transition rather than a gradient. If Watch B is dominant (as in RC-035), the hypothesis would require the lead-crime literature to be at low maturity, which would need to be reconciled with the natural experiment evidence.

## What Makes This Prediction Diagnostic

**If APG primary + Watch B secondary (gradient confirmed):** Domain-maturity hypothesis survives across three data points spanning low, intermediate, and high maturity. The hypothesis becomes trackable as a pattern, though still not promotable.

**If APG primary + Watch B absent (sharp transition):** Lead-crime literature is effectively as mature as climate attribution for the domain-maturity hypothesis. The "intermediate" categorization was wrong, or the transition from Watch B to APG is sharper than the gradient model predicts.

**If Watch B primary (low maturity):** Lead-crime sits closer to industrial policy than pre-registration assessed. The natural experiment evidence has not moved the field as far toward causal acceptance as expected. The domain-maturity hypothesis still holds, but the intermediate-maturity categorization was wrong.

**If new object appears:** The domain may have features that generate a third epistemic object (epidemiological attribution has its own methodological tradition distinct from both climate attribution and development economics).
